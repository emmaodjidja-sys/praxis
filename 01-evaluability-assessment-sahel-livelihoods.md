# Evaluation Matrix: Girls' Education and Empowerment Programme in Conflict-Affected Settings

## Scenario

**Test prompt**: "I need an evaluation matrix for a girls' education programme operating in northeast Nigeria (Borno, Adamawa, Yobe states), South Sudan, and Afghanistan. The programme runs accelerated learning centres for out-of-school girls aged 10-19, provides cash transfers conditional on attendance, trains female teachers, and runs community dialogue sessions to shift norms around girls' education. It's a 4-year FCDO-funded programme, now entering Year 4. I need the matrix for the endline evaluation."

---

## Evaluation Matrix

### Notes on Design Choices

This matrix is structured around five OECD-DAC criteria (relevance, effectiveness, impact, sustainability) plus conflict sensitivity as a supplementary criterion. Coherence and efficiency are folded into other questions rather than given standalone sections, to keep the question count manageable (10 main questions) given the multi-country scope and access constraints.

The matrix assumes a mixed-methods contribution analysis as the primary design. In three active conflict zones, counterfactual-based impact evaluation is not feasible. The evaluation will triangulate quantitative evidence (attendance data, learning assessments, cash transfer records) with qualitative evidence (case studies, FGDs, KIIs) to build a credible contribution story.

Each question specifies data collection across all three countries, but the matrix acknowledges that coverage will vary by access. Northeast Nigeria and South Sudan will have more restricted access than urban/peri-urban areas of Afghanistan (depending on current Taliban policy on girls' education at the time of data collection).

## Key Methodological Notes

**No counterfactual**: This evaluation cannot establish what would have happened to these girls without the programme. The contribution analysis approach tests the plausibility of the programme's contribution story using multiple evidence streams. The inclusion of non-enrolled eligible girls in the survey provides a comparison group, but selection bias (why some girls enrolled and others didn't) means this comparison is illustrative, not causal.

**Country-level analysis first, then synthesis**: Given the vastly different contexts (active insurgency in northeast Nigeria, civil war in South Sudan, Taliban governance in Afghanistan), analyse each country as a separate case first. Cross-country synthesis should focus on common mechanisms and patterns, not pooled averages.

**Access-adjusted expectations**: Data collection coverage will vary by security conditions. The evaluation should be transparent about which areas were reached and which were not, and should avoid generalising from accessible areas to the full programme footprint.
