# Realist Evaluation Design: Multi-Donor Rule of Law and Access to Justice Programme

## Scenario

**Test prompt**: "We're evaluating a multi-donor rule of law programme in the Democratic Republic of Congo, funded by the EU, Netherlands, and Sweden. It operates in South Kivu, North Kivu, and Ituri. The programme supports mobile courts, legal aid clinics, community-based paralegals, police training on sexual and gender-based violence, and transitional justice dialogues. It's been running 6 years across two phases. Phase 1 was evaluated using a standard OECD-DAC framework and found 'mixed results' but couldn't explain why the programme worked in some areas and not others. The donors want the Phase 2 evaluation to explain the variation. They've specifically asked us to consider a realist approach."

---

## Realist Evaluation Design

### 1. Why Realist Evaluation Is the Right Fit

The Phase 1 evaluation identified outcome variation across sites without explaining it. This is precisely the problem realist evaluation is designed to address. Rather than asking "did the programme work?" (a question Phase 1 answered with "sometimes"), this evaluation asks "what worked, for whom, in what circumstances, and why?"

Rule of law programming in eastern DRC operates in a context where the state has limited territorial control, armed groups administer parallel justice systems, customary law coexists (and sometimes conflicts) with statutory law, sexual violence is endemic and often tied to armed conflict, and community trust in formal justice institutions is extremely low. These contextual factors do not simply "surround" the programme; they actively shape whether and how programme mechanisms fire. A realist approach treats this context-mechanism interaction as the central analytical object.

### 2. Initial Programme Theory

The initial programme theory (IPT) is constructed from Phase 1 evaluation findings, programme documents, and the academic literature on rule of law in conflict-affected settings. It will be refined through stakeholder consultations during the inception phase.

The programme operates through five components, each with its own sub-theory:

**Component 1: Mobile Courts**

IPT: When mobile courts are deployed to areas where formal courts are physically absent or inaccessible (Context), and when the courts hear cases that are locally relevant and visible (resource offered), community members begin to perceive formal justice as accessible and relevant (reasoning triggered), which leads to increased demand for formal justice and reduced reliance on informal or armed-group justice systems (Outcome).

Initial CMO configurations to test:

CMO-1a: In areas where armed groups are absent or have recently been pushed back (C), mobile courts can operate safely and visibly (resource), which builds community confidence that using formal justice will not result in retaliation (reasoning), leading to increased case filings and reduced forum shopping to customary or armed-group systems (O).

CMO-1b: In areas where armed groups maintain influence (C), mobile courts operate under threat and community members fear being seen engaging with state justice (reasoning), leading to low uptake despite physical presence of the court (O).

CMO-1c: Where mobile courts hear SGBV cases publicly (C), survivors may face stigma and retaliation (reasoning), leading to withdrawal from proceedings and reinforcement of silence norms (O). Conversely, where confidential hearing procedures are in place (C), survivors may feel protected enough to participate (reasoning), leading to completed proceedings and some deterrence effect (O).

**Component 2: Legal Aid Clinics and Paralegals**

IPT: When community-based paralegals are recruited from the communities they serve (Context), and trained to provide legal information and mediation in local languages and cultural idioms (resource), community members perceive legal assistance as trustworthy and culturally appropriate (reasoning), leading to increased access to justice for marginalised groups, particularly women and land dispute claimants (Outcome).

CMO-2a: In communities where the paralegal is a respected local figure (C), their legal advice carries social weight (reasoning), and disputes are resolved before escalation (O).

CMO-2b: In communities with deep ethnic divisions (C), a paralegal from one group may be mistrusted by another (reasoning), leading to selective use and potential perceptions of bias (O).

CMO-2c: Where paralegals are connected to functional legal aid clinics with qualified lawyers (C), they can refer complex cases upward (resource), which builds a referral pathway that makes the system feel coherent (reasoning), increasing sustained engagement with formal justice (O).

**Component 3: Police Training on SGBV**

IPT: When police officers are trained in survivor-centred approaches and evidence collection for SGBV cases (resource), and when institutional accountability mechanisms (supervision, sanctions for misconduct) are in place (Context), officers change their investigative behaviour (reasoning), leading to improved case handling, increased survivor willingness to report, and higher prosecution rates (Outcome).

CMO-3a: Where training is combined with functional accountability mechanisms and supportive commanding officers (C), officers apply new skills because they face consequences for reverting to old practices (reasoning), leading to sustained behaviour change (O).

CMO-3b: Where training occurs in isolation, without supervision reform or accountability mechanisms (C), officers may understand the new approaches but face no incentive to apply them (reasoning), and revert to prior practices within months (O).

**Component 4: Transitional Justice Dialogues**

IPT: When community dialogue processes are facilitated by credible, neutral facilitators (Context), and address locally relevant grievances (resource), participants develop shared narratives about past violence (reasoning), which reduces inter-group hostility and creates space for coexistence (Outcome).

CMO-4a: In communities where the conflict has a clear trajectory and the main perpetrators are no longer present (C), dialogue can address past harms without fear (reasoning), leading to acknowledgement and some reconciliation (O).

CMO-4b: In communities where the conflict is ongoing or perpetrators still hold power (C), dialogue is experienced as dangerous or performative (reasoning), leading to superficial participation or avoidance (O).

**Component 5: Cross-component synergies**

IPT: When multiple components operate in the same geographic area simultaneously (Context), they reinforce each other: paralegals identify cases, refer to legal aid, which connects to mobile courts; police training improves SGBV case handling, which mobile courts adjudicate; dialogue processes create the social trust that enables justice-seeking behaviour. The whole is greater than the sum of parts (Outcome).

CMO-5a: In areas with all components active and coordinated (C), a functional justice chain emerges (resource), community members experience coherent support from identification through resolution (reasoning), leading to system-level improvements in access to justice (O).

### 3. Data Collection Design

Data collection is method-neutral in realist evaluation; what matters is that evidence can test the CMO configurations. This evaluation will use:

**Phase 1: Programme theory refinement (Months 1-3)**

- Realist interviews with 15-20 programme designers, managers, and senior implementers to refine the IPT and CMO configurations
- Realist interview technique: present the initial CMO configurations and ask informants to confirm, challenge, or refine them based on their experience
- Workshop with donors and programme leadership to validate refined programme theory

**Phase 2: Empirical testing of CMOCs (Months 4-10)**

Organised by site, not by component. Select 8-10 sites representing variation on key contextual dimensions:

| Site selection dimension | Variation |
|---|---|
| Security level | Active conflict / post-conflict / relatively stable |
| Component combination | All 5 components / 2-3 components / single component |
| Ethnic composition | Homogeneous / multi-ethnic |
| Programme maturity | Operating since Phase 1 / started in Phase 2 |
| Perceived programme performance | High / moderate / low (based on programme monitoring data) |

At each site:

- Realist interviews with local programme staff (2-3 per site)
- Realist interviews with justice sector actors: judges, prosecutors, police, paralegals, legal aid lawyers (3-5 per site)
- Realist interviews with community leaders and customary justice actors (2-3 per site)
- FGDs with community members who have used programme-supported services (1-2 per site, stratified by type of service used)
- FGDs with community members who have not engaged with programme-supported services (1 per site, to understand why not)
- SGBV-specific interviews with survivors who engaged with the justice system (conducted by trained female researchers with safeguarding protocols) (3-5 per site where safe and ethical)
- Programme data review: caseload data, case completion rates, referral records
- Direct observation of mobile court sessions, paralegal mediation sessions, or dialogue processes where active and accessible

**Secondary quantitative data** (across all programme areas, not site-specific):

- Programme MIS: case numbers, types, disposition, referral pathways, completion rates, by location and time
- Police administrative data on SGBV case handling: reporting rates, investigation completion, prosecution referrals
- Perception surveys (if conducted by the programme as part of monitoring)
- ACLED data on armed group presence and conflict events, geocoded to programme areas

### 4. Analysis

**Within-site analysis**: For each site, map the evidence against the CMO configurations. Determine which CMOCs are supported, which are refuted, and which require modification. Document the context conditions that appear to enable or constrain programme mechanisms.

**Cross-site comparison**: Compare CMOC patterns across sites. Identify which contextual conditions are most consequential for programme outcomes. Look for:
- Configurations that are consistently confirmed across diverse sites (robust CMOCs)
- Configurations that are consistently refuted (mechanisms that do not fire as theorised)
- Configurations where context makes the difference (the same mechanism fires in one context but not another)

**Refined programme theory**: Produce a refined set of CMO configurations that explain the observed variation in outcomes. These should be specific enough to guide programme adaptation and general enough to inform similar programming elsewhere in eastern DRC or comparable contexts.

**Middle-range theory development**: Abstract from the DRC-specific findings to develop middle-range theories about how rule of law programming works in conflict-affected settings. These MRTs can be tested in future evaluations and contribute to the evidence base for the sector.

### 5. Products

The evaluation will produce:

1. **Inception report** with the refined programme theory and CMO configurations, site selection rationale, and detailed data collection plan
2. **Country report** presenting the full realist analysis: confirmed CMOCs, refuted CMOCs, refined programme theory, and site-level case narratives
3. **Policy brief** translating CMO findings into practical guidance: "Under these conditions, do this. Under those conditions, do that instead."
4. **Presentation** for donor steering committee with key CMOCs visualised and programme adaptation recommendations
5. **Theory brief** presenting the middle-range theories for the rule of law in conflict-affected settings literature

### 6. Ethical Considerations

- SGBV survivor interviews require the highest ethical standards: trained female interviewers, private settings, referral protocols for distress and safety concerns, informed consent that explicitly addresses the risk of re-traumatisation
- Data on armed group influence in specific communities must be handled with extreme sensitivity; identifying which communities are under armed group control could endanger both communities and programme staff
- Realist interviews with justice actors (judges, police) may reveal corruption, complicity with armed groups, or other sensitive information; data must be anonymised and stored securely
- Community members discussing their engagement with formal justice may face retribution from customary authorities, armed groups, or perpetrators; informed consent must address these risks
- Conflict sensitivity: the evaluation itself must not exacerbate tensions between ethnic groups, between formal and customary justice actors, or between communities and armed groups

### 7. Quality Assurance

Aligned with RAMESES II quality standards for realist evaluation:
- Programme theory is explicit and based on both programme logic and existing evidence
- CMO configurations are clearly articulated with each element (C, M, O) specified
- Data collection is designed to test specific CMOCs, not to generate broad thematic findings
- Analysis transparently shows how evidence was mapped against CMOCs
- Refined programme theory is presented alongside the evidence that supports it
- The distinction between mechanisms (reasoning and responses of actors) and programme activities is maintained throughout
