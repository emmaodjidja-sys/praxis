# Evaluability Assessment: Multi-Country Sahel Youth Livelihoods Programme

## Endline Evaluation Readiness Assessment

**Programme**: Youth Economic Empowerment and Resilience Programme
**Countries**: Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger
**Target population**: Youth aged 15-30 in communities vulnerable to armed group recruitment
**Duration**: 3 years (approaching endline)
**Assessment date**: [Current]
**Assessment type**: Pre-evaluation evaluability assessment

---

## 1. Purpose of This Assessment

This evaluability assessment determines whether the programme is ready for an endline evaluation, given that (a) baseline data exists, (b) midline data collection was disrupted by the security situation in northern Burkina Faso, and (c) the programme operates across three countries with varying levels of access and stability.

The assessment examines evaluability across three dimensions: programme design and theory, data availability and M&E systems, and institutional and stakeholder context. It concludes with recommendations on evaluation scope, design, and timing.

---

## 2. Dimension A: Programme Design and Theory

### A1. Theory of Change

**Assessment needed**: Does the programme have a documented ToC linking economic empowerment activities (vocational training, microenterprise support, savings groups, etc.) to reduced vulnerability to armed group recruitment?

**Key questions to verify**:
- Is the causal pathway from livelihoods to reduced recruitment vulnerability explicitly articulated?
- Are the assumptions about why economic empowerment reduces recruitment risk specified and testable? (Economic opportunity as alternative to armed group incentives? Social cohesion through group-based activities? Psychosocial resilience through self-efficacy?)
- Has the ToC been updated since programme inception to reflect the evolving security context?

**Likely gap**: In P/CVE programming, the link between economic interventions and radicalisation outcomes is contested in the literature. The evaluation will need to test this causal logic, not assume it. If the ToC does not articulate the specific mechanisms through which livelihoods reduce vulnerability, this is a significant gap that constrains the evaluation's ability to assess impact.

**Rating**: Requires verification. Request the ToC document and assess against the criteria in the frameworks reference.

### A2. Objectives and Indicators

**Assessment needed**: Are outcome-level indicators defined that capture both livelihoods outcomes (income, employment, asset accumulation) and P/CVE-relevant outcomes (attitudes toward armed groups, social cohesion, perceived governance legitimacy, sense of belonging)?

**Key questions**:
- Are P/CVE outcome indicators validated for the Sahel context?
- Are indicators disaggregated by sex, age band (15-19, 20-24, 25-30), country, and urban/rural?
- Are targets set, and were they realistic given the security deterioration?

**Likely gap**: Many livelihoods programmes in P/CVE settings have strong livelihood indicators but weak or proxy-based measures of radicalisation vulnerability. If the programme relies on attitudinal scales that have not been validated in the Central Sahel, construct validity is at risk.

### A3. Attribution and Contribution

**Assessment needed**: Can the programme's contribution to observed changes be distinguished from other factors, particularly the significant security changes across the Sahel since the programme started?

**Key considerations**:
- The security environment has deteriorated markedly in all three countries, with coups in Burkina Faso and Mali, withdrawal of French forces, and expansion of armed group territorial control. These contextual shifts may overwhelm programme effects.
- Multiple other actors (UN agencies, bilateral donors, INGOs) are implementing similar or complementary programmes in overlapping geographies.
- A counterfactual design (what would have happened without the programme?) is likely infeasible given the context. A contribution analysis or realist evaluation approach is more appropriate.

**Rating**: Partially adequate. Attribution will not be feasible; contribution assessment is achievable with appropriate design.

### A4. Programme Stability

**Assessment needed**: Has the programme been implemented as designed, or have significant adaptations been made in response to the security situation?

If the programme shifted geographies, changed target groups, or modified activities substantially, the endline evaluation must account for this. If adaptations were major, evaluating against original objectives may not be meaningful, and a developmental or utilization-focused evaluation frame may be more appropriate.

## 3. Dimension B: Data Availability and M&E Systems

### B1. Baseline Data

**Status**: Available.

**Assessment needed**:
- What was the baseline sample (size, coverage, representativeness)?
- Were the same instruments and indicators used that will be used at endline?
- Is the baseline data cleaned, documented, and accessible?
- Were comparison communities included in the baseline?

### B2. Midline Data

**Status**: Disrupted in northern Burkina Faso.

**Critical question**: What is the extent of disruption?
- Was midline completed in Mali and Niger? What is the coverage?
- For Burkina Faso, was any midline data collected in the south or in accessible areas?
- What proportion of the baseline sample is affected by the midline gap?

**Implications**: If midline was completed in two of three countries, a two-wave panel (baseline-endline) remains feasible for those countries. For Burkina Faso, the evaluation will have weaker trend data and will need to rely more heavily on qualitative methods and contribution analysis.

### B3. Monitoring Data

**Assessment needed**:
- Has routine monitoring continued across all three countries?
- What is the quality of activity-level data (participation records, outputs delivered)?
- Has monitoring adapted to the security context (remote monitoring, third-party monitoring)?
- Are there gaps in monitoring data that correspond to periods of insecurity?

### B4. Comparison Data

**Assessment needed**:
- Were comparison communities identified at baseline?
- Are comparison communities still accessible and comparable, or has displacement and conflict changed the population composition?
- Are there secondary data sources (ACLED, IPC, DHS, LSMS, HFIAS) that could provide contextual comparison data?

**Likely gap**: In the Sahel context, maintaining a valid comparison group over three years is extremely challenging. Displacement, armed group activity, and the presence of other programmes will have contaminated any initial comparability.

### B5. Data Accessibility and Quality

- Is data stored securely and accessible to evaluators?
- Are there data protection protocols in place, particularly for P/CVE-sensitive data?
- Has there been a data quality assessment?

## 4. Dimension C: Institutional and Stakeholder Context

### C1. Physical Access

**Critical constraint**. Access varies dramatically across the three countries:
- **Burkina Faso**: Northern regions likely inaccessible. Central and eastern regions with restricted access. Southwest and some urban areas accessible.
- **Mali**: Northern and central regions highly insecure. Southern regions and Bamako corridor accessible with security protocols.
- **Niger**: Western border areas insecure. Other programme areas may be more accessible depending on current security dynamics.

**Implication**: A conventional household survey across all programme sites is almost certainly not feasible. The evaluation must plan for partial coverage and use remote methods for inaccessible areas.

### C2. Resources and Timeline

**Assessment needed**:
- Is there a dedicated evaluation budget? Is it sufficient for a multi-country evaluation in a high-cost, high-security environment?
- What is the timeline? Is there pressure for findings before a funding decision?
- Are there resources for security management, armored vehicles, or remote data collection?

### C3. Stakeholder Demand and Use

**Assessment needed**:
- Who are the primary intended users? (Donor, programme management, government counterparts, community stakeholders?)
- What decisions will the evaluation inform? (Phase 2 funding? Programme redesign? Scale-up?)
- Are stakeholders prepared for findings that may show limited impact in a deteriorating context?

### C4. Ethical and Safety Considerations

**Critical in this context**:
- Asking about attitudes toward armed groups or recruitment in areas where those groups are present creates risk for both respondents and enumerators
- Data security for P/CVE-related data is paramount; a breach could endanger participants
- Informed consent must account for the power dynamics and security fears of youth in conflict-affected communities
- Referral protocols needed for respondents who disclose involvement with armed groups or exposure to violence
- The evaluation itself must be conflict-sensitive: being seen as "studying radicalisation" could stigmatise communities or attract armed group attention

## 5. Overall Evaluability Assessment

| Dimension | Rating | Key Issues |
|---|---|---|
| A. Programme design and theory | Partially adequate | ToC plausibility needs verification; P/CVE outcome measures may lack validation; contribution (not attribution) is the feasible standard |
| B. Data and M&E systems | Partially adequate | Baseline exists; midline gap in Burkina Faso; comparison group validity uncertain; monitoring data quality needs assessment |
| C. Institutional and stakeholder context | Constrained | Access limitations are the primary obstacle; ethical and safety risks are high; demand for findings appears present |

**Overall**: The programme is partially evaluable. An endline evaluation can proceed, but not as originally designed. The scope, methodology, and expectations need to be adjusted to reflect what is feasible given the security context and data gaps.

## 6. Recommendations

### 6.1. Evaluation Scope

Proceed with an endline evaluation, but adjust the scope:

- Frame the evaluation as a **summative outcome and contribution evaluation**, not an impact evaluation. Do not claim attribution.
- Prioritise **effectiveness** and **relevance** as the lead OECD-DAC criteria. Assess sustainability and coherence as secondary criteria. Efficiency only where cost data is reliable.
- Add **conflict sensitivity** as an additional criterion given the operating context.
- Limit the number of evaluation questions to 6-8 to ensure analytical depth given data constraints.

### 6.2. Evaluation Design

Recommend a **mixed-methods contribution analysis** drawing on realist evaluation principles:

- **Quantitative component**: Endline survey in accessible programme areas, designed to be comparable with baseline. Accept that geographic coverage will be partial. Use phone-based surveys (CATI) for areas where face-to-face data collection is not feasible. Clearly document which areas are covered and which are not.
- **Qualitative component**: KIIs with programme staff, partners, government counterparts, and community leaders. FGDs with programme participants (where safe and accessible). Case studies in 2-3 sites per country representing different security contexts (accessible, partially accessible, remote-monitored).
- **Contribution story**: Build the programme's contribution story using Mayne's framework. Test each link in the ToC against available evidence. Identify alternative explanations for observed changes.
- **Realist lens**: Use CMO configurations to understand how the programme worked differently across the three country contexts and across varying security conditions. This will produce more useful findings than a pooled average effect estimate.

### 6.3. Addressing the Midline Gap in Burkina Faso

- Reconstruct a partial midline narrative using monitoring data, partner reports, and retrospective KIIs with programme staff
- Use recall-based questions in the endline survey (acknowledge the limitations of recall data)
- Treat Burkina Faso as a separate analytical strand rather than forcing comparability with Mali and Niger

### 6.4. Addressing Access Constraints

- Deploy a tiered data collection strategy:
  - **Tier 1 (accessible areas)**: Full face-to-face survey and qualitative data collection
  - **Tier 2 (restricted access)**: Phone-based surveys, remote KIIs, third-party monitoring data
  - **Tier 3 (inaccessible areas)**: Secondary data only (ACLED, displacement data, partner monitoring reports)
- Be transparent about coverage limitations in the evaluation report. Do not generalise findings from accessible areas to the entire programme footprint.

### 6.5. Ethical and Safety Protocols

- Commission a conflict sensitivity assessment of the evaluation design before fieldwork
- Develop enhanced data security protocols for P/CVE-sensitive data
- Train enumerators in do-no-harm approaches and trauma-informed interviewing
- Establish referral pathways for respondents who disclose exposure to violence
- Do not collect personally identifiable data linked to attitudes toward armed groups
- Use indirect questioning techniques for sensitive P/CVE outcome measures

### 6.6. Immediate Actions to Improve Evaluability

Before the endline evaluation begins:

1. **Verify and update the ToC** through a half-day workshop with programme staff across all three countries
2. **Assess baseline data quality** and document any comparability issues with planned endline instruments
3. **Compile and clean monitoring data** across all three countries
4. **Map current access constraints** with security focal points and develop the tiered data collection plan
5. **Review P/CVE outcome indicators** against available validated instruments for the Sahel context (consider BRAVE14 or adapted social cohesion measures)
