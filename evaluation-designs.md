## 1. Theory of Change

A Theory of Change (ToC) articulates the causal logic of how and why a programme is expected to produce its intended results. It is not a logframe. A ToC is richer, more explicit about assumptions, and maps the full causal chain including contextual factors.

### Components of a ToC

**Problem statement**: The issue the programme addresses, grounded in evidence. Include root causes and contributing factors. Avoid circular definitions (e.g., "the problem is the lack of the intervention").

**Target population**: Who the programme intends to benefit, directly and indirectly. Include demographic, geographic, and vulnerability characteristics.

**Inputs and activities**: What the programme does. Resources deployed, actions taken, services delivered.

**Outputs**: Direct, countable products of activities. Training sessions held, materials distributed, services provided. Outputs are within the programme's direct control.

**Outcomes (short, medium, long-term)**: Changes in behaviour, practice, capacity, access, or status among target populations. Organised by timeframe:
- Short-term (immediate to 6 months): Knowledge, awareness, attitudes, skills
- Medium-term (6 months to 2 years): Behaviour change, practice adoption, institutional change
- Long-term (2+ years): Sustained wellbeing improvements, systemic change

**Impact**: Higher-level, population-wide changes the programme contributes to (often shared with other actors). Aligned with national development plans, SDGs, or strategic objectives.

**Assumptions**: Conditions that must hold true for the causal logic to work. These are testable and should be monitored. Distinguish between:
- Causal assumptions: "If X, then Y because..."
- Contextual assumptions: "This works provided the political environment remains stable"
- Implementation assumptions: "Staff have the capacity to deliver the training"

**Risks**: Factors that could undermine the causal logic. Map risks to specific causal links.

**Evidence base**: What evidence supports each causal link? Prior evaluations, research literature, theory, practitioner knowledge. Flag where evidence is weak.

### ToC Quality Criteria

A strong ToC:
- Makes every causal link explicit and justified
- Identifies assumptions at each step, not just globally
- Distinguishes between what the programme controls (outputs) and what it influences (outcomes)
- Acknowledges alternative explanations for observed changes
- Is falsifiable: states conditions under which the theory would be wrong
- Is developed participatorily with stakeholders, not imposed by evaluators
- Is revisable based on emerging evidence

### Producing a ToC

When asked to develop or review a ToC:

1. Extract the implicit theory from programme documents (proposal, logframe, M&E plan)
2. Map the causal chain from inputs through to impact
3. Identify and articulate assumptions at each causal link
4. Flag gaps: missing links, unjustified assumptions, weak evidence
5. Produce both a narrative description and a visual diagram
6. For diagrams, use mermaid or describe the visual clearly enough for the user to render it

### Foundation level
Provide a simple linear pathway diagram with plain-language labels. Explain what assumptions are and why they matter. Use a template structure.

### Practitioner level
Produce a multi-pathway ToC with branching outcomes, feedback loops where relevant, and explicit assumption chains. Include evidence ratings for each causal link.

### Advanced level
Engage with contribution analysis pathways, nested ToCs for complex programmes, realist programme theory (Context-Mechanism-Outcome configurations), and the distinction between programme theory and implementation theory.

## 2. Logical Frameworks

The logframe is a summary matrix, not a substitute for a Theory of Change. When asked to develop or review a logframe:

### Standard Logframe Structure

| Level | Description | Indicators | Means of Verification | Assumptions |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Goal/Impact | Long-term change the programme contributes to | Population-level indicators | National surveys, admin data | External factors |
| Outcome/Purpose | Changes in behaviour, practice, or status among target groups | Outcome indicators (SMART) | Surveys, assessments, records | What must hold for outcomes to lead to impact |
| Outputs | Direct products of activities | Output indicators (countable) | Activity records, reports | What must hold for outputs to produce outcomes |
| Activities | What the programme does | Activity milestones | Work plans, budgets | What must hold for activities to produce outputs |
| Inputs | Resources deployed | Budget lines | Financial records | Resource availability |

### Indicator Quality (SMART+)

All indicators should be:
- **Specific**: Clearly defined, one thing measured
- **Measurable**: Quantifiable or verifiable with available methods
- **Achievable**: Realistic targets given context and resources
- **Relevant**: Directly linked to the result it measures
- **Time-bound**: Specified timeframe for measurement

Additionally, good indicators are:
- **Disaggregable**: By sex, age, disability, geography, and other relevant dimensions
- **Sensitive**: Capable of detecting meaningful change within the programme timeframe
- **Cost-effective**: Measurable without disproportionate data collection burden

### Common Logframe Problems

Flag these when reviewing:
- Indicators that measure activities, not outcomes ("number of trainings held" as an outcome indicator)
- Assumptions column left blank or filled with platitudes
- Missing baseline data or unrealistic targets
- Indicators that cannot be disaggregated
- Goal-level indicators the programme cannot plausibly influence
- Outputs and outcomes conflated

## 3. Results Frameworks

Results frameworks vary by donor. Common structures:

**USAID Results Framework**: Goal, Development Objectives (DOs), Intermediate Results (IRs), Sub-IRs. Each level has indicators and performance targets. Emphasises contribution to higher-level DOs.

**EU/EC Results Framework**: Overall objective, specific objectives, expected results, activities. Uses intervention logic aligned with EU programming guidelines.

**DFID/FCDO Logframe**: Impact, outcome, outputs, inputs. Uses milestone-based tracking and annual reviews. Emphasises value-for-money indicators.

**World Bank Results Framework**: Project Development Objective (PDO), PDO-level indicators, intermediate outcome indicators, disbursement-linked indicators (for PforR).

**UN Results Framework**: UNDAF/UNSDCF outcomes, agency-specific outputs, indicators aligned with SDG targets.

When the user specifies a donor, adapt the framework terminology and structure accordingly. When not specified, use the generic logframe structure.

## 4. Evaluation Matrix Construction

The evaluation matrix is the methodological backbone of an evaluation. It links every evaluation question to the criteria, indicators, data sources, collection methods, and analysis methods needed to answer it.

### Structure

| Evaluation Question | OECD-DAC Criterion | Judgement Criteria / Indicators | Data Sources | Collection Methods | Analysis Methods |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| EQ1: To what extent... | Effectiveness | Indicator 1.1, 1.2 | Beneficiary survey, project records, KIIs with partners | Household survey (n=X), KIIs (n=X) | Descriptive statistics, thematic analysis, triangulation |

### Construction Process

1. Derive evaluation questions from the ToR, evaluation purpose, and stakeholder priorities
2. Map each question to OECD-DAC criteria (or other organising framework)
3. For each question, define what would constitute a "good" answer (judgement criteria) and how you would observe it (indicators)
4. Identify what data you need and where it exists (data sources)
5. Specify how you will collect the data (methods and sample)
6. Specify how you will analyse the data to answer the question

### Quality Checks

- Every evaluation question has at least two independent data sources (triangulation)
- Collection methods are feasible within budget and timeline
- Analysis methods match the data types (don't propose regression for 15 observations)
- The matrix is internally consistent: what you collect is what you analyse, and what you analyse answers the question

## 5. Evaluation Questions

Good evaluation questions are:
- Answerable with available or collectible evidence
- Specific enough to guide methodology
- Broad enough to allow for unexpected findings
- Aligned with the evaluation purpose and intended use
- Prioritised (not everything can be answered equally well)

### Question Formulation

Use "to what extent" or "how" rather than "did" for outcome/impact questions. Binary questions (yes/no) rarely produce useful evaluation findings.

Weak: "Did the programme improve food security?"
Better: "To what extent has the programme contributed to improved food security among targeted households, and through which pathways?"

Group questions under OECD-DAC criteria or thematic areas. Limit to 8-12 main evaluation questions for a standard evaluation; more dilutes analytical depth.

### Sub-questions

Each main evaluation question can have 2-4 sub-questions that operationalise the main question. Sub-questions are more specific and directly guide data collection.

Main: "To what extent has the programme achieved its intended outcomes?"
Sub: "What changes in [specific outcome] have occurred among [target group] since baseline?"
Sub: "To what extent can observed changes be attributed to the programme rather than external factors?"
Sub: "What unintended outcomes (positive or negative) have emerged?"

## 6. Programme Theory and Causal Pathways

### Contribution Analysis

For evaluations where experimental designs are not feasible, contribution analysis provides a structured approach to assessing causal contribution:

1. Set out the attribution problem and the specific causal claim
2. Develop or refine the Theory of Change with stakeholders
3. Gather existing evidence on the ToC and its assumptions
4. Assemble and assess the contribution story
5. Seek out additional evidence to strengthen or weaken the story
6. Revise and strengthen the contribution story

### Realist Evaluation

For Advanced users. Based on Context-Mechanism-Outcome (CMO) configurations:

- **Context**: Pre-existing conditions that enable or constrain the mechanism
- **Mechanism**: The causal process triggered by the programme in a given context (not the activity itself, but the reasoning/response it triggers in participants)
- **Outcome**: The resulting change pattern

Realist evaluation asks: "What works, for whom, in what circumstances, and why?"

### Process Tracing

For Advanced users. A within-case analytical method for causal inference:

1. Specify the causal claim to be tested
2. Identify observable implications (evidence) you would expect if the claim is true
3. Classify evidence by probative value (straw-in-the-wind, hoop, smoking gun, doubly decisive)
4. Collect and assess evidence against these categories
5. Update confidence in the causal claim based on evidence found
