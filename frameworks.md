## 1. What is Evaluability Assessment?

Evaluability assessment (EA) is a systematic process for determining the extent to which a programme, policy, or intervention can be evaluated in a reliable and credible fashion. The OECD-DAC defines evaluability as "the extent to which an activity or project can be evaluated in a reliable and credible fashion" (OECD-DAC, 2010).

An EA is not an evaluation. It is a pre-evaluation exercise that examines whether the conditions are in place for a useful evaluation to be conducted. Failing to assess evaluability before launching an evaluation can lead to poorly timed evaluations, invalid findings, wasted resources, and findings that no one can use.

EAs have been used since the 1970s, initially by US government agencies (building on the work of Joseph Wholey), and since the early 2000s by international development agencies. They can be applied to individual projects, sets of projects, policy areas, country strategies, strategic plans, and partnerships.

### What an EA Produces

A good EA delivers:
- An assessment of the current state of evaluability across multiple dimensions
- Identification of specific gaps and weaknesses that would undermine an evaluation
- Actionable recommendations for improving evaluability (if the evaluation is not yet timely)
- Guidance on what type of evaluation is feasible given current conditions
- Recommendations on the timing, scope, and design of a future evaluation
- In some cases, a return-on-investment assessment: whether the expected learning justifies the cost and effort of the evaluation

## 2. When to Conduct an Evaluability Assessment

### Timing Options

An EA can be conducted at different points, each with different purposes and effects:

**Early in programme design (or shortly after approval)**:
- Focus: Is the Theory of Change clear and plausible? Are indicators defined? Is the M&E framework adequate?
- Effect: Can improve programme design and M&E planning. Widest impact on long-term evaluability.
- Limitation: Too early to assess data availability or stakeholder readiness for evaluation.

**During implementation (midpoint)**:
- Focus: Is the programme on track to be evaluable? Is monitoring data being collected as planned? Are baselines in place?
- Effect: Can address data gaps, improve monitoring, and set the stage for endline evaluation.
- Most common timing for international development EAs.

**Before commissioning a major evaluation**:
- Focus: Can this programme be evaluated right now, given what data exists, what access is available, and what stakeholders want to know?
- Effect: Informs evaluation design, prevents investment in evaluations that cannot deliver credible findings.
- Most practically useful for evaluation managers and commissioners.

### Triggers for an EA

Consider an EA when:
- A large or complex evaluation is being planned and the cost is significant
- The programme design is ambiguous or has evolved substantially since inception
- There are concerns about data quality or availability
- The operating context is volatile or has changed significantly
- Multiple donors or actors are involved and evaluation expectations may differ
- The programme has not been evaluated before and no M&E baseline exists
- There is uncertainty about what type of evaluation is feasible

## 3. Dimensions of Evaluability

Drawing on the synthesis by Rick Davies (2013, DFID Working Paper 40), evaluability can be assessed across three core dimensions:

### Dimension A: Evaluability "In Principle" (Programme Design and Theory)

This dimension asks: Does the programme have a sufficiently clear and plausible design to support an evaluation?

Key questions:
- Is there an explicit Theory of Change or programme logic? Is it documented?
- Is the ToC plausible, coherent, and supported by evidence? Are the causal links explicit?
- Are the assumptions identified and testable?
- Are programme objectives clear, specific, and measurable?
- Are results expected to be observable within the evaluation timeframe?
- Can the programme's contribution be distinguished from other factors?
- Is the programme sufficiently stable in design, or has it changed so much that original objectives no longer apply?

### Dimension B: Evaluability "In Practice" (Data Availability and M&E Systems)

This dimension asks: Does the data and institutional infrastructure exist to support an evaluation?

Key questions:
- Are indicators defined for all levels of the results chain (outputs, outcomes, impact)?
- Is baseline data available? Was it collected in a methodologically sound way?
- Is monitoring data being collected consistently and to adequate quality?
- Are comparison groups identifiable? Is comparison data available?
- Is programme coverage and targeting data available?
- Are there existing data sources (surveys, administrative data, census) that the evaluation could draw on?
- Does the programme have an M&E plan and an M&E officer/unit?
- Is data stored, managed, and accessible for analysis?
- Are there capacity gaps in M&E (staff skills, systems, resources)?

### Dimension C: Evaluability in Context (Stakeholder and Institutional Factors)

This dimension asks: Is there demand for the evaluation, and are the institutional conditions in place for it to be useful?

**Utility sub-dimension**:
- Who are the intended users of the evaluation? Are they identifiable and engaged?
- What are the intended uses (accountability, learning, decision-making)?
- Is there genuine demand for the evaluation, or is it driven solely by compliance?
- Will the findings influence decisions? What decisions are upcoming?
- Are stakeholders willing to engage with findings, including negative ones?

**Practicality sub-dimension**:
- Is the programme accessible for data collection (physical access, security, permissions)?
- Are key stakeholders (implementers, beneficiaries, partners, government) available and willing to participate?
- Are there resources (budget, time) to conduct the evaluation?
- Are there ethical review processes in place?
- Are there political sensitivities or conflicts of interest that could undermine the evaluation?

## 4. The Evaluability Assessment Process

### Step 1: Scope and Plan the EA

- Define the purpose of the EA (inform evaluation design, improve programme, decide whether to evaluate)
- Identify the scope (single project, portfolio, strategy)
- Determine the EA methodology: desk-based review, field-based assessment, or hybrid
- Identify the documents to review and stakeholders to consult
- Estimate timeline and resources (a desk-based EA can take 1-3 weeks; a field-based EA 4-8 weeks)

### Step 2: Collect and Review Evidence

**Document review**:
- Programme design documents (proposals, logframes, ToC, M&E frameworks)
- Monitoring data and reports (progress reports, data quality assessments)
- Prior evaluations, reviews, or assessments
- Contextual documents (conflict analyses, sector assessments, donor strategies)

**Stakeholder consultations**:
- Programme managers and M&E staff
- Implementing partners
- Donor/commissioner evaluation officers
- Key government counterparts
- Where feasible, intended beneficiaries

### Step 3: Analyse Against the Evaluability Dimensions

Apply the checklists below to systematically assess evaluability across the three dimensions. For each checklist item:
- Rate the current status (e.g., adequate, partially adequate, inadequate, not assessable)
- Identify specific gaps and the severity of each
- Distinguish between "obstacle" problems (must be resolved before an evaluation can proceed) and "improvement" problems (would enhance the evaluation but are not showstoppers)

### Step 4: Synthesise and Make Recommendations

The EA should produce clear, actionable recommendations:

**If the programme is evaluable now**: Recommend the evaluation type, design, and timing. Flag any remaining limitations to manage.

**If the programme is partially evaluable**: Recommend what can be evaluated now (which questions are answerable) and what steps must be taken to improve evaluability for the remaining questions. Provide a realistic timeline for achieving full evaluability.

**If the programme is not evaluable**: Explain why, identify the critical gaps, and recommend corrective actions with a timeline for reassessment. Consider whether lighter-touch alternatives (review, learning exercise, rapid assessment) could still produce useful findings.

## 5. Evaluability Assessment Checklists

These checklists synthesise the frameworks used by DFID, UNICEF, USAID, the World Bank, Austrian Development Cooperation, and other agencies. They should be adapted to the specific programme and context.

### Checklist A: Programme Design and Theory

A1. **Theory of Change**: Does the programme have an explicit ToC or logic model? Is it documented?

A2. **Causal logic**: Is the causal chain from activities to outputs to outcomes to impact clearly articulated? Are the links plausible and supported by evidence?

A3. **Assumptions**: Are the key assumptions at each step of the ToC identified? Are they testable?

A4. **Objectives clarity**: Are programme objectives specific enough to be measurable? Are they realistic given the timeframe and resources?

A5. **Attribution/contribution**: Can the programme's contribution to observed changes be plausibly assessed? Are there identifiable comparison conditions?

A6. **Timeframe**: Are the expected results observable within the proposed evaluation timeframe? Has sufficient time elapsed for outcomes to materialise?

A7. **Programme stability**: Has the programme design remained stable enough to evaluate, or has it changed so substantially that original objectives are no longer relevant?

A8. **Complexity**: Is the programme's complexity (multiple components, actors, levels) manageable within a single evaluation, or does it need to be broken into evaluable components?

### Checklist B: Data and M&E Systems

B1. **Indicators**: Are indicators defined for outputs, outcomes, and (where applicable) impact? Are they SMART and disaggregable?

B2. **Baseline**: Was baseline data collected? Is it methodologically sound? Does it cover the key outcome indicators?

B3. **Monitoring data**: Is routine monitoring data being collected as planned? What is the data quality (completeness, accuracy, timeliness)?

B4. **Comparison data**: Is data available for a comparison or control group? Are there existing surveys or datasets that could serve this purpose?

B5. **Data accessibility**: Can evaluators access the data? Are there data-sharing agreements, permissions, or security protocols needed?

B6. **M&E capacity**: Does the programme have adequate M&E staff, skills, and systems?

B7. **Prior evaluations**: Have there been previous evaluations or reviews? What did they find? Are their findings and data accessible?

### Checklist C: Institutional and Stakeholder Context

**Practicality**:

C1. **Physical access**: Can evaluators access programme sites and beneficiaries? Are there security, logistical, or administrative barriers?

C2. **Resources**: Is there sufficient budget and time allocated for the evaluation?

C3. **Stakeholder availability**: Are key informants (staff, partners, government, beneficiaries) available and willing to participate?

C4. **Coordination**: If multiple actors are involved, is there a coordination mechanism for the evaluation?

**Utility**:

C5. **Demand**: Is there genuine demand for evaluation findings? Who will use them, and for what?

C6. **Decision points**: Are there upcoming decisions (programme renewal, scale-up, policy change) that the evaluation could inform?

C7. **Learning orientation**: Is the programme/organisation open to critical findings? Is there a learning culture?

C8. **Influence risks**: Are there political sensitivities, conflicts of interest, or pressures that could compromise the evaluation's independence or utility?

## 6. From Assessment to Action

### Scoring and Prioritisation

Not all checklist items carry equal weight. Some issues are "showstoppers" (no viable ToC means no viable evaluation), while others are quality-enhancement issues (disaggregated data would be ideal but its absence does not prevent the evaluation entirely).

A practical approach:
- Classify each gap as **critical** (blocks the evaluation), **significant** (limits the evaluation's credibility or scope), or **minor** (reduces quality but is manageable)
- For critical gaps, specify the corrective action and timeline
- For significant gaps, assess whether the evaluation can still proceed with appropriate caveats
- For minor gaps, note them as limitations to be documented in the evaluation report

### Return on Investment Framing

For decision-makers, frame the EA findings in terms of return on investment: What will the evaluation cost (money, time, staff effort, stakeholder engagement)? What is the expected utility of the findings? Is the investment justified?

This is particularly useful when recommending against an evaluation or recommending a lighter-touch alternative.

### Linking EA to Evaluation Design

The EA should directly inform:
- Which evaluation questions are answerable with current data and conditions
- What evaluation design is feasible (experimental, quasi-experimental, theory-based, etc.)
- What additional data collection is needed and whether it is worth the investment
- What the evaluation's limitations will be (these should be transparent from the start)
- The optimal timing for the evaluation

### Design Recommendations by Evaluability Profile

When the EA is complete, use these mappings to recommend appropriate designs from `references/evaluation-designs.md`:

**Fully evaluable (strong ToC, baseline and comparison data, good access, clear demand)**:
- Full range of designs available. Match design to evaluation questions and causal ambition.
- Quasi-experimental designs (DiD, PSM, ITS) feasible if assignment mechanism supports them.
- Mixed-methods designs with sequential or convergent integration.

**Partially evaluable: weak or missing comparison group**:
- Contribution analysis (Mayne's framework) as the primary causal approach
- Realist evaluation if the programme operates across multiple contexts with varying outcomes
- Pre-post comparison with qualitative explanation of change processes
- ITS if time-series monitoring data exists

**Partially evaluable: access constraints (FCV settings)**:
- Remote data collection methods (CATI, SMS, third-party monitoring) combined with qualitative methods in accessible areas
- Tiered data collection strategy (face-to-face where possible, remote where not, secondary data for inaccessible areas)
- Contribution analysis or process tracing (can work with limited data)
- Outcome harvesting (does not require baseline or comparison data)
- Developmental evaluation if the programme is still adapting

**Partially evaluable: weak or missing baseline**:
- Retrospective baseline reconstruction through recall-based questions and document review (acknowledge limitations)
- Cross-sectional comparison with matched non-beneficiaries
- Outcome harvesting or Most Significant Change for qualitative evidence of change
- Contribution analysis using monitoring data and stakeholder evidence

**Partially evaluable: unclear or shifting Theory of Change**:
- Developmental evaluation if the programme is still evolving
- Goal-free evaluation to capture actual effects without being bound to original objectives
- Theory of Change reconstruction as a first step, then evaluation design based on the reconstructed ToC

**Not evaluable (critical gaps in multiple dimensions)**:
- Recommend corrective actions with a timeline for reassessment
- Consider lighter alternatives: learning review, process documentation, rapid assessment
- If the organisation insists on proceeding, be transparent about what the "evaluation" can and cannot deliver
