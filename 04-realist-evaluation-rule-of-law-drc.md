# FGD Guide: Community Climate Adaptation and Resilience Programme Endline

## Scenario

**Test prompt**: "Write me an FGD guide for smallholder farming households in a climate adaptation programme endline. The programme provided climate-smart agriculture training, drought-resistant seed varieties, weather-indexed crop insurance, and community water harvesting infrastructure across 6 districts in Malawi and Zambia. I need guides for two groups: programme participants and non-participants in the same communities."

---

## FGD Guide A: Programme Participants

### Target Group
Smallholder farmers (mixed gender, 6-10 participants) who participated in the climate adaptation programme. Conduct separate FGDs for women-only and mixed groups where gender dynamics may affect responses. Aim for diversity in age, farm size, and specific programme components received.

### Materials Needed
Flipchart paper, markers, printed seasonal calendar template, printed timeline (covering the programme period), small coloured cards (for ranking exercise), voice recorder (with permission).

---

### Opening (10 minutes)

**Facilitator introduction and ground rules**

"Thank you for joining us today. My name is [name] and this is [note-taker]. We are conducting a study to understand how farming has changed in your community over the past [X] years and what role, if any, the [programme name] has played. There are no right or wrong answers. We want to hear about your real experiences, both positive and negative. Everything you say here will be kept confidential and your names will not appear in any report. You are free to leave at any time. We would like to record this discussion so we don't miss anything. Is that acceptable to everyone?"

[Obtain verbal consent. Record consent on the consent form.]

**Warm-up question**

"To start, could each of you briefly tell us what you grow on your farm and how this last season went for you?"

[Allow each participant to respond briefly. This gets everyone talking and provides baseline context.]

---

### Section 1: Climate Experience and Vulnerability (15 minutes)

**Question 1**: "Thinking back over the past 5 years, what have been the biggest climate-related challenges for farming in this area?"

Probes:
- What types of weather events have you experienced? (drought, flooding, erratic rainfall, late onset, early cessation)
- Have these events become more frequent or severe compared to earlier years?
- Which of these events caused the most damage to your crops and livelihoods?

**Question 2**: "Before the programme, when a drought or flood hit, what did you do to cope?"

Probes:
- What strategies did you use? (selling livestock, reducing meals, borrowing, casual labour, migration)
- Were these strategies enough to recover, or did each shock push you further back?

---

### Section 2: Programme Experience and Uptake (20 minutes)

**Question 3**: "Which parts of the programme did you participate in, and what was most useful to you?"

[Use a card exercise: write each programme component on a separate card (climate-smart agriculture training, drought-resistant seeds, crop insurance, water harvesting). Ask participants to rank them from most to least useful, then discuss.]

Probes:
- Why did you rank [highest] as most useful?
- Was there anything you were offered but chose not to take up? Why?
- Were there things you needed that the programme did not provide?

**Question 4**: "Tell me about your experience with the climate-smart agriculture techniques. What did you try, and what happened?"

Probes:
- Which specific techniques did you adopt? (conservation agriculture, intercropping, mulching, agroforestry, soil and water conservation)
- Did you apply them on all your land or only part of it? Why?
- Did the techniques work as you expected? What went well and what didn't?
- Are you still using these techniques now, or have you gone back to previous practices? Why?

**Question 5**: "Tell me about the drought-resistant seed varieties. How did they perform compared to your traditional varieties?"

Probes:
- Did you plant only the new varieties or mix them with traditional ones?
- How did they perform during dry spells?
- What about taste, storage, and marketability? Were there trade-offs?
- Will you continue using them? Can you access them without the programme?

**Question 6**: "For those who received crop insurance, how was that experience?"

Probes:
- Did you understand how the insurance worked before you signed up?
- Did you receive a payout? If yes, how quickly and was it enough?
- If no payout, why not? Did you feel the trigger (rainfall threshold) was set correctly?
- Would you pay for this insurance yourself if the programme stopped subsidising it?

---

### Section 3: Changes and Outcomes (20 minutes)

**Question 7**: "Thinking about your farming and your household overall, what has changed since you joined the programme?"

Probes:
- Has your crop yield changed? In good seasons and in bad seasons?
- Has your ability to cope with a bad season changed? How?
- Has your household food situation changed?
- Has your income changed? Are you growing or selling different things?

**Question 8**: "Has anything changed in how your community manages water since the water harvesting infrastructure was built?"

Probes:
- Who uses the infrastructure? Is access fair?
- Has it made a difference during dry periods?
- Who maintains it? Is there a committee or contribution system?
- Do you think it will still be functioning in 5 years? Why or why not?

**Participatory timeline exercise** (if time allows):

On the flipchart, draw a timeline covering the programme period. Ask participants to mark:
- Major climate events (droughts, floods)
- When they joined the programme / received different inputs
- Key changes in their farming or household situation

Discuss the connections between events, programme inputs, and changes.

---

### Section 4: Sustainability and Future (10 minutes)

**Question 9**: "If the programme ended tomorrow, which changes would continue and which would stop?"

Probes:
- Which practices can you maintain on your own? Which require continued support?
- Can you access the seeds, insurance, and technical advice without the programme?
- What would you need to continue on your own?

**Question 10**: "Based on your experience, if you could advise the people running this programme, what would you tell them to do differently?"

---

### Closing (5 minutes)

"We are coming to the end of our discussion. Of everything we have talked about today, what is the single most important thing you would want the people reading this report to understand?"

"Is there anything important that we have not discussed?"

"Thank you very much for your time and for sharing your experiences. Your input is very valuable and will help improve future programmes."

---

## FGD Guide B: Non-Participants in Programme Communities

### Target Group
Smallholder farmers (mixed gender, 6-10 participants) in the same communities as the programme but who did not participate. This group provides comparison evidence on whether changes are programme-specific or community-wide.

### Adaptation from Guide A

The structure is similar but the framing shifts from "your experience with the programme" to "what has changed in farming and climate in your area." Key differences:

---

### Opening
Same as Guide A, but replace programme-specific language:

"We are studying how farming and climate have changed in this area over the past [X] years."

### Section 1: Climate Experience
Same as Guide A, Questions 1-2. Identical.

### Section 2: Awareness and Non-Participation (replaces Section 2)

**Question 3**: "Are you aware of the [programme name] that has been operating in this area? What do you know about it?"

Probes:
- What have you heard from neighbours or family who participated?
- Were you invited to participate? If yes, why did you choose not to?
- If you were not invited, do you know why? (targeting criteria, distance, timing)

**Question 4**: "Have you adopted any new farming practices in the past few years, whether or not they came from the programme?"

Probes:
- What practices? Where did you learn about them?
- Have you used drought-resistant seed varieties? Where did you get them?
- Have you tried any soil or water conservation techniques? What prompted you?

This question tests for spillover: are programme techniques spreading to non-participants through informal channels?

**Question 5**: "Has the water harvesting infrastructure in your area benefited you? How?"

Probes:
- Do you have access to it? Is it open to non-programme households?
- Has it changed water availability for your farm or household?

### Section 3: Changes and Outcomes (replaces Section 3)

**Question 6**: "Thinking about the past [X] years, has your farming situation improved, stayed the same, or gotten worse? Why?"

Probes:
- What has driven any changes?
- How do you cope when the rains are bad?
- Has your food situation or income changed?

**Question 7**: "Compared to your neighbours who participated in the programme, do you see differences in how they farm or how they cope with bad seasons?"

Probes:
- What differences do you observe?
- Do you attribute those differences to the programme, or to other factors?

### Section 4: Future
Same as Guide A, Questions 9-10, adapted for non-participants:

**Question 8**: "What support would be most useful to you for dealing with climate challenges in the future?"

### Closing
Same as Guide A.

## Facilitation Notes

- **Gender separation**: Conduct at least one women-only FGD per site. Women's experiences with climate adaptation, household decision-making, and programme access may differ substantially from men's and may not surface in mixed groups.
- **Power dynamics**: Avoid including community leaders or programme staff in participant FGDs. Their presence inhibits honest critique.
- **Sensitive topics**: Questions about food insecurity and coping strategies (meal reduction, distress migration) can be sensitive. Approach with care and do not press if participants show discomfort.
- **Language**: Conduct in the local language. Ensure the facilitator is fluent and can translate agricultural terminology accurately.
- **Seasonal timing**: Schedule FGDs after harvest when farmers are less busy and can reflect on the full season. Avoid planting season.
- **Duration**: Aim for 75-90 minutes. Do not exceed 2 hours.
- **Compensation**: Provide a small refreshment or transport reimbursement per local norms. Do not provide cash compensation that could incentivise participation for the wrong reasons.
