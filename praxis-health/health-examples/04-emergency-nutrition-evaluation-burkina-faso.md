# Worked Example: Evaluation of an Emergency Nutrition Response Programme in Burkina Faso

## Scenario

UNICEF and the Ministry of Health are commissioning an evaluation of the emergency nutrition response in the Centre-Nord and Sahel regions of Burkina Faso, which have experienced protracted conflict, displacement, and food insecurity since 2019. The programme (2022-2025, $25M from ECHO, USAID/BHA, and UNICEF own resources) scaled up CMAM services from 180 to 420 health facilities, deployed 1,800 community nutrition volunteers (relais communautaires) for MUAC screening and referral, established a real-time MUAC surveillance system via mobile phone reporting, and provided IYCF counselling through mother-to-mother support groups. The security situation restricts access to approximately 30% of the target area. IPC Acute Food Insecurity analysis classifies parts of both regions as Phase 3-4 (Crisis/Emergency). The most recent SMART survey (2024) found GAM prevalence of 14.2% (95% CI: 11.8-17.0%) in Sahel region and 10.5% (95% CI: 8.4-13.1%) in Centre-Nord. The evaluation must assess programme performance, coverage, and contribution to reducing acute malnutrition, and inform the design of the next phase.

## Sub-Skills Exercised

- RMNCAH and Nutrition (primary — UNICEF framework, CMAM cascade, SMART survey interpretation, anthropometry, IPC, SQUEAC/SLEAC, Sphere standards, IYCF, emergency nutrition)
- Epidemiological Methods (GAM/SAM prevalence, survey methodology, mortality measurement, surveillance data)
- HSS Evaluation (community health systems, supply chain for RUTF, HMIS data quality)
- Implementation Science (coverage, fidelity, adaptation in crisis context)
- Donor Frameworks (ECHO, USAID/BHA reporting)
- Health Economics (cost per SAM child treated, cost-efficiency)

## Evaluation Design

### Evaluation Purpose

Summative-formative: Assess programme performance and coverage (summative) and generate evidence for the next phase design (formative). The evaluation operates in a protracted crisis context where the programme is ongoing, not completed.

### Theory of Change

Grounded in the UNICEF Conceptual Framework for Malnutrition:

**Immediate causes targeted**: Inadequate dietary intake (through CMAM treatment and IYCF counselling) and disease (through referral linkages with health services and WASH messaging).

**Underlying causes targeted**: Inadequate care practices (IYCF counselling, mother-to-mother groups), household food insecurity (referral to food security partners, not directly addressed), and inadequate health services (CMAM service scale-up, community screening).

**Programme pathways**:
1. Community screening pathway: Relais communautaires conduct monthly MUAC screening → identify SAM/MAM cases → refer to health facility → admission to OTP/TSFP → treatment → recovery → follow-up
2. IYCF pathway: Mother-to-mother support groups → improved breastfeeding and complementary feeding practices → reduced incidence of malnutrition
3. Surveillance pathway: Real-time MUAC data from community screening → early warning of deterioration → triggers scale-up response

**Critical assumptions**: Health facilities remain accessible and functional; RUTF supply is uninterrupted; relais remain active and motivated; displaced populations can be reached; security allows access for supervision and monitoring.

### Evaluation Questions

**EQ1 (Coverage)**: What is the treatment coverage of the CMAM programme (OTP and TSFP) in accessible and hard-to-reach areas, and what are the main barriers to coverage?

**EQ2 (Performance)**: Does the CMAM programme meet Sphere minimum standards for treatment outcomes (recovery, death, default, non-response rates)?

**EQ3 (Effectiveness)**: To what extent has the programme contributed to reducing acute malnutrition prevalence in the target regions, and through which pathways?

**EQ4 (IYCF)**: To what extent have IYCF practices improved among the target population, and is there evidence linking IYCF counselling to improved infant feeding?

**EQ5 (Adaptation)**: How has the programme adapted to the security and access constraints, and how have these adaptations affected programme quality and coverage?

**EQ6 (Efficiency)**: What is the cost per SAM child treated and cost per MAM child treated, and how do these compare with regional benchmarks?

**EQ7 (Sustainability and Transition)**: What elements of the emergency nutrition response can be transitioned to the routine health system, and what requires continued emergency financing?

### Methodology

#### SQUEAC for Coverage Assessment (EQ1)

Use Semi-Quantitative Evaluation of Access and Coverage (SQUEAC) in both regions. SQUEAC is the appropriate method for CMAM coverage assessment because it is designed for the specific challenges of measuring treatment coverage in emergency settings.

**Stage 1 — Identify areas of high and low coverage**: Analyse routine programme data (admissions, defaulters, MUAC screening data) and conduct qualitative investigations (small-area surveys, key informant discussions, focus groups with caregivers) to identify barriers and boosters to coverage and map likely areas of high and low coverage.

**Stage 2 — Confirm coverage hypothesis**: Conduct small-area surveys in areas identified as high and low coverage to verify the hypotheses from Stage 1. Use active and adaptive case-finding to identify SAM cases in the community (MUAC screening of all children 6-59 months in sampled communities), then determine whether identified cases are in the programme (covered) or not (not covered).

**Stage 3 — Estimate overall coverage**: Use a Bayesian approach combining the prior (from Stages 1-2) with a wide-area survey to estimate overall programme coverage with a credible interval.

**Coverage types**: Report point coverage (proportion of current SAM cases currently in the programme), period coverage (proportion of SAM cases who were in the programme at any point during a defined period), and treatment coverage (point coverage adjusted for recovering cases).

**Access-restricted areas**: For the 30% of the target area with restricted access, use remote data sources where available (mobile phone MUAC data, partner reports) and clearly flag that coverage estimates exclude these areas. Conduct proxy estimation using available community volunteer screening data from border areas and displacement sites receiving populations from restricted areas.

#### CMAM Programme Performance Analysis (EQ2)

Analyse treatment outcomes from OTP and TSFP registers across all 420 programme sites. Calculate:

| Indicator | Formula | Sphere Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Recovery rate | Recovered / Total discharges | >75% |
| Death rate | Died / Total discharges | <10% (OTP), <3% (TSFP) |
| Default rate | Defaulted / Total discharges | <15% |
| Non-response rate | Non-responders / Total discharges | — |
| Average length of stay | Sum of treatment days / Number recovered | Report (no standard, but 4-8 weeks typical for OTP) |
| Average weight gain | Sum of weight gain (g/kg/day) / Number recovered | >8 g/kg/day for SAM |

Disaggregate by region, district, facility type, admission criteria (MUAC vs. WHZ vs. oedema), sex, and age group. Compare performance across quarters to assess trends. Identify facilities with performance below Sphere standards and investigate root causes (late presentation, supply gaps, staffing, protocol adherence).

**Data quality**: Verify a random sample of treatment cards against register entries at 20% of facilities. Assess completeness of outcome recording — facilities with high "not evaluated" or "transferred out" proportions are masking true outcomes.

#### Contribution Analysis for Malnutrition Reduction (EQ3)

Direct attribution of malnutrition prevalence reduction to the programme is not possible because:
- No comparison group exists (the programme covers the entire target area)
- Multiple concurrent factors affect malnutrition (food security, rainfall, conflict dynamics, other humanitarian programmes)
- Cross-sectional SMART surveys measure prevalence, not incidence — prevalence can decrease due to treatment (the programme) or increased mortality among malnourished children (not the programme)

Use contribution analysis:
1. **Causal claim**: The CMAM programme contributed to preventing deterioration in acute malnutrition prevalence (or reducing it from what it would have been without the programme)
2. **Evidence for the ToC**: Community screening coverage, treatment admission numbers, treatment outcomes, RUTF supply continuity
3. **Alternative explanations**: Improved food security (assess using IPC data and food security partner reports), seasonal effects (compare SMART survey timing across years), displacement patterns (population changes affect denominators), other health and nutrition programmes
4. **Contribution story**: Assemble evidence assessing the plausibility of the programme's contribution relative to alternative explanations

Supplement with dose-response analysis: correlate programme intensity (admissions per 1,000 children, screening coverage, number of active relais per commune) with GAM prevalence at the district level, controlling for IPC phase, displacement, and food distribution coverage. This is ecological analysis and subject to the ecological fallacy — use as supportive evidence within the contribution story, not as standalone causal evidence.

#### SMART Survey Interpretation (EQ3)

Critically interpret the existing SMART survey data:

**Quality assessment**: Review SMART plausibility reports for both surveys. Check:
- Overall data quality score (acceptable, problematic, serious)
- Age and sex distribution (ratio, digit preference)
- Standard deviation of WHZ (0.8-1.2 acceptable)
- Percentage of flags excluded
- Mortality data quality (recall period, household definition, dissolved households)

**Trend analysis**: Compare 2024 SMART results with previous surveys (2022, 2023 if available). Assess whether trends are consistent with programme performance data. If GAM has decreased while programme admissions increased, this is consistent with (but not proof of) programme contribution. If GAM increased despite programme scale-up, investigate whether the nutrition situation deteriorated faster than the programme could respond.

**GAM decomposition**: Report GAM, SAM, and MAM separately. Report both WHZ-based and MUAC-based classification. Analyse oedema prevalence separately (oedema indicates kwashiorkor, which has different aetiology and programme implications). Report concurrent wasting and stunting prevalence as a severity indicator.

#### IYCF Assessment (EQ4)

Conduct a cross-sectional IYCF practices survey in a subsample of communities with and without mother-to-mother support groups. Measure all standard WHO IYCF indicators. Compare practices between intervention and comparison communities using multivariate analysis controlling for maternal education, wealth, food security, and access to health services.

Supplement with qualitative assessment: FGDs with mother-to-mother group participants and non-participants exploring knowledge, motivations, barriers, and perceived changes in feeding practices.

#### Adaptation Assessment (EQ5)

Document programme adaptations through KIIs with programme managers, facility staff, and relais communautaires:
- Remote supervision modalities (phone-based, proxy supervision through community leaders)
- Modified supply chain for RUTF delivery to insecure areas (reduced frequency, larger deliveries, buffer stock strategies)
- Community-based treatment in areas without functioning health facilities
- Use of mobile phone MUAC data for real-time monitoring when physical supervision is impossible
- Role of local community structures in facilitating access

Assess each adaptation using the fidelity-adaptation framework: identify core components (CMAM protocol, MUAC-based screening and admission, RUTF dosing) vs. adaptable periphery (supervision modality, supply frequency, data reporting method). Determine whether adaptations preserved core components or compromised them.

#### Costing (EQ6)

Calculate cost per child treated for SAM (OTP) and MAM (TSFP) using programme financial records:

**Cost categories**: RUTF and therapeutic milk, other medical supplies, staffing (facility and community), training, supervision, supply chain (transport, storage, last-mile delivery), coordination and management, monitoring and evaluation.

**Cost per SAM child treated** = Total OTP programme cost / Total SAM children admitted (or recovered, depending on whether you want cost per admission or cost per successful treatment).

**Cost per MAM child treated** = Total TSFP programme cost / Total MAM children admitted.

**Benchmark comparison**: Compare with published CMAM cost data from similar settings. The Evidence to Action (E2A) project, UNICEF costing studies, and published literature provide regional benchmarks. Cost per SAM child treated typically ranges from $150-350 in the Sahel depending on context, RUTF procurement price, and programme intensity.

**Cost-efficiency analysis**: Assess unit cost variation across districts and identify cost drivers. High unit costs may indicate low caseload (fixed costs spread over few children), supply chain inefficiency (high RUTF wastage or transport costs), or overstaffing. Low unit costs may indicate high efficiency or may indicate underinvestment (inadequate supervision, low screening coverage).

#### Sustainability and Transition Assessment (EQ7)

Assess each programme component on a transition readiness spectrum:

- **CMAM treatment at facility level**: Can the Ministry of Health sustain CMAM within the routine health system? Assess integration into facility service packages, government drug supply, health worker capacity, and recurrent financing.
- **Community screening**: Can relais communautaires be sustained? Assess government community health worker policy, motivation structures, and supply of MUAC tapes.
- **RUTF supply**: What is the government's capacity to procure and distribute RUTF? Assess national nutrition commodity procurement, budget allocation, and supply chain integration.
- **Surveillance**: Can the mobile MUAC surveillance system be maintained? Assess government ownership, technical capacity, and financing.

Distinguish between components that can transition to routine systems (CMAM in stable areas with functional health facilities) and components that require continued emergency financing (hard-to-reach areas, mobile populations, surge capacity for acute deterioration).

### Reporting

**Primary report**: Full evaluation report with findings by evaluation question, synthesis, and recommendations.

**Donor-specific outputs**:
- ECHO: Report against ECHO Single Form indicators and the Humanitarian Aid Results Framework. Present VfM assessment using cost per beneficiary and cost per SAM/MAM child treated.
- USAID/BHA: Report against BHA emergency nutrition indicators. Present findings aligned with the BHA Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning plan.

**Operational outputs**: Two-page CMAM performance summary per district for district health teams. Coverage map with barriers analysis for programme targeting.

**Next-phase design brief**: 5-page evidence brief synthesising evaluation findings into concrete recommendations for the next programme phase, including coverage strategy, adaptation protocols, transition roadmap, and budget implications.

### Ethical Considerations

- National ethics committee approval and local administrative authorisation
- Informed consent adapted for the context: verbal consent in local languages, witnessed and documented; specific consent provisions for displaced populations who may fear documentation
- Referral protocol: Any child identified as SAM during evaluation data collection activities must be immediately referred to the nearest treatment site — evaluation cannot withhold services
- Security: Evaluation team movement governed by security protocols; data collection suspended in areas with active fighting; evaluator safety takes precedence over data completeness
- Data protection: GPS coordinates of vulnerable communities not included in reports; individual-level data anonymised and stored securely; beneficiary lists not shared outside the evaluation team
- Cultural sensitivity: Gender-separated data collection where culturally required; female enumerators for household surveys in conservative areas; engagement with community leaders before data collection
