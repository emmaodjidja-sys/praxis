# Worked Example: Evaluation of a National Universal Health Coverage Reform in Kenya

## Scenario

The Government of Kenya launched its Universal Health Coverage (UHC) reform in 2018, with a phased rollout initially covering 4 pilot counties before expanding to all 47 counties by 2024. The reform package includes: (1) expansion of the National Hospital Insurance Fund (NHIF) to cover all citizens, with government-subsidised premiums for the poorest 20%; (2) a revised essential benefits package covering primary care, maternal health, chronic disease management, and mental health; (3) performance-based financing for county health facilities linked to quality and utilisation indicators; (4) community health volunteer (CHV) programme expansion under the Community Health Strategy; (5) HMIS strengthening through full DHIS2 rollout and a new electronic medical records system; and (6) supply chain reform including pooled county procurement of essential medicines. The reform is supported by multiple donors: World Bank ($200M PforR operation with disbursement-linked indicators), Global Fund ($45M HSS grant aligned with UHC goals), USAID ($80M bilateral health system strengthening activity), and FCDO ($30M governance and accountability programme). Total government domestic commitment is KES 120 billion over 5 years. An independent evaluation has been commissioned jointly by the Ministry of Health, the World Bank, and the donor coordination group, with a budget of $2.5M over 18 months.

## Sub-Skills Exercised

All seven sub-skills simultaneously:

- **HSS Evaluation**: All six building blocks, system-level ToC, PBF, health financing, governance, health workforce, HMIS, supply chain, system-level properties (equity, resilience, sustainability, efficiency), political economy, multi-level evaluation
- **Epidemiological Methods**: Coverage estimation, mortality trends, routine data quality, population survey interpretation, standardisation
- **Health Economics**: Cost-effectiveness of the subsidy, financial protection measurement, catastrophic health expenditure, budget impact, NHA, cost-efficiency
- **Disease Programme Evaluation**: HIV, TB, malaria cascade performance under UHC, NCD service coverage, immunisation coverage
- **RMNCAH and Nutrition**: Continuum of care under UHC, EmOC, quality of maternal care, child health coverage
- **Implementation Science**: CFIR across 47 counties, fidelity-adaptation of the reform package, RE-AIM dimensions, scale-up from pilots
- **Donor Frameworks**: World Bank ICR/PforR, Global Fund, USAID, FCDO VfM — all simultaneously with harmonised reporting

## Evaluation Design

### Evaluation Purpose

Summative: Assess the overall contribution of the UHC reform to progress toward universal health coverage (population coverage, service coverage, financial protection). Formative: Generate evidence on what is working, what is not, and what needs adjustment for the next reform phase. Accountability: Assess performance against World Bank DLIs and donor-specific targets.

### System-Level Theory of Change

The UHC reform operates through a system-level ToC with six interacting pathways converging on three UHC outcomes (the UHC cube: population coverage × service coverage × financial protection):

**Financing pathway**: NHIF expansion + premium subsidies → increased insurance enrolment → reduced financial barriers → increased utilisation → improved financial protection

**Service delivery pathway**: Revised benefits package + PBF incentives → expanded and improved service availability and quality → increased effective coverage → improved health outcomes

**Community pathway**: CHV expansion → increased health promotion, screening, referral → earlier care-seeking, improved demand → increased coverage of preventive and curative services

**Data pathway**: DHIS2 + EMR → improved data quality and use → evidence-based planning and resource allocation → more equitable and efficient service delivery

**Supply chain pathway**: Pooled procurement → reduced stockout rates → improved service readiness → better quality of care

**Governance pathway**: Devolved health management + PBF accountability mechanisms + citizen engagement → improved governance and responsiveness → better resource utilisation and accountability

**Cross-pathway interactions**: DHIS2 enables PBF verification. PBF incentivises both quality and data reporting. CHVs generate demand that PBF incentivises facilities to meet. Supply chain improvement enables both facilities and CHVs to deliver services. Governance reform enables all other pathways to function in a devolved system where 47 counties have significant autonomy.

### Evaluation Questions

**EQ1 (Population coverage)**: To what extent has NHIF enrolment increased, particularly among the poorest populations, and what are the barriers to enrolment and retention?

**EQ2 (Service coverage)**: To what extent has the UHC reform increased coverage and effective coverage of essential health services across the continuum of care?

**EQ3 (Financial protection)**: Has the reform reduced catastrophic and impoverishing health expenditure, and how does financial protection vary by wealth, geography, and insurance status?

**EQ4 (Quality)**: Has the quality of care improved in reform facilities, and what has been the contribution of PBF, training, and supply chain reform to quality changes?

**EQ5 (Equity)**: Has the reform reduced equity gaps in coverage, utilisation, quality, and financial protection across wealth quintiles, counties, urban/rural, and gender?

**EQ6 (Health outcomes)**: What changes in health outcomes (disease-specific and all-cause) have occurred during the reform period, and to what extent can they be attributed to the reform?

**EQ7 (Implementation)**: How has the reform been implemented across counties, what explains variation in implementation quality and outcomes, and what adaptations have been made?

**EQ8 (Efficiency)**: Has the reform improved health system efficiency, and what is the cost-effectiveness of the NHIF subsidy?

**EQ9 (Sustainability)**: Is the reform fiscally and institutionally sustainable? What are the main risks?

**EQ10 (Governance)**: How has devolution affected reform implementation, and what governance mechanisms have facilitated or hindered reform success?

### Methodology

#### Overarching Design: Theory-Based Mixed-Methods Evaluation

No single evaluation design can credibly answer all 10 questions. The evaluation uses contribution analysis as the overarching causal framework, with embedded quasi-experimental components for specific questions.

#### Quantitative Component

**Difference-in-Differences (EQ2, EQ4, EQ6)**: The phased rollout (4 pilot counties 2018, remaining counties 2019-2024) creates a natural experiment. Compare trends in coverage, quality, and outcome indicators between early-adopting pilot counties and late-adopting counties. Use monthly DHIS2 data for ITS within each group and DiD across groups.

**Parallel trends assessment**: Test parallel trends in the 24+ months before the pilot counties received the reform. If parallel trends do not hold (likely, since pilot counties were selected partly on readiness), use synthetic control to construct a weighted comparator from late-adopting counties.

**Pre-post with comparison for PBF (EQ4)**: Within counties, compare PBF facilities with non-PBF facilities (if rollout was not universal within counties). If all facilities in reform counties received PBF, use the temporal variation in PBF start dates.

**Household health expenditure survey (EQ3)**: Commission a dedicated household survey module in 6 counties (stratified by reform phase, wealth, urban/rural) measuring out-of-pocket health expenditure, NHIF utilisation, care-seeking patterns, and financial coping strategies. Calculate CHE at 10% and 25% thresholds. Compare with the 2018 Kenya Household Health Expenditure Survey (pre-reform baseline) for pre-post analysis.

**Facility assessment (EQ2, EQ4)**: Conduct a service readiness and quality assessment in a stratified sample of 200 facilities (across county types, facility types, PBF/non-PBF). Use the harmonised health facility assessment (HHFA) tool. Include direct clinical observation of ANC, delivery, child illness, and NCD consultations. Compare with the 2018 SPA (pre-reform baseline).

**Data quality assessment**: Conduct a WHO Data Quality Review in 10 counties before using DHIS2 data for any quantitative analysis. Assess reporting completeness, verification ratios, and internal consistency. Exclude counties with completeness <80% from the ITS analysis.

#### Qualitative Component

**CFIR-informed county case studies (EQ7, EQ10)**: Conduct in-depth case studies in 8 purposively selected counties (maximum variation on reform performance, wealth, devolution maturity, urban/rural):
- County health leadership (County Executive Committee Member for Health, Chief Officer, County Director of Health)
- Facility managers and health workers
- Community health volunteers and community health committee members
- NHIF county staff
- Civil society and citizen accountability groups
- Beneficiaries and non-beneficiaries

Code to CFIR constructs and governance dimensions. Use cross-case analysis (QCA logic with 8 cases) to identify which configurations of context, governance, and implementation quality produce good reform outcomes.

**Political economy analysis (EQ10)**: Conduct targeted PEA in 4 counties and at national level. Assess: political dynamics of devolution (national-county relations), county health budget politics (competition with other sectors), NHIF governance (board composition, political interests), provider payment politics (resistance from health workers, professional associations), and civil society accountability mechanisms.

#### Economic Component

**Cost-efficiency analysis (EQ8)**: Calculate unit costs (cost per outpatient visit, per inpatient day, per delivery, per insured person, per CHV deployed) from facility costing data and NHA. Compare across counties and over time. Use DEA to assess relative efficiency of county health systems.

**CEA of the NHIF subsidy (EQ8)**: Estimate the cost-effectiveness of the premium subsidy for the poorest 20%:
- Cost: Government subsidy per insured person per year × number subsidised
- Outcomes: Additional service utilisation, CHE averted, DALYs averted (modelled from utilisation changes and effectiveness evidence)
- ICER: Cost per additional utilisation event, cost per CHE case averted, cost per DALY averted
- Compare with alternative uses of the same fiscal resources (expanding supply-side investments, direct cash transfers)

**Fiscal sustainability analysis (EQ9)**: Analyse NHIF revenue-expenditure balance, government health budget trends (as % of total government budget and % of GDP), donor financing trends and transition projections, and the fiscal space for health. Assess whether the reform is financially sustainable under realistic domestic revenue scenarios, or whether it depends on continued external financing.

#### Equity Analysis (EQ5)

Cross-cutting across all questions. For every indicator:
- Disaggregate by wealth quintile (using DHS asset index or household survey equivalent)
- Disaggregate by county (as a proxy for geographic equity and governance capacity)
- Disaggregate by urban/rural
- Disaggregate by sex where relevant
- Calculate absolute and relative equity gaps
- Calculate concentration indices for key coverage and financial protection indicators
- Conduct benefit-incidence analysis: who benefits from public health spending under the reform?

Track whether equity gaps are narrowing or widening over the reform period. If coverage is increasing but equity gaps are widening, the reform is disproportionately benefiting the better-off — a common pattern in UHC reforms that must be flagged.

### Multi-Donor Reporting

The evaluation serves four donors simultaneously. Harmonise through:

**Single comprehensive evaluation report** following UNEG quality standards, with findings organised by evaluation question. This is the primary product.

**World Bank annex**: Assessment of each Disbursement-Linked Indicator (DLI) — achieved, partially achieved, not achieved. Performance ratings aligned with ICR methodology (PDO achievement, implementation performance, M&E quality). Lessons for the Bank's health PforR portfolio.

**Global Fund annex**: Assessment of RSSH grant contribution to UHC reform. Findings mapped to the Global Fund modular framework. Contribution to disease outcomes (HIV, TB, malaria) through system-level pathways.

**USAID annex**: Learning-focused summary aligned with CLA framework. Findings mapped to USAID health system strengthening indicators. Emphasis on adaptive management recommendations.

**FCDO annex**: Full VfM assessment using the 4E framework. Cost per outcome, equity analysis, governance and accountability findings.

**Ministry of Health products**: Policy brief (4 pages), county-level scorecards (1 page each for all 47 counties), and a presentation for the Health Sector Working Group.

### Limitations

- The phased rollout is a useful natural experiment but county selection was not random — pilot counties were selected on political and logistical criteria, biasing naive DiD estimates. Synthetic control partially addresses this but cannot fully eliminate selection bias.
- The reform is multi-component — isolating the effect of any single component (NHIF vs. PBF vs. supply chain vs. CHV) is methodologically difficult. Contribution analysis assesses plausibility but cannot provide component-specific attribution.
- DHS 2022 provides one post-reform data point for survey-based indicators. Trend analysis of survey-based coverage indicators is limited to two points (DHS 2014 vs. 2022), though DHIS2 data provide monthly trends.
- Financial protection measurement requires household expenditure data that are expensive and infrequent. The dedicated household survey module covers only 6 counties and is not nationally representative.
- QCA with 8 county cases is methodologically appropriate but the findings are context-specific and may not generalise to all 47 counties.
- The evaluation timeline (18 months) is short relative to the reform timeline (6+ years). Some reform effects, particularly on mortality and chronic disease outcomes, may not yet be observable.
- Political sensitivity: UHC reform is a presidential priority. The evaluation must maintain independence while navigating political dynamics. Publish all findings regardless of whether they support the reform narrative.

### Ethical Considerations

- National ethics approval (NACOSTI) and county-level approvals
- Informed consent for all primary data collection
- Patient-level data from EMR and NHIF accessed under data sharing agreements; all data anonymised before analysis
- Evaluation independence protocol: evaluation steering committee includes government, donors, and civil society; evaluation team has final authority over findings and recommendations
- Findings on county performance published as comparative analysis, not rankings — to avoid political weaponisation of evaluation data
- Financial and expenditure data treated with sensitivity — PFM weaknesses reported constructively with system-strengthening recommendations, not as accusations
