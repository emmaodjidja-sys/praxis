## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill guides the economic evaluation of health programmes and interventions. It covers the methods evaluators need to estimate costs, compare costs with outcomes, and present economic evidence for decision-making.

### What Belongs Here

Cost-effectiveness analysis, cost-utility analysis, cost-benefit analysis, budget impact analysis, costing studies, efficiency analysis, willingness-to-pay studies, and any evaluation component that compares the costs of an intervention with its health or economic outcomes.

### What Belongs Elsewhere

- Health financing system evaluation (insurance, PBF, fiscal space) → HSS Evaluation sub-skill, Section 3.5
- DALYs and QALYs as epidemiological measures → Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 2
- Efficiency analysis at the facility level (DEA, SFA) → HSS Evaluation sub-skill, Section 4
- SROI for non-health programmes → PRAXIS core

### When Evaluators Encounter Economic Evaluation

Economic evaluation appears in health programme evaluation in several ways:

- **As a standalone component**: A cost-effectiveness study conducted alongside or after an impact evaluation. Common in donor-funded health programmes, particularly for USAID, DFID/FCDO, and Global Fund evaluations.
- **As a value-for-money assessment**: Donors increasingly require VfM analysis as part of programme evaluation. FCDO uses the 4E framework (economy, efficiency, effectiveness, equity). USAID and the Global Fund require cost analysis in impact evaluations.
- **As part of decision-making**: Cost-effectiveness evidence informs resource allocation, programme scale-up, and priority-setting. Health Technology Assessment (HTA) bodies use economic evaluation to recommend which interventions to fund.
- **As a retrospective exercise**: Estimating the cost per outcome achieved for a completed programme, typically using evaluation outcome data and programme expenditure records.

Many programme evaluators are not health economists. This sub-skill provides sufficient guidance for evaluators to conduct basic costing and cost-effectiveness analysis, commission more complex economic evaluations competently, and interpret economic evidence critically.

### Relationship to Other Sub-Skills

- **HSS Evaluation**: Covers health financing systems and efficiency at the facility/system level. This sub-skill covers the analytical methods for comparing costs and outcomes of specific interventions.
- **Epidemiological Methods**: Defines DALYs and QALYs. This sub-skill uses these metrics as denominators in cost-effectiveness and cost-utility analysis.
- **Disease Programme Evaluation**: Uses cost-effectiveness evidence to compare disease intervention strategies. This sub-skill provides the methods.

## 2. Types of Economic Evaluation

### Full Economic Evaluation

A full economic evaluation compares both the costs and consequences of two or more alternatives. There are four main types, distinguished by how consequences (outcomes) are measured:

| Type | Cost Measure | Outcome Measure | Result Expressed As | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA) | Monetary | Natural units (lives saved, cases averted, years of schooling) | Cost per unit of outcome (e.g., cost per DALY averted, cost per life saved) | Comparing interventions with the same type of outcome |
| Cost-Utility Analysis (CUA) | Monetary | Quality-adjusted life years (QALYs) or DALYs | Cost per QALY gained or cost per DALY averted | Comparing interventions with different types of outcomes using a common metric |
| Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) | Monetary | Monetary (outcomes converted to money) | Net benefit (benefits − costs) or benefit-cost ratio | When outcomes can be monetised; cross-sectoral comparisons |
| Cost-Minimisation Analysis (CMA) | Monetary | Assumed equivalent | Lowest cost option | Only when outcomes are demonstrably equivalent |

Cost-utility analysis is technically a subset of cost-effectiveness analysis (DALYs/QALYs are the outcome measure), but is often distinguished because of the specific methods required to generate utility weights.

### Partial Economic Evaluation

Partial evaluations examine costs or consequences but not both comparatively:

- **Cost analysis / cost description**: Documents the costs of an intervention without comparing with alternatives or outcomes. Useful as a building block for full economic evaluation or for budget planning.
- **Cost-of-illness study**: Estimates the economic burden of a disease or condition. Not a programme evaluation method, but evaluators encounter these studies as background evidence.
- **Outcome description**: Documents outcomes without cost comparison. This is what most programme evaluations do — the economic evaluation adds the cost dimension.

### The Comparator Problem

Every economic evaluation compares the intervention against something. The choice of comparator fundamentally affects the results.

**Common comparators**:
- Status quo / current practice: The most common and usually most relevant comparator. What would have happened without the programme?
- Do-nothing: No intervention at all. Rarely realistic but sometimes used when no current practice exists.
- Next-best alternative: Another intervention that could achieve similar outcomes. Most useful for resource allocation decisions.
- Minimum package: The most basic version of the intervention, against which enhanced versions are compared.

**Guidance for evaluators**: Always state the comparator explicitly. If the evaluation uses a comparison group (as in a quasi-experimental design), the comparator in the economic evaluation should reflect what the comparison group actually received, which is often existing services rather than "nothing." If no comparison group exists, the comparator must be constructed through modelling or assumption — state this transparently.

## 3. Costing Methods

### Perspectives

The costing perspective determines whose costs are included. The choice of perspective significantly affects results and must be stated explicitly.

**Provider/programme perspective**: Costs incurred by the organisation delivering the intervention. Includes personnel, supplies, equipment, training, supervision, transport, and overhead. The most common perspective in programme evaluation.

**Health system perspective**: All costs to the health system, including provider costs plus costs incurred at other levels (district management, national coordination, referral facilities). Broader than the provider perspective.

**Societal perspective**: All costs regardless of who bears them, including health system costs, patient/household costs (transport, lost income, out-of-pocket payments), and broader economic costs (productivity losses, informal caregiving). The most comprehensive but most data-intensive perspective.

**Patient/household perspective**: Costs borne by patients and their families. Includes direct costs (transport, user fees, medicines purchased privately) and indirect costs (lost wages, opportunity cost of time). Important for evaluating financial protection and equity but often neglected.

**Recommendation**: At minimum, conduct the analysis from the provider/programme perspective (the standard for most programme evaluations). If the evaluation has equity objectives or is assessing financial protection, add the patient/household perspective. Report the perspective clearly and discuss how results might differ under alternative perspectives.

### Costing Approaches

**Ingredients-based (bottom-up) costing**: Identify and quantify every resource (ingredient) used by the intervention, then assign a unit cost to each. The most transparent and accurate method.

Steps:
1. **Identify resources**: List all inputs required for the intervention (staff time, supplies, equipment, facilities, training, supervision, transport, overhead)
2. **Quantify resource use**: Measure how much of each input is used (hours of staff time, number of supplies consumed, proportion of facility space used)
3. **Assign unit costs**: Apply a monetary value to each unit of resource. Use actual prices paid (financial costs) or economic costs (including the value of donated resources and opportunity costs)
4. **Calculate total cost**: Multiply quantities by unit costs and sum

**Top-down (expenditure-based) costing**: Use financial records (budgets, expenditure reports) to determine total programme spending, then allocate across activities, outputs, or sites. Faster but less transparent — cannot distinguish efficient from inefficient resource use.

**Combination approach**: Use top-down data for overall programme expenditure and bottom-up data for specific cost components where detailed analysis is needed. Common in practice.

### Financial Costs vs. Economic Costs

**Financial costs**: Actual monetary expenditures — what was spent. Derived from accounting records, budgets, and financial reports.

**Economic costs**: The value of all resources used, including those not purchased. Economic costs include:
- **Opportunity costs**: The value of resources in their next-best use. A government health worker seconded to the programme has an opportunity cost equal to their salary even if the programme does not pay it.
- **Donated goods and volunteer time**: Valued at market price or replacement cost.
- **Existing infrastructure**: If the programme uses existing facilities or equipment, include an annualised value even if no rent or purchase cost was incurred.

**When to use which**: Financial costs are appropriate for budget impact analysis and accountability reporting. Economic costs are required for cost-effectiveness analysis and decision-making because they reflect the true resource requirements for replication or scale-up.

### Capital vs. Recurrent Costs

**Recurrent costs**: Consumed within one year and must be repurchased (salaries, supplies, transport, utilities). Include at full annual value.

**Capital costs**: Assets with a useful life beyond one year (buildings, vehicles, equipment, software, initial training). Do not include at full purchase price — annualise using straight-line depreciation over the asset's useful life, or use an equivalent annual cost approach with discounting.

**Annualisation**: Annual capital cost = Purchase price / Useful life (straight-line), or Annual capital cost = Purchase price × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n − 1] (equivalent annual cost with discount rate r over n years).

Typical useful life assumptions: Buildings 30 years, vehicles 5-10 years, computers and electronics 3-5 years, furniture 10 years, medical equipment 5-10 years.

### Shared Costs and Overhead

Health interventions rarely operate in isolation — they share staff, facilities, equipment, and management with other programmes. Allocating shared costs is one of the most challenging aspects of health costing.

**Allocation methods**:
- **Time allocation**: Allocate staff costs based on the proportion of time spent on the intervention (time-motion studies, self-reported time allocation, or observation)
- **Output allocation**: Allocate facility costs based on the proportion of outputs attributable to the intervention (e.g., proportion of outpatient visits related to the programme)
- **Space allocation**: Allocate building costs based on floor area used by the intervention
- **Direct allocation**: Where costs can be directly traced to the intervention, assign them directly

**Overhead**: Administrative, management, and support costs that benefit all programmes. Typically allocated as a percentage of direct costs (common rates: 10-30% depending on context and organisation). Use the organisation's actual overhead rate if available.

### Handling Prices and Inflation

**Currency and price year**: Report all costs in a single currency and price year. State both clearly.

**Inflation adjustment**: When costs span multiple years, adjust to a common price year using a price index (GDP deflator or Consumer Price Index). Use local price indices for local costs and international indices for internationally procured goods.

**Currency conversion**: When comparing costs across countries, use purchasing power parity (PPP) exchange rates rather than market exchange rates. PPP rates account for differences in price levels across countries. Report the exchange rate or PPP conversion factor used.

**Discounting**: Future costs (and outcomes) are discounted to present value to reflect time preference — a dollar today is worth more than a dollar next year. Standard discount rate: 3% per year (WHO-CHOICE, most LMIC guidelines). Some guidelines use 5% (World Bank) or 3.5% (NICE/UK). Always conduct sensitivity analysis on the discount rate.

### Data Sources for Costing

| Data Need | Primary Sources | Secondary Sources |
|---|---|---|
| Programme expenditure | Financial reports, accounting records, invoices | Budget documents, donor financial reports |
| Staff costs | Payroll records, salary scales, time allocation studies | Government salary scales, WHO-CHOICE unit cost database |
| Supply costs | Procurement records, pharmacy records | International Drug Price Indicator Guide (MSH), UNICEF Supply Catalogue |
| Infrastructure | Rental agreements, construction costs, asset registers | WHO-CHOICE facility cost estimates |
| Patient costs | Patient exit interviews, household surveys | Transport cost surveys, published estimates |

### Scale-Up Cost Estimation

One of the most common errors in health economic evaluation is using pilot programme costs to project costs at scale. Pilot costs are systematically different from at-scale costs for several reasons:

**Costs that decrease at scale**:
- Fixed costs (management, coordination, systems development) are spread across more beneficiaries
- Procurement economies (bulk purchasing of commodities and supplies)
- Learning effects (efficiency improves as implementers gain experience)

**Costs that increase at scale**:
- Reaching harder-to-reach populations (the last 20% of coverage is disproportionately expensive — geographic remoteness, marginality, resistance)
- Quality assurance and supervision across a larger number of sites
- Coordination complexity in multi-district or national implementation
- Political and institutional costs of sustaining reform

**Costs that change in character**:
- Pilot programmes often use project-funded staff and parallel systems. At scale, the intervention must be delivered through existing government systems, which have different cost structures and efficiency levels.
- Technical assistance is typically front-loaded in pilots and diminishes at scale — but may be replaced by government management costs.

**Guidance for evaluators**: When projecting costs from an evaluation to inform scale-up decisions, do not simply multiply pilot unit costs by a larger denominator. Adjust for economies of scale on fixed costs, increased marginal costs of reaching underserved populations, the shift from project to government implementation modalities, and the time profile of costs (heavy upfront investment in systems, training, and rollout diminishing to recurrent costs over time). Report these adjustments transparently and conduct sensitivity analysis on the scale-up cost assumptions.

## 4. Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

### Trial-Based vs. Model-Based CEA

**Trial-based (evaluation-based) CEA**: Collects cost and outcome data alongside an impact evaluation (RCT, quasi-experimental study). Costs are measured through resource use data collection during the trial. Outcomes are observed directly. Time horizon is limited to the evaluation follow-up period.

When sufficient:
- The evaluation follow-up period captures the full duration of costs and outcomes
- The outcomes measured in the evaluation are the final outcomes of interest (not intermediate proxies)
- The evaluation population is representative of the population for which the intervention is being considered
- No extrapolation beyond the observed data is needed

**Model-based CEA**: Uses a mathematical model to combine data from multiple sources (evaluation data, epidemiological data, cost data, literature) to estimate costs and outcomes over the relevant time horizon.

When needed:
- The evaluation time horizon is shorter than the relevant outcome horizon (common for chronic conditions, prevention interventions, and system-level reforms)
- The evaluation measured intermediate outcomes that need to be translated to final outcomes
- The evaluation was conducted in a different setting from the decision context (transferability requires modelling)
- Multiple intervention options need to be compared, not all of which were included in the evaluation

**In practice**: Most programme evaluations will use a trial-based approach for the core analysis and may supplement with simple modelling to extrapolate outcomes or explore scenarios. Full model-based CEA is typically conducted by health economists as a separate exercise.

### Structure

A cost-effectiveness analysis compares the incremental costs and incremental outcomes of the intervention relative to the comparator.

**Incremental Cost-Effectiveness Ratio (ICER)**:
ICER = (Cost_intervention − Cost_comparator) / (Outcome_intervention − Outcome_comparator)

The ICER represents the additional cost per additional unit of outcome achieved by the intervention compared to the comparator. It answers: "How much extra does it cost to achieve one additional unit of outcome?"

### Outcome Measures

The outcome measure should be the most meaningful health outcome that can be credibly attributed to the intervention. Common outcome measures in health CEA:

- **DALYs averted**: The standard generic outcome measure in global health CEA. Allows comparison across interventions addressing different health conditions. See Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 2 for DALY calculation.
- **Deaths averted / lives saved**: Intuitive but does not account for age at death or quality of life.
- **Life years saved (LYS)**: Accounts for age at death but not quality of life. Calculated using life tables.
- **Cases averted**: Disease-specific (malaria cases averted, HIV infections averted, TB cases averted). Useful for within-disease comparison but cannot compare across diseases.
- **Disease-specific outcomes**: Successful treatment outcomes, disability-free survival, cure rates. Programme-relevant but not comparable across disease areas.

**Choosing the outcome measure**: Use DALYs averted when the evaluation needs to compare with other health interventions or with published cost-effectiveness thresholds. Use disease-specific outcomes (cases averted, deaths averted) when the comparison is within a single disease area and the audience is programme managers rather than priority-setting bodies.

### The Cost-Effectiveness Plane

Plot incremental costs (y-axis) against incremental outcomes (x-axis) to visualise the cost-effectiveness result:

- **Quadrant I (northeast)**: More effective and more costly. The intervention works but costs more. Whether it is "cost-effective" depends on the willingness-to-pay threshold.
- **Quadrant II (northwest)**: Less effective and more costly. The intervention is dominated — it is worse and more expensive. Reject.
- **Quadrant III (southwest)**: Less effective and less costly. Savings but worse outcomes. Rarely desirable.
- **Quadrant IV (southeast)**: More effective and less costly. The intervention dominates — it is better and cheaper. Adopt.

Most interventions fall in Quadrant I (they work but cost money). The decision then depends on whether the ICER falls below a threshold that society is willing to pay.

### Cost-Effectiveness Thresholds

**WHO-CHOICE thresholds** (historical): An intervention was classified as "very cost-effective" if the ICER was less than 1× GDP per capita per DALY averted, and "cost-effective" if less than 3× GDP per capita. These thresholds have been widely used but are increasingly criticised for being too generous — most health systems cannot afford to fund all interventions meeting the 3× threshold.

**Country-specific thresholds**: Increasingly, researchers are estimating empirical thresholds based on what health systems actually spend to gain a unit of health (opportunity cost approach). These thresholds are typically much lower than the WHO 1-3× GDP per capita range — often 10-50% of GDP per capita.

**Recommendation for evaluators**: Do not rely on a single threshold. Present the ICER and discuss it in context: compare with ICERs of other interventions the health system is currently funding; compare with country-specific estimates if available; present a cost-effectiveness acceptability curve showing the probability of cost-effectiveness at different willingness-to-pay thresholds. Avoid stating that an intervention "is cost-effective" without specifying the threshold and perspective.

### Sensitivity Analysis

Cost-effectiveness results depend on assumptions about costs, outcomes, discount rates, and model parameters. Sensitivity analysis tests how results change when assumptions vary.

**One-way sensitivity analysis**: Vary one parameter at a time across its plausible range while holding all others constant. Identifies which parameters most influence the results. Present as a tornado diagram (parameters ranked by their influence on the ICER).

**Multi-way / scenario analysis**: Vary multiple parameters simultaneously to test specific scenarios (best case, worst case, different implementation scenarios).

**Probabilistic sensitivity analysis (PSA)**: Assign probability distributions to all uncertain parameters and run the model many times (Monte Carlo simulation), producing a distribution of ICERs rather than a single point estimate. Present as a scatterplot on the cost-effectiveness plane and as a cost-effectiveness acceptability curve (CEAC).

For Foundation users: Present one-way sensitivity analysis results showing which assumptions matter most. Explain that "the answer changes depending on these assumptions — here is the range."

For Practitioner users: Conduct one-way and scenario analyses. Commission PSA for major evaluations.

For Advanced users: Discuss distributional assumptions for PSA parameters, value of information analysis (EVPI, EVPPI), and structural uncertainty (model structure itself is uncertain).

## 5. Cost-Utility Analysis

### When to Use CUA over CEA

Cost-utility analysis measures outcomes in QALYs (or DALYs) rather than natural units. Use CUA when:
- The intervention affects both length and quality of life
- Comparison across interventions for different conditions is needed
- The decision-maker requires a generic outcome measure
- The analysis will inform HTA or priority-setting processes

### Measuring Utility / Health State Valuations

**Utility** is a preference-based valuation of health states on a scale from 0 (death) to 1 (perfect health). States worse than death can have negative values.

**Methods for deriving utility weights**:

**Generic preference-based instruments** (most common in evaluation):
- **EQ-5D**: Five dimensions, 3 or 5 severity levels. Respondents self-report their health state; utility weights are derived from population-based valuation studies (tariffs). Country-specific tariffs exist for many countries. See Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 3.
- **SF-6D**: Derived from SF-36 data using a preference-based scoring algorithm. Six dimensions, each with 4-6 levels.
- **HUI (Health Utilities Index)**: Available in HUI2 and HUI3 versions. Eight dimensions. Common in Canada and some HTA contexts.

**Direct elicitation methods** (used to generate tariffs, rarely by evaluators):
- **Time trade-off (TTO)**: How much life expectancy would you trade for perfect health?
- **Standard gamble (SG)**: What risk of death would you accept for a chance of perfect health?
- **Visual analogue scale (VAS)**: Rate health states on a 0-100 scale. Simpler but less theoretically grounded.

**Disability weights** (for DALYs): Derived through population surveys using paired comparisons or VAS. Published in the Global Burden of Disease study. Not elicited from patients but from general population samples.

**For evaluation purposes**: Most evaluators will use EQ-5D administered to programme participants and apply published tariffs. If no country-specific tariff exists, use a neighbouring country's tariff or the UK tariff (widely used as a default). Report the tariff used and discuss the implications of applying tariffs from a different population.

### Calculating QALYs

QALYs = Σ (Utility weight for each health state × Duration in that health state)

For an intervention that moves a patient from a health state with utility 0.5 to one with utility 0.8 for 10 years: QALY gain = (0.8 − 0.5) × 10 = 3 QALYs.

When calculating from individual patient data, sum each patient's utility × time, accounting for changes in health state over the follow-up period. Area-under-the-curve methods can handle utility measured at multiple time points.

## 6. Cost-Benefit Analysis

### When CBA is Appropriate

Cost-benefit analysis measures all outcomes in monetary terms. This enables direct comparison of total costs and total benefits, and allows comparison across sectors (health vs. education vs. infrastructure). CBA is less common in health evaluation than CEA/CUA because monetising health outcomes is methodologically and ethically contested.

**Use CBA when**:
- Cross-sectoral comparison is required (is this health programme a better investment than a roads programme?)
- The intervention has substantial non-health benefits (productivity gains, economic output) that should be captured
- The decision-maker requires a benefit-cost ratio or net present value

### Monetising Health Outcomes

**Human capital approach**: Values health outcomes based on productivity (earnings lost due to illness or premature death). Advantages: uses observable data (wages). Limitations: undervalues health of non-earners (children, elderly, unpaid caregivers), reproduces income inequality, does not capture intrinsic value of health.

**Willingness-to-pay (WTP)**: Values health outcomes based on what people would pay to avoid illness or death. Methods:
- **Contingent valuation**: Direct survey asking respondents their WTP for a health improvement. Subject to hypothetical bias, strategic behaviour, and scope insensitivity.
- **Discrete choice experiments (DCE)**: Respondents choose between scenarios with different attributes (health outcomes, costs, waiting times). Derives WTP from revealed preferences. More robust than contingent valuation for complex choices.
- **Value of a Statistical Life (VSL)**: Derived from labour market studies (wage premiums for risky jobs) or stated preference studies. Used to monetise mortality reduction. VSL estimates vary enormously across studies and countries.

**For evaluation purposes**: If CBA is required, state the monetisation method and its assumptions transparently. Report sensitivity analysis using alternative valuations. Be explicit that monetising health outcomes involves ethical choices (whose values? how to value a child's life vs. an adult's?) that are not purely technical.

### Presenting CBA Results

**Net Present Value (NPV)**: Total discounted benefits − Total discounted costs. NPV > 0 means benefits exceed costs.

**Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR)**: Total discounted benefits / Total discounted costs. BCR > 1 means benefits exceed costs. Be cautious: the BCR can be manipulated by reclassifying cost offsets (savings) as benefits rather than negative costs.

**Internal Rate of Return (IRR)**: The discount rate at which NPV = 0. Higher IRR indicates a better investment. Less intuitive than NPV for non-economists.

## 7. Budget Impact Analysis

### What BIA Does

Budget impact analysis estimates the financial consequences of adopting a new intervention for a specific payer or budget holder over a defined time horizon (typically 1-5 years). It answers: "Can we afford this?" — a different question from "Is this cost-effective?"

An intervention can be cost-effective (good value for money) but unaffordable (the total budget required exceeds available resources). BIA addresses affordability.

### When to Use

- When presenting a cost-effective intervention to a decision-maker who needs to know the budget implications of adoption
- When evaluating the financial sustainability of scaling up a programme
- When comparing the budget implications of different implementation scenarios (phased vs. immediate rollout, different coverage targets)

### Key Components

**Target population**: How many people are eligible for the intervention? What is the expected uptake/coverage over the time horizon?

**Current practice costs**: What is currently being spent on the condition or target population?

**New intervention costs**: What will the intervention cost per person and in total at projected coverage?

**Budget impact**: Net budget impact = New intervention costs − Current practice costs (including any cost offsets from reduced downstream utilisation, e.g., fewer hospitalisations).

**Time horizon**: Typically 1-5 years. Shorter than CEA (which may use a lifetime horizon). Undiscounted (BIA reports actual annual cash flows, not discounted present values).

### Presentation

Present BIA results as annual cash flows over the time horizon, showing:
- Total costs of current practice (baseline)
- Total costs of the new intervention (including implementation costs)
- Net budget impact per year
- Cumulative budget impact
- Sensitivity analysis on key parameters (uptake rate, unit costs, eligible population size)

For Foundation users: Explain BIA as "figuring out what the intervention will actually cost the health system each year, and whether the budget can handle it."

For Practitioner users: Construct a BIA using programme costing data and uptake projections. Present alongside the CEA to give decision-makers both value-for-money and affordability evidence.

For Advanced users: Discuss dynamic BIA (where adoption of the intervention changes the epidemiology of the condition over time), multi-payer BIA, and the interaction between BIA and fiscal space analysis.

## 8. Equity-Weighted Economic Evaluation

### Why Standard CEA Misses Equity

Standard cost-effectiveness analysis is equity-neutral — a DALY averted in the richest quintile is valued the same as a DALY averted in the poorest quintile. An intervention that is cost-effective on average may achieve its outcomes primarily among the better-off, widening health inequities.

### Approaches to Incorporating Equity

**Extended Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (ECEA)**: Disaggregates cost-effectiveness results by wealth quintile, geography, or other equity dimensions. Assesses the distribution of health gains, financial risk protection gains, and costs across population groups. Does not aggregate into a single number but presents distributional information alongside average CEA results. Developed by Verguet and colleagues; increasingly used in LMIC priority-setting.

**Distributional Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (DCEA)**: Goes further than ECEA by applying explicit equity weights — additional weight to health gains accruing to the worse-off. Aggregates into a single equity-weighted effectiveness measure. Requires explicit choices about how much additional weight to give to disadvantaged groups (inequality aversion parameters). Developed by Asaria, Griffin, and Cookson.

**Benefit Incidence Analysis (BIA)**: Assesses who benefits from health spending. Combines utilisation data by wealth quintile with unit costs to estimate the distribution of public health spending. Shows whether spending is progressive (pro-poor) or regressive (pro-rich). Cross-reference to HSS sub-skill, Section 4 (Equity).

**For evaluation purposes**: At minimum, disaggregate cost-effectiveness results by relevant equity dimensions (wealth, geography, sex). Present the distribution of costs and outcomes across groups even if a formal ECEA or DCEA is not conducted. This allows decision-makers to assess equity implications alongside average cost-effectiveness.

## 9. Modelling for Economic Evaluation

### When Modelling is Needed

Not all economic evaluations require modelling. Trial-based or evaluation-based economic analyses use observed cost and outcome data directly. Modelling is needed when:

- The evaluation time horizon is shorter than the relevant time horizon for outcomes (e.g., a 2-year evaluation of an intervention whose benefits accrue over a lifetime)
- Outcomes need to be extrapolated beyond the observed data
- The evaluation measured intermediate outcomes (cases detected, treatment initiated) but the economic evaluation requires final outcomes (DALYs, deaths averted)
- Comparison with interventions evaluated in different studies is needed
- Policy scenarios need to be explored (different coverage levels, different target populations)

### Common Model Types

**Decision tree**: Maps a sequence of choices and chance events, with probabilities and outcomes at each branch. Appropriate for acute conditions with a short time horizon and a limited number of health states.

**Markov model**: Divides the disease/treatment process into a finite number of health states. Individuals transition between states at defined probabilities over discrete time cycles. Appropriate for chronic conditions, diseases with recurring events, and longer time horizons. The most commonly used model type in health economic evaluation.

Key parameters: health states, transition probabilities (from clinical data, cohort studies, or literature), cycle length (e.g., 1 month, 1 year), time horizon, costs and utilities assigned to each state.

**Microsimulation**: Simulates individual patient trajectories rather than cohort averages. Tracks individual histories and allows for patient heterogeneity. More flexible than Markov models but more computationally intensive and data-hungry.

**Transmission dynamic models**: For infectious diseases, capture the indirect effects of interventions (herd immunity, reduced transmission). Standard CEA models (decision trees, Markov) assume individual independence and miss these effects, potentially underestimating the cost-effectiveness of interventions like vaccination.

**The Lives Saved Tool (LiST)**: A modelling tool widely used in LMIC settings to estimate the impact of scaling up maternal, newborn, and child health interventions on mortality. Uses published effectiveness estimates and coverage data to project lives saved. Not a cost-effectiveness model per se, but frequently used to estimate outcomes for CEA.

### Model Validation

All models should undergo:
- **Internal validation**: Does the model reproduce its input data correctly? Check by running the model with known inputs and verifying outputs.
- **External validation**: Does the model reproduce observed data not used to build it? Compare model predictions with epidemiological data, trial results, or routine data from different settings.
- **Cross-validation**: Do results align with other published models of the same condition? Explain discrepancies.
- **Face validity**: Do the model structure and results make clinical and epidemiological sense to experts?

### For Evaluators Who Are Not Modellers

Most programme evaluators will not build economic models themselves. When commissioning or interpreting modelled economic evaluations:

- Verify that the model structure reflects the disease and intervention logic (does the model capture the relevant health states and transitions?)
- Check that input parameters come from credible sources and are documented
- Assess whether sensitivity analysis covers the parameters most likely to affect results
- Evaluate whether the model time horizon is appropriate for the condition
- Check whether the model has been validated
- Be sceptical of models that produce very precise-looking results from very uncertain inputs

## 10. Reporting Standards

### CHEERS 2022

The Consolidated Health Economic Evaluation Reporting Standards (CHEERS 2022) is the standard reporting guideline for health economic evaluations. The checklist covers 28 items across seven sections. Key reporting requirements:

**Title and abstract**: Identify the study as an economic evaluation. State the interventions compared, setting, study perspective, time horizon, and main results including uncertainty.

**Methods**:
- Target population and setting
- Comparator(s) and justification
- Study perspective
- Time horizon and justification
- Discount rate and justification
- Outcome measure and justification
- Measurement of effectiveness (trial-based, model-based, or literature synthesis)
- Costing methodology: perspective, resource identification, measurement, valuation, currency, price year
- For models: model type, structure, assumptions, validation, and input parameter sources

**Results**:
- Resource use and cost results (disaggregated by cost category)
- Outcome results
- Incremental analysis (ICER)
- Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis results
- Distributional/equity results if applicable

**Discussion**: Compare with previous evidence, discuss limitations (including generalisability), and address implications for decision-making.

### Reporting Costs

Always report:
- Currency and price year
- Perspective
- Discount rate
- Total costs and disaggregation by cost category (personnel, supplies, capital, overhead)
- Unit costs for key cost drivers
- How shared costs were allocated
- Whether financial or economic costs (or both) are reported
- Data sources for all cost inputs

### Reporting ICERs

- Report the ICER with its numerator and denominator explicitly
- Present uncertainty: confidence intervals (from bootstrapping or PSA), tornado diagram, CEAC
- Report results on the cost-effectiveness plane
- Do not present an ICER when the intervention dominates (more effective and less costly) or is dominated (less effective and more costly) — state dominance directly
- Discuss the result in relation to relevant thresholds, comparator interventions, and the decision context

## 11. Interpreting and Using Economic Evidence

### Common Pitfalls in Interpreting Cost-Effectiveness

**Confusing cost-effectiveness with affordability**: An intervention can be highly cost-effective (low cost per DALY averted) but unaffordable at scale. Always present BIA alongside CEA.

**Ignoring the comparator**: An ICER is only meaningful relative to a specific comparator. "Cost per DALY averted" without specifying compared to what is incomplete.

**Using outdated or inappropriate thresholds**: The WHO 1-3× GDP per capita thresholds are increasingly viewed as too generous. Country-specific or opportunity-cost-based thresholds are more appropriate.

**Generalising without adjustment**: Cost-effectiveness results from one country cannot be directly applied to another without adjusting for differences in costs, baseline disease burden, health system context, and intervention effectiveness. Transferability analysis should assess which parameters are context-dependent.

**Ignoring equity**: A cost-effective intervention that benefits primarily the wealthy may not be the right investment for a health system with equity objectives.

**Over-interpreting precise ICERs**: A point estimate ICER of $47 per DALY averted implies false precision. Always present uncertainty ranges. If the 95% confidence interval spans both "very cost-effective" and "not cost-effective" zones, the evidence is inconclusive.

### Value for Money Frameworks

Donors increasingly use VfM frameworks rather than pure CEA. The most common is the FCDO 4E framework:

- **Economy**: Are inputs purchased at the lowest price for acceptable quality? (Getting the best price)
- **Efficiency**: Are inputs converted to outputs at optimal rates? (Spending well)
- **Effectiveness**: Are outputs producing intended outcomes? (Spending wisely)
- **Equity**: Are costs and benefits distributed fairly? (Spending fairly)

VfM assessments typically use a mix of quantitative indicators (unit costs, cost-efficiency ratios, cost-effectiveness ratios) and qualitative judgement (narrative assessment of economy and equity). They are less methodologically standardised than formal CEA but more useful for programme management decisions.

For Foundation users: Explain cost-effectiveness as "getting the most health improvement for each dollar spent." Focus on unit costs (cost per person reached, cost per service delivered) as the most accessible VfM indicator.

For Practitioner users: Conduct basic CEA using programme costing data and evaluation outcome data. Use the 4E framework for VfM assessments. Present results with appropriate uncertainty.

For Advanced users: Commission or conduct full CEA/CUA with PSA, modelling, and equity analysis. Engage with transferability of results, structural uncertainty, and value of information analysis.

### Communicating Economic Evidence to Decision-Makers

Decision-makers rarely want to see ICERs and tornado diagrams. Translate economic evidence into decision-relevant language:

- "The programme costs $X per person reached and achieves Y outcomes. Compared with the alternative, it costs $Z more per additional outcome achieved."
- "At current funding levels, expanding the programme to full coverage would require an additional $X per year over Y years."
- "The programme is good value for money compared with other health investments in this country, but scaling up will require a Z% budget increase that is not currently funded."
- Present equity implications: "The poorest quintile receives X% of the health gains but bears Y% of the costs."

Always pair economic evidence with implementation feasibility, political context, and programme quality evidence. Cost-effectiveness alone does not make the case for or against a programme.
