## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill provides evaluators with the donor-specific indicator frameworks, reporting structures, evaluation expectations, and terminology they need to produce evaluation outputs that meet donor requirements. It is a practical reference — less methodological, more operational.

### When to Use

Use this sub-skill whenever an evaluation is commissioned by or reports to a specific health donor. Donors have specific indicator frameworks, reporting formats, evaluation terminology, and quality expectations. Producing an evaluation that does not speak the donor's language reduces its utility and uptake.

### How This Sub-Skill Relates to Others

The other PRAXIS Health sub-skills cover evaluation methodology. This sub-skill covers how to present evaluation findings and recommendations within donor-specific frameworks. It complements, not replaces, the methodological sub-skills.

## 2. The Global Fund

### Grant Architecture

Global Fund grants are structured around a **modular framework**: modules (e.g., RSSH, prevention, case management, treatment), interventions within modules, and indicators for each intervention. Grants operate on a 3-year implementation period within a funding cycle (currently the Grant Cycle 7 framework).

### Key Indicator Frameworks

**Programme indicators**: Disease-specific indicators aligned with national strategic plans and global targets. Selected from the Global Fund's indicator guidance documents for HIV, TB, malaria, and RSSH.

**RSSH indicators**: Cover health management information systems, human resources for health, procurement and supply chain management, community systems strengthening, health financing, and national health strategies.

**Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**: Organisational-level indicators measuring Global Fund portfolio performance. Include programme coverage indicators, financial performance, and management indicators.

### Evaluation Expectations

**Prospective Country Evaluations (PCEs)**: Independent, multi-year evaluations conducted by IHME, PATH, and partners in selected countries. Assess the contribution of Global Fund investments to health outcomes using mixed methods.

**Technical evaluation reference group (TERG) evaluations**: Thematic evaluations commissioned by the Global Fund on specific topics (e.g., sustainability, equity, community systems strengthening).

**Grant-level evaluation**: Less standardised. Evaluations of individual grants may be commissioned by Country Coordinating Mechanisms (CCMs), Principal Recipients, or the Global Fund Secretariat. Expectations vary but typically include assessment of: performance against grant targets, contribution to National Strategic Plan outcomes, RSSH strengthening, equity and gender, sustainability, and value for money.

**Performance framework assessment**: Evaluate grant performance against the Performance Framework (PF) targets agreed at grant signing. The PF specifies coverage indicators, impact indicators, and output indicators with annual or semi-annual targets. Assess both target achievement and data quality.

### Reporting Terminology

- **PUDR**: Progress Update and Disbursement Request — the primary grant reporting instrument
- **CCM**: Country Coordinating Mechanism — the national governance body
- **PR**: Principal Recipient — the grant implementing entity
- **SR**: Sub-Recipient
- **LFA**: Local Fund Agent — the in-country verification agent
- **OPN**: Operational Policy Note — operational guidance on specific grant management topics

## 3. PEPFAR

### Programme Framework

PEPFAR organises its programming through the **Country Operational Plan (COP)**, which specifies targets, strategies, and budgets for each country annually. COP targets are disaggregated by implementing mechanism (IM), site, and population.

### MER Indicators

The Monitoring, Evaluation, and Reporting (MER) indicator system is the backbone of PEPFAR data. MER indicators cover the full HIV cascade and are reported quarterly. Key MER indicators:

**Prevention**: HTS_TST (number tested for HIV), HTS_TST_POS (number testing positive), PrEP_NEW (newly enrolled on PrEP), VMMC_CIRC (voluntary medical male circumcisions performed), PP_PREV (prevention programmes for priority populations), KP_PREV (key population prevention).

**Treatment**: TX_NEW (newly initiated on ART), TX_CURR (currently on ART), TX_PVLS (viral load suppression among those tested).

**PMTCT**: PMTCT_STAT (ANC clients with known HIV status), PMTCT_ART (HIV+ pregnant women on ART).

**OVC**: OVC_SERV (orphans and vulnerable children served), OVC_HIVSTAT (OVC with known HIV status).

**TB**: TB_STAT (TB patients with known HIV status), TB_ART (TB/HIV co-infected patients on ART), TB_PREV (PLHIV on TPT).

### Evaluation Expectations

PEPFAR evaluations assess contribution to the 95-95-95 targets and epidemic control. Key evaluation dimensions:

- **Programme effectiveness**: Are MER targets being achieved? Is the cascade progressing?
- **Quality**: Is service quality adequate? (Clinical quality, retention, viral suppression)
- **Sustainability and transition**: Is the programme transitioning to partner government ownership? Use the Sustainability Index and Dashboard (SID) as a framework.
- **Equity**: Are key and priority populations being reached?
- **Cost efficiency**: What is the cost per patient on treatment? Cost per new infection averted?

PEPFAR requires evaluations to use PEPFAR reporting categories and MER indicators as the primary performance framework. Evaluations that use alternative indicator frameworks without cross-walking to MER indicators reduce their utility for PEPFAR decision-making.

## 4. Gavi

### Support Framework

Gavi provides support through several windows: New Vaccine Support (NVS), Health System Strengthening (HSS), Cold Chain Equipment Optimisation Platform (CCEOP), and Vaccine Investment Strategy (VIS) for new vaccines.

### Key Indicators

Gavi tracks performance through:

**Coverage indicators**: National and subnational immunisation coverage for Gavi-supported vaccines (pentavalent, PCV, rotavirus, HPV, measles, yellow fever, etc.). Coverage data come from WHO/UNICEF Estimates of National Immunisation Coverage (WUENIC) — the official global immunisation coverage estimates.

**Equity indicators**: Coverage by wealth quintile, urban/rural, maternal education, subnational. Zero-dose children (never received DPT1) is a priority equity indicator in Gavi 5.0/5.1 strategy.

**System indicators**: Drop-out rates, cold chain functionality, surveillance performance, data quality (administrative coverage vs. survey coverage).

### Evaluation Expectations

**Full Country Evaluations (FCEs)**: Independent evaluations of Gavi support in selected countries. Assess the contribution of Gavi support to immunisation outcomes, equity, and health system strengthening. Mixed-methods, typically multi-year.

**Post-Introduction Evaluations (PIEs)**: Standardised evaluations 6-12 months after new vaccine introduction. Assess programmatic readiness, cold chain, AEFI surveillance, coverage, acceptance, and integration with routine immunisation. WHO provides the PIE tool.

**Joint Appraisals**: Annual country-level reviews of Gavi-supported programmes. Not a full evaluation but a structured performance review involving national stakeholders and Gavi.

**Gavi 5.0/5.1 Strategic Framework**: Gavi's current strategy prioritises reaching zero-dose and under-immunised children, building resilient health systems, improving sustainability, and ensuring healthy markets for vaccines. Evaluations should frame findings against these strategic priorities.

## 5. World Bank Health Sector Projects

### Project Architecture

World Bank health sector projects are designed around a **Project Development Objective (PDO)** — a specific, measurable statement of the outcome the project aims to achieve. Performance is tracked through PDO-level indicators and Intermediate Outcome Indicators.

Financing instruments relevant to health:
- **Investment Project Financing (IPF)**: Traditional project lending with disbursement based on expenditure
- **Programme for Results (PforR)**: Disbursement linked to achievement of specific results (Disbursement-Linked Indicators — DLIs)
- **Development Policy Financing (DPF)**: Budget support linked to policy reform actions

### Results Framework

The World Bank Results Framework specifies:
- PDO statement
- PDO-level indicators (2-5 outcome indicators, measured at project completion)
- Intermediate outcome indicators (10-15 indicators tracking progress during implementation)
- For PforR: Disbursement-Linked Indicators with specific targets and verification protocols

### Evaluation Expectations

**Implementation Completion and Results Report (ICR)**: Prepared by the project team at completion. Assesses achievement of the PDO, implementation performance, and lessons learned. Rates performance on a 6-point scale (Highly Satisfactory to Highly Unsatisfactory).

**Independent Evaluation Group (IEG) review**: IEG validates ICR ratings and may conduct independent project performance assessments for selected projects. Ratings cover outcome, Bank performance, and quality of M&E.

**Impact evaluations**: The Development Impact Evaluation (DIME) unit coordinates impact evaluations of Bank-financed programmes. These use rigorous experimental or quasi-experimental designs and are conducted separately from project supervision.

### Reporting Terminology

- **PDO**: Project Development Objective
- **DLI**: Disbursement-Linked Indicator (for PforR)
- **ISR**: Implementation Status and Results Report (semi-annual)
- **ICR**: Implementation Completion and Results Report
- **IEG**: Independent Evaluation Group
- **DIME**: Development Impact Evaluation
- **PAD**: Project Appraisal Document

## 6. WHO Monitoring Frameworks

### UHC Monitoring Framework

WHO/World Bank jointly monitor progress toward Universal Health Coverage through:

**UHC Service Coverage Index**: Composite index of 14 tracer indicators across four categories: reproductive/maternal/newborn/child health; infectious diseases; non-communicable diseases; service capacity and access. Ranges from 0-100.

**Financial protection indicators**: Proportion of population with catastrophic health expenditure (>10% of household consumption) and impoverishing health expenditure.

These indicators provide the benchmark framework for evaluating UHC-oriented health sector programmes.

### SDG Health Indicators

SDG 3 (Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being) includes 13 targets with associated indicators. Health programme evaluations can align their indicators with relevant SDG 3 indicators for global comparability:

- SDG 3.1: Maternal mortality ratio
- SDG 3.2: Under-5 and neonatal mortality rates
- SDG 3.3: HIV incidence, TB incidence, malaria incidence, hepatitis B incidence, NTD interventions
- SDG 3.7: Family planning demand satisfied, adolescent birth rate
- SDG 3.8: UHC service coverage index, catastrophic health expenditure
- SDG 3.b: Immunisation coverage (DTP3)

### Disease-Specific Global Strategies

Each major disease programme has a WHO global strategy with targets and indicators:

**End TB Strategy**: Targets 90% reduction in TB deaths and 80% reduction in TB incidence by 2030 (compared to 2015). Monitored through TB incidence, mortality, treatment success rates, catastrophic costs.

**Global Technical Strategy for Malaria 2016-2030**: Targets 90% reduction in malaria case incidence and mortality by 2030 (compared to 2015). Monitored through incidence, mortality, elimination milestones.

**Global Health Sector Strategies on HIV, Hepatitis, and STIs (2022-2030)**: Targets for HIV include 95-95-95 and 90% reduction in new infections. STI targets include 90% reduction in syphilis and gonorrhoea incidence.

Align evaluation indicators with these global strategies when the evaluation audience includes WHO, multilateral organisations, or governments tracking global commitments.

## 7. Bilateral Donors

### USAID

**Results framework**: USAID uses a hierarchical results framework: Goal → Development Objectives (DOs) → Intermediate Results (IRs) → Sub-IRs, with indicators at each level.

**Evaluation types**: USAID distinguishes between:
- **Performance evaluations**: Assess whether activities are achieving expected results. Do not require counterfactual. Most common.
- **Impact evaluations**: Use experimental or quasi-experimental designs to attribute outcomes to the intervention.

**Evaluation policy**: USAID's Evaluation Policy (updated 2020) requires evaluations to be independent, transparent, and technically sound. All evaluations must be made publicly available through the Development Experience Clearinghouse (DEC).

**Reporting expectations**: Focus on learning and adaptive management. Evaluations should answer learning questions agreed in advance with USAID. Findings should be actionable for programme adaptation. Use USAID's Collaboration, Learning, and Adapting (CLA) framework.

**Health-specific**: USAID health evaluations typically report against PEPFAR MER indicators (for HIV), PMI indicators (for malaria), and Feed the Future indicators (for nutrition). Health system strengthening evaluations align with the Health Systems Sustainability Activity framework.

### FCDO (UK)

**Value for Money (VfM)**: FCDO evaluation policy centres on VfM assessed through the 4E framework:
- **Economy**: Minimising cost of inputs while maintaining quality
- **Efficiency**: Maximising outputs per unit of input
- **Effectiveness**: Achieving intended outcomes
- **Equity**: Fair distribution of costs and benefits

**Evaluation expectations**: FCDO commissions Annual Reviews, Project Completion Reviews, and independent evaluations. The evaluation quality assurance framework (QAF) rates evaluations against quality criteria. FCDO increasingly expects contribution analysis or theory-based evaluation approaches.

**Log frame**: FCDO uses a standard logframe (impact, outcome, outputs, inputs) with milestone-based tracking. Annual Reviews assess progress against logframe targets.

### EU

**Results Framework**: EU development cooperation uses intervention logic: Overall Objectives → Specific Objectives → Expected Results → Activities. ROM (Results-Oriented Monitoring) provides external monitoring of EU-funded programmes.

**Evaluation criteria**: EU evaluations use the OECD-DAC criteria (relevance, coherence, effectiveness, efficiency, impact, sustainability) plus EU added value. The EU evaluation methodology guide provides standardised evaluation matrices.

### GIZ

**Results-based monitoring**: GIZ uses a results model with module objectives, indicators, and target-actual comparison. Emphasises capacity development at individual, organisational, and systemic levels.

**Evaluation focus**: GIZ evaluations emphasise sustainability, scalability, and contribution to structural transformation. Often include institutional capacity assessments.

## 8. Cross-Donor Considerations

### Multi-Donor Programmes

Many health programmes receive funding from multiple donors, each with their own reporting requirements. Evaluations of multi-donor programmes must:

- **Harmonise indicator frameworks**: Map programme indicators to each donor's framework. Present results using a common evaluation matrix while providing donor-specific indicator tables in annexes.
- **Address attribution**: Each donor wants to know the contribution of their funding. If the programme receives GF, PEPFAR, and bilateral funding simultaneously, the evaluation must either assess the programme as a whole (contribution analysis of the combined investment) or isolate donor-specific contributions where possible and meaningful.
- **Manage reporting formats**: Produce a single comprehensive evaluation report with donor-specific summaries or formatted annexes. Avoid producing entirely separate reports for each donor — this fragments the evidence and wastes resources.

### Aligning Evaluation with National Systems

The best health evaluations align with and strengthen national M&E systems rather than creating parallel evaluation processes. This means:

- Using national health indicators alongside donor-specific indicators
- Drawing on national data systems (HMIS, DHIS2, DHS/MICS) as primary data sources
- Including Ministry of Health as evaluation stakeholders and users
- Presenting findings in formats that inform national health planning, not just donor reporting
- Building evaluation capacity within national institutions as a by-product of the evaluation process

### The Paris Declaration and Evaluation

The Paris Declaration principles (ownership, alignment, harmonisation, results, mutual accountability) apply to evaluation. Evaluations should: be led or co-led by national stakeholders where possible; use national data systems; align with national evaluation plans; and present findings to national audiences, not only donors. These principles are increasingly operationalised through joint evaluation frameworks and country-led evaluation platforms.
