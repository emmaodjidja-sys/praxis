## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill guides evaluation of programmes across the reproductive, maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health continuum, and nutrition programmes at all life stages. These domains share overlapping target populations, service delivery platforms, and measurement challenges.

### What Belongs Here

Evaluation of family planning and contraceptive programmes, maternal health services (ANC, skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric care, postnatal care), newborn care programmes, integrated management of childhood illness (IMCI/iCCM), adolescent sexual and reproductive health programmes, nutrition-specific interventions (growth monitoring, micronutrient supplementation, management of acute malnutrition, IYCF promotion), and nutrition-sensitive programmes (food security, WASH, social protection linkages to nutrition outcomes).

### What Belongs Elsewhere

- Immunisation programmes → Disease Programme sub-skill (Section 6)
- Health system-level service delivery assessment → HSS sub-skill (Section 3.1)
- Mortality measurement methods → Epidemiological Methods sub-skill (Section 5)
- Cost-effectiveness of RMNCAH interventions → Health Economics sub-skill

## 2. The Continuum of Care Framework

### Structure

The RMNCAH continuum of care organises services across two dimensions:

**Life stages**: Pre-pregnancy → Pregnancy → Birth → Postnatal (mother and newborn) → Infancy → Childhood → Adolescence

**Service delivery levels**: Household/community → Primary care → Referral/hospital

The continuum framework is the standard evaluation architecture for RMNCAH programmes because gaps in care at any stage or level undermine outcomes at subsequent stages. A programme that achieves high ANC coverage but low skilled birth attendance has a break in the continuum that limits its impact on maternal and newborn mortality.

### Evaluating the Continuum

**Coverage along the continuum**: Track coverage at each contact point. The standard continuum indicators:
- Demand for family planning satisfied with modern methods
- ANC 4+ visits (or ANC 8+ contacts per 2016 WHO guidelines)
- Skilled birth attendance
- Institutional delivery
- Emergency obstetric care availability and utilisation (met need for EmOC)
- Early postnatal care (within 48 hours for mother and newborn)
- Exclusive breastfeeding (0-5 months)
- Full immunisation
- Care-seeking for childhood illness (fever, diarrhoea, ARI)

**Coverage cascade analysis**: Plot coverage levels sequentially along the continuum. The shape of the cascade reveals where the largest drop-offs occur — and therefore where programme investment is most needed. Present as a cascade diagram or waterfall chart.

**Co-coverage**: The proportion of individuals who receive multiple services along the continuum. A woman who receives ANC but delivers without a skilled attendant has incomplete co-coverage. Measure co-coverage using linked individual-level data from household surveys (DHS/MICS).

### Linking Coverage to Outcomes

Coverage alone does not explain outcomes. The coverage-outcome relationship depends on:
- **Quality of care**: Coverage with poor-quality care has limited health impact (see Section 4 for quality of care measurement)
- **Timing**: Services delivered at the wrong time (late ANC booking, delayed treatment of complications) reduce effectiveness
- **Equity**: Aggregate coverage may mask inequities that concentrate poor outcomes among the most marginalised

The effective coverage concept (see HSS sub-skill, Section 3.1) is particularly relevant for RMNCAH: what matters is not just that women attend ANC, but that they receive evidence-based content during those visits.

## 3. Reproductive Health Programme Evaluation

### Family Planning Programme Evaluation

**Key indicators**:
- **Contraceptive prevalence rate (CPR)**: Proportion of women of reproductive age (or married women, depending on the indicator) currently using any modern contraceptive method. The standard coverage indicator.
- **Modern method mix**: Distribution of users across method types. A skewed method mix (dominated by one method) may indicate limited choice.
- **Unmet need for family planning**: Proportion of women who want to delay or stop childbearing but are not using contraception. Measured using the revised DHS algorithm.
- **Demand satisfied with modern methods**: CPR / (CPR + Unmet need). SDG indicator 3.7.1. More informative than CPR alone because it relates use to demand.
- **Discontinuation rates**: Proportion of users who stop using a method within 12 months. Measured from DHS calendar data. High discontinuation with switching indicates informed choice; high discontinuation without switching indicates programme failure.
- **Adolescent birth rate**: Age-specific fertility rate for women 15-19. An outcome indicator reflecting both access to contraception and broader determinants.

**Method-specific evaluation**: Different contraceptive methods require different evaluation approaches. Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs — implants, IUDs) require assessment of trained provider availability, removal access, and client counselling. Short-acting methods (pills, injectables) require assessment of supply continuity and resupply mechanisms. Community-based distribution requires assessment of CHW competency, supply chain, and referral for complications.

**Rights-based evaluation**: Family planning evaluation must assess voluntarism, informed choice, and absence of coercion. Evaluate whether clients receive comprehensive counselling on method options, side effects, and the right to discontinue. Assess whether provider incentives or programme targets create pressure toward specific methods. Use client exit interviews, mystery clients, and qualitative methods to assess the quality of counselling.

### Sexual and Reproductive Health Services

When evaluating broader SRH programmes (beyond contraception), assess:
- STI prevention, screening, and treatment services
- Gender-based violence (GBV) response services: availability of clinical management of rape, psychosocial support, referral pathways, and integration with legal services
- Safe abortion and post-abortion care where legally available: access, quality, and complications
- Cervical cancer screening and treatment: see Disease Programme sub-skill (NCD section)

## 4. Maternal Health Programme Evaluation

### Quality of Care in Maternal Health

Quality of care is the central evaluation challenge in maternal health because high coverage of substandard care does not reduce mortality. Two complementary frameworks:

**WHO Standards for Improving Quality of Maternal and Newborn Care in Health Facilities**: Eight domains of quality across provision of care (evidence-based practices, actionable information systems, functional referral, competent staff) and experience of care (effective communication, respect and dignity, emotional support).

**Signal functions for Emergency Obstetric Care (EmOC)**:

| Signal Function | BEmONC (Basic) | CEmONC (Comprehensive) |
|---|---|---|
| Parenteral antibiotics | ✓ | ✓ |
| Parenteral oxytocics | ✓ | ✓ |
| Parenteral anticonvulsants (magnesium sulphate) | ✓ | ✓ |
| Manual removal of placenta | ✓ | ✓ |
| Removal of retained products | ✓ | ✓ |
| Assisted vaginal delivery | ✓ | ✓ |
| Neonatal resuscitation with bag and mask | ✓ | ✓ |
| Blood transfusion | | ✓ |
| Caesarean section | | ✓ |

A facility qualifies as BEmONC if it performed all 7 basic signal functions in the past 3 months. CEmONC requires all 9 including blood transfusion and caesarean section.

**EmOC process indicators** (UN standard):
- Availability: At least 5 BEmONC and 1 CEmONC facility per 500,000 population
- Geographical distribution: Facilities reasonably distributed
- Met need for EmOC: Proportion of women with major direct obstetric complications treated in EmOC facilities (target: 100%)
- Caesarean section rate: Population-based C-section rate as a proportion of all births (acceptable range: 5-15%)
- Case fatality rate: Direct obstetric deaths among women treated for obstetric complications in EmOC facilities (target: <1%)

### Maternal Death Surveillance and Response (MDSR)

MDSR is both a programme component and an evaluation data source. The system identifies and reviews maternal deaths to determine cause of death, contributing factors, and avoidable factors, and to recommend actions.

**For evaluation purposes**: MDSR data provide facility-level and subnational information on where, why, and how women die — information that population-based mortality measurement cannot provide. Use MDSR data to complement quantitative evaluation with facility-level causal analysis. Assess the MDSR system itself: notification completeness, review quality, timeliness of recommendations, and evidence of response to recommendations.

### Respectful Maternity Care

Increasing recognition that disrespect and abuse during childbirth (physical abuse, non-consented procedures, lack of privacy, abandonment, detention for non-payment) deter facility-based delivery and violate human rights. Evaluate respectful maternity care using:
- Direct observation of provider-client interactions during labour and delivery
- Postpartum client interviews (exit interviews or community follow-up)
- Validated tools: the Mothers on Respect (MOR) index, WHO person-centred maternity care measures
- Complaints mechanisms and their functionality

## 5. Newborn Health Programme Evaluation

### Key Newborn Health Interventions

**Essential Newborn Care (ENC)**: Immediate drying, skin-to-skin contact, delayed cord clamping, early initiation of breastfeeding, thermal care, hygienic cord care. Evaluate using direct observation of deliveries (the gold standard but resource-intensive) or provider recall/simulation assessments. DHS/MICS capture some ENC practices through maternal self-report but with recall limitations.

**Neonatal resuscitation**: Availability of trained providers and bag-and-mask at the point of delivery. Evaluate training, equipment availability, and provider competency (simulation assessment). Helping Babies Breathe (HBB) programme evaluations use pre-post assessments of knowledge and skills with standardised simulation scenarios.

**Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC)**: For preterm and low birth weight newborns: continuous skin-to-skin contact, exclusive breastfeeding, and early discharge with follow-up. Evaluate KMC initiation, duration (hours per day), facility vs. community-based KMC, and outcomes (weight gain, mortality, breastfeeding rates).

**Small and sick newborn care**: Evaluate availability and quality of inpatient neonatal care units: staffing, equipment (CPAP, phototherapy, incubators), infection prevention, monitoring, family-centred care, and treatment outcomes (survival, complications, follow-up).

### Newborn-Specific Measurement Challenges

**Stillbirth and neonatal mortality distinction**: See Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 5. Misclassification between stillbirth and early neonatal death is common in settings without skilled birth attendance. Perinatal mortality (stillbirths + early neonatal deaths) may be a more robust outcome indicator.

**Birth weight measurement**: Birth weight is a critical indicator (low birth weight <2500g is a major risk factor for neonatal mortality) but is not measured for births outside facilities. DHS captures maternal self-report of birth weight and perceived size at birth, but with substantial measurement error. Facility-based birth weight data exclude home deliveries, which are often higher-risk.

**Cause of neonatal death**: Three causes account for the majority of neonatal deaths: prematurity/low birth weight, intrapartum complications (birth asphyxia), and neonatal sepsis. Cause-specific mortality data are rarely available outside of verbal autopsy and facility-based records.

## 6. Child Health Programme Evaluation

### IMCI and iCCM

**Integrated Management of Childhood Illness (IMCI)**: The WHO/UNICEF framework for facility-based case management of childhood illness. Three components: improving health worker skills (case management training), strengthening health systems (drug supply, supervision), and improving family and community practices.

Evaluate IMCI through:
- Health worker adherence to IMCI clinical algorithms (assessment, classification, treatment, counselling). Measure through direct observation of consultations or case simulations.
- Correct classification and treatment of common childhood illnesses (pneumonia, diarrhoea, malaria, measles, malnutrition)
- Referral compliance for severe illness
- Caregiver counselling quality

**Integrated Community Case Management (iCCM)**: Extension of case management to the community level through CHWs for malaria, pneumonia, and diarrhoea in children under 5. Evaluate CHW performance (adherence to protocols, correct treatment), supply availability, supervision quality, referral completion, and community utilisation rates. Cross-reference to HSS sub-skill, Section 3.2 (CHW programme evaluation).

### Key Child Health Indicators

- **Under-5 mortality rate**: The ultimate impact indicator but difficult to attribute to a specific programme. See Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 5.
- **Care-seeking for childhood illness**: Proportion of children with fever/diarrhoea/ARI symptoms for whom care was sought from an appropriate provider within 48 hours. Measured through household surveys (DHS/MICS).
- **Treatment indicators**: ORS and zinc for diarrhoea (proportion of children with diarrhoea receiving ORS and zinc); antibiotics for pneumonia (proportion of children with suspected pneumonia receiving antibiotics); appropriate malaria treatment (see Disease Programme sub-skill).
- **Prevention indicators**: Bednet use in children under 5, vitamin A supplementation coverage, deworming coverage.

## 7. Adolescent Health Programme Evaluation

### Specific Considerations

Adolescent health programme evaluation requires attention to:

**Developmental appropriateness**: Evaluation instruments must be adapted for adolescent respondents — language, format, and topics should be age-appropriate. Ensure informed consent processes account for minor status (parental consent + adolescent assent for younger adolescents; mature minor provisions for older adolescents where legally applicable).

**Sensitive topics**: Adolescent health programmes often address sexual and reproductive health, substance use, mental health, and violence — all sensitive topics requiring confidential data collection methods. Consider audio computer-assisted self-interviewing (ACASI), sealed-envelope techniques, or anonymous self-administered questionnaires.

**Disaggregation**: Disaggregate adolescent health data by age band (10-14 vs. 15-19, at minimum), sex, school attendance status, marital status, and urban/rural. The 10-14 and 15-19 age groups have fundamentally different health profiles and service needs.

**School-based vs. community-based evaluation**: Many adolescent health programmes operate through schools. School-based surveys miss out-of-school adolescents, who are often the most vulnerable. Design evaluation sampling to capture both populations.

### Key Indicators

- Adolescent birth rate (age-specific fertility rate, 15-19)
- Knowledge of HIV prevention methods (disaggregated by sex)
- Contraceptive use among sexually active adolescents
- Adolescent nutritional status (BMI-for-age, anaemia)
- Mental health indicators: PHQ-A (adolescent depression), adolescent wellbeing scales
- Substance use prevalence (tobacco, alcohol, other substances)
- Experience of violence (physical, sexual, emotional — using validated instruments with appropriate ethical protections)
- Youth-friendly health service utilisation rates and quality

## 8. Nutrition Programme Evaluation

### The UNICEF Conceptual Framework

The UNICEF Conceptual Framework for Malnutrition is the foundational causal model for nutrition programme evaluation. It organises causes of malnutrition at three levels:

**Immediate causes** (individual level): Inadequate dietary intake and disease. These interact — disease reduces appetite and nutrient absorption; malnutrition weakens immune function and increases disease susceptibility.

**Underlying causes** (household/community level): Household food insecurity, inadequate care and feeding practices, unhealthy household environment and inadequate health services. These are the domains targeted by nutrition-specific and nutrition-sensitive interventions.

**Basic causes** (societal level): Poverty, inequality, governance, social norms, natural resources. These are the structural determinants that nutrition-sensitive programmes and policies address.

**For evaluation**: The UNICEF framework structures the ToC for any nutrition programme. Map the programme's interventions to the appropriate causal level. Nutrition-specific interventions (CMAM, IYCF counselling, micronutrient supplementation) target immediate and underlying causes directly. Nutrition-sensitive interventions (social protection, agriculture, WASH, women's empowerment) target underlying and basic causes. The evaluation design must match the causal level — direct attribution is more feasible for nutrition-specific interventions; contribution analysis is more appropriate for nutrition-sensitive programmes.

For Foundation users: Present the UNICEF framework as a "map of why children become malnourished" with three layers: the child's body (not enough food, too much disease), the household (not enough food available, not enough care, poor environment), and the society (poverty, inequality).

For Practitioner users: Use the framework to structure the evaluation matrix — evaluation questions should cover all relevant causal levels, not just the immediate causes. Assess whether the programme's ToC correctly identifies the binding constraints at each level.

For Advanced users: Engage with the framework's limitations — it is primarily a child undernutrition model and doesn't adequately capture overweight/obesity, micronutrient deficiencies in adults, or the double burden of malnutrition. Consider extended frameworks (Lancet Maternal and Child Nutrition Series 2013, Black et al.) for more complex nutrition evaluations.

### IPC and Cadre Harmonisé

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) and Cadre Harmonisé (CH, used in the Sahel and West Africa) provide standardised classification of food security and nutrition severity at area level.

**IPC Acute Malnutrition Scale**: Classifies areas into five phases based on GAM prevalence, aggravating factors, and contextual analysis:
- Phase 1 (Acceptable): GAM <5%
- Phase 2 (Alert): GAM 5-9.9%
- Phase 3 (Serious): GAM 10-14.9% or 5-9.9% with aggravating factors
- Phase 4 (Critical): GAM 15-29.9%
- Phase 5 (Extremely Critical): GAM ≥30%

**IPC Acute Food Insecurity Scale**: Classifies populations into five phases from Minimal (Phase 1) to Famine (Phase 5) based on food consumption, livelihood change, nutritional status, and mortality.

**For evaluation**: IPC/CH classifications provide the situational context for nutrition programme evaluation. Use IPC data to stratify evaluation findings by severity, to assess whether programme targeting aligns with severity, and to contextualise outcome findings (a programme in a Phase 4 area faces different challenges than one in Phase 2). IPC analyses are updated regularly and provide trend data useful for ITS-type designs.

### Nutrition-Specific Interventions

**Management of acute malnutrition**: Community-based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM) is the standard delivery model.

Evaluate across the CMAM cascade:
- Community screening coverage (proportion of target population screened using MUAC)
- Referral from community screening to treatment site
- Admission to treatment programme (OTP for SAM, TSFP for MAM)
- Adherence and default rates (weekly attendance at OTP)
- Recovery rate (target: >75%), death rate (target: <10%), default rate (target: <15%), non-response rate — reported against Sphere standards
- Length of stay and weight gain velocity (g/kg/day — target: ≥8 g/kg/day for SAM in OTP)
- Post-discharge relapse (re-admission within 3-6 months)

**Sphere minimum standards for nutrition** (2018): The Sphere standards define minimum performance benchmarks for nutrition programmes in humanitarian settings:

| Indicator | Sphere Standard |
|---|---|
| OTP recovery rate | >75% |
| OTP death rate | <10% |
| OTP default rate | <15% |
| SC (inpatient) recovery rate | >75% |
| SC death rate | <10% |
| SC default rate | <15% |
| TSFP (MAM) recovery rate | >75% |
| TSFP death rate | <3% |
| TSFP default rate | <15% |

Evaluate programme performance against Sphere standards. When performance falls below standards, investigate root causes: late presentation (children arriving in critical condition), drug/supply stockouts (RUTF, antibiotics), staffing inadequacy, poor protocol adherence, or barriers to follow-up.

Data sources: programme treatment records, admission/discharge registers, community screening records.

**Infant and Young Child Feeding (IYCF)**: Evaluate IYCF practices using the WHO/UNICEF IYCF indicators:
- Early initiation of breastfeeding (within 1 hour of birth)
- Exclusive breastfeeding under 6 months
- Continued breastfeeding at 1 year and 2 years
- Introduction of solid, semi-solid, or soft foods (6-8 months)
- Minimum dietary diversity (children 6-23 months receiving foods from ≥5 of 8 food groups)
- Minimum meal frequency
- Minimum acceptable diet (meets both diversity and frequency)

Evaluate IYCF promotion programmes through coverage of counselling, quality of counselling content and skills, and changes in IYCF practices at population level.

For Foundation users: Present IYCF indicators as a checklist of "what good feeding looks like at each age" and explain that the evaluation checks how many children are being fed according to these standards.

For Practitioner users: Use the full WHO IYCF indicator set. Assess both counselling delivery (process) and practice change (outcome). Discuss the challenge of measuring exclusive breastfeeding accurately (24-hour recall overestimates exclusivity; since-birth recall underestimates it).

**Micronutrient supplementation**: Evaluate coverage and adherence for vitamin A supplementation (children 6-59 months), iron-folic acid supplementation (pregnant women), zinc for diarrhoea treatment, and multiple micronutrient supplementation. Administrative coverage (doses distributed / target population) is the primary indicator, supplemented by survey-based coverage verification. Biomarker data (serum retinol for vitamin A, haemoglobin for iron/anaemia) provide objective outcome measures.

**Growth monitoring and promotion (GMP)**: Evaluate coverage (proportion of children weighed regularly), quality (correct plotting of growth curves, appropriate counselling for faltering growth), and referral of malnourished children. The evidence base for GMP's impact on nutritional outcomes is contested — evaluate the specific mechanism through which GMP is expected to work in the programme's theory of change.

### Nutrition-Sensitive Interventions

Nutrition-sensitive programmes address the underlying determinants of malnutrition — food security, WASH, social protection, agriculture, and women's empowerment — rather than direct nutritional causes.

**Evaluation challenges**: Attribution is more difficult because the pathway from intervention to nutritional outcome is longer and mediated by multiple factors. Recommended approaches:
- Theory-based evaluation: Test the causal chain from intervention to intermediate outcomes (food security, dietary diversity, hygiene practices) to nutritional status
- Mixed methods: Combine quantitative outcome measurement with qualitative assessment of behaviour change pathways
- Long evaluation timeframes: Nutrition-sensitive interventions typically require longer to show nutritional impact (2+ years minimum)
- Multi-sector coordination: Evaluate whether nutrition objectives are effectively integrated into non-nutrition sector programmes (agriculture, social protection, WASH)

**WASH-nutrition linkages**: Evaluate whether WASH interventions (clean water, sanitation, hygiene promotion) reduce environmental enteric dysfunction and contribute to improved nutritional status. Key indicators: access to improved water and sanitation, handwashing practices, diarrhoea incidence, and nutritional status. The evidence linking WASH to nutrition outcomes is growing but mixed — the WASH Benefits and SHINE trials found limited nutritional impact from WASH alone. Evaluate the specific pathways and context conditions.

For Advanced users: Discuss the implications of the WASH Benefits/SHINE results for nutrition-sensitive evaluation: the theory is sound but the effect sizes in trials were smaller than expected, suggesting that either the WASH interventions were insufficient, the pathway operates differently than modelled, or other binding constraints dominate. This has implications for how evaluators set expectations and interpret null or small effects.

### Anthropometric Measurement

Anthropometry is the foundation of nutrition evaluation. Correct measurement requires standardised protocols and trained measurers.

**Key indicators**:

| Indicator | Measure | Cut-off | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stunting | Height/length-for-age z-score (HAZ) | < −2 SD | Chronic undernutrition; reflects long-term nutritional deprivation |
| Wasting | Weight-for-height z-score (WHZ) | < −2 SD (moderate), < −3 SD (severe) | Acute malnutrition; reflects recent nutritional deprivation or illness |
| Underweight | Weight-for-age z-score (WAZ) | < −2 SD | Composite indicator; does not distinguish between chronic and acute |
| Overweight | WHZ or BMI-for-age z-score | > +2 SD | Increasing prevalence in many LMICs; the "double burden" |
| MUAC | Mid-upper arm circumference | < 125 mm (moderate), < 115 mm (severe) for 6-59 months | Rapid screening tool; predictor of mortality; used in community screening and emergencies |

**Global Acute Malnutrition (GAM)**: WHZ < −2 SD and/or bilateral oedema. GAM = SAM + MAM.
**Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)**: WHZ < −3 SD and/or bilateral oedema and/or MUAC < 115 mm (6-59 months).
**Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM)**: WHZ ≥ −3 SD and < −2 SD and/or MUAC ≥ 115 mm and < 125 mm.

**Reporting prevalence**: Report as percentage with 95% confidence intervals. Report both WHZ-based and MUAC-based prevalence separately — they identify partially overlapping but different populations (children classified as acutely malnourished by WHZ are not always the same children classified by MUAC). This matters for programme targeting and evaluation.

**Wasting-stunting concurrence**: A child can be simultaneously wasted and stunted. Concurrent wasting and stunting carries a substantially higher mortality risk than either condition alone. Evaluate the prevalence of concurrent wasting-stunting as a severity indicator, particularly in protracted crises. Report as the proportion of children who are both WHZ < −2 SD and HAZ < −2 SD.

**WHO Growth Standards (2006)**: The reference for z-score calculations for children 0-5 years. Use WHO Anthro software for z-score calculation. Report prevalence as the proportion below the relevant cut-off, and mean z-scores.

**Measurement standardisation**: Conduct anthropometric standardisation exercises for all measurers before data collection. Assess technical error of measurement (TEM) and inter-observer reliability. Poor measurement technique inflates or deflates prevalence estimates. Common errors: incorrect age estimation (affects HAZ), length vs. height measurement (children <24 months measured lying down, ≥24 months standing — a 0.7 cm systematic difference), scale calibration, and MUAC tape tension.

**SMART survey methodology for nutrition**: The SMART methodology combines anthropometric assessment with mortality estimation. Uses two-stage cluster sampling (typically 25-35 clusters × variable household numbers to achieve target sample of children). Provides point-in-time estimates of GAM and SAM prevalence with confidence intervals.

**Interpreting SMART survey results**: Key quality checks:
- Flags (out-of-range z-scores): SMART flags exclude biologically implausible values. Report the percentage flagged — high flag rates indicate measurement problems.
- Age distribution: Check for age heaping (clustering at certain ages, suggesting poor age estimation) and flat or irregular age distributions.
- Digit preference: Check for heaping at certain height/weight digits (0 and 5), indicating imprecise measurement.
- Sex ratio: Should be approximately 1:1. Significant deviation suggests selection bias.
- Standard deviation of WHZ: Should be between 0.8 and 1.2. Below 0.8 suggests data fabrication or over-exclusion; above 1.2 suggests measurement error.
- Skewness and kurtosis of z-score distributions: Should approximate normal. Report SMART plausibility checks.

For Foundation users: Explain GAM and SAM as "how many children are dangerously thin" and present the emergency thresholds simply: GAM above 15% is a crisis requiring urgent response.

For Practitioner users: Use the full anthropometric indicator set, conduct standardisation, and apply SMART plausibility checks. Understand the GAM-MUAC discrepancy and its implications for programme targeting.

For Advanced users: Discuss the limitations of cross-sectional anthropometric surveys for measuring programme impact (incidence vs. prevalence of wasting, the "incidence correction factor"), the debate on WHZ vs. MUAC as admission and outcome criteria, and statistical methods for analysing anthropometric data (mean z-score analysis vs. prevalence analysis, adjusting for clustering and design effect).

### Nutrition Surveillance and Early Warning

**Nutrition Information Systems (NIS)**: Many countries operate nutrition surveillance systems that provide periodic or continuous data on nutritional status. These include sentinel site monitoring (regular anthropometric assessments at fixed sites), community-based screening (using MUAC by CHWs or community volunteers), facility-based nutrition data (growth monitoring records, treatment programme data), and food security monitoring (WFP's VAM, Famine Early Warning Systems Network — FEWS NET).

**For evaluation**: Nutrition surveillance data can serve as outcome data for evaluation (trends in GAM prevalence from sentinel sites) but require the same data quality assessment as any routine data source. Assess site representativeness, measurement quality, reporting completeness, and selection bias (surveillance sites may not be representative of the broader population).

### Emergency Nutrition Evaluation

In humanitarian settings, nutrition evaluation has specific requirements:

**Timeliness over precision**: In acute emergencies, rapid assessments (MUAC screening, rapid SMART surveys) take priority over comprehensive evaluation designs. Accept wider confidence intervals in exchange for timely data for decision-making.

**Integration with food security assessment**: Nutrition evaluation in emergencies should be integrated with food security assessment (IPC/CH analysis, household food security surveys). Nutritional status reflects food security, health, and care — assessing all three provides a complete picture.

**Coverage assessment (SQUEAC/SLEAC)**: Semi-Quantitative Evaluation of Access and Coverage (SQUEAC) and Simplified LQAS Evaluation of Access and Coverage (SLEAC) are methods specifically designed to estimate the coverage of CMAM programmes. SQUEAC uses a Bayesian approach combining qualitative data, routine programme data, and small-area surveys. SLEAC uses LQAS to classify coverage at district level. These methods are more appropriate for CMAM coverage assessment than standard household surveys because they are designed for the specific challenges of measuring treatment coverage (small denominator, mobile population, short treatment episode).

## 9. RMNCAH-Specific Data Sources and Measurement

### DHS and MICS

The Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) and Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS) are the primary data sources for RMNCAH evaluation at the population level. They provide coverage estimates for most continuum-of-care indicators, child mortality estimates (from birth histories), nutritional status (from anthropometry), and morbidity and care-seeking data.

**Limitations for evaluation**: DHS and MICS are conducted every 3-5 years and may not align with programme evaluation timelines. They are nationally representative but often lack subnational precision for programme-level evaluation. Self-reported coverage data may not reflect quality of care received.

### Facility-Based Data

**Maternity registers and delivery records**: Source data for institutional delivery rates, C-section rates, birth outcomes, birth weight, and maternal complications. Quality varies — assess completeness and accuracy before use.

**HMIS/DHIS2 RMNCAH indicators**: ANC visits, deliveries, postnatal care visits, family planning new acceptors, childhood illness consultations. Subject to the same data quality considerations as all HMIS data (see HSS sub-skill, Section 3.3).

**Maternal and perinatal death reviews**: Source data for cause of death, contributing factors, and avoidable factors. Invaluable for qualitative evaluation of care quality but subject to completeness biases.

### Health Facility Assessment for RMNCAH

Facility assessments (SARA, SPA, EmOC needs assessments) provide data on service readiness for RMNCAH. Key domains: staffing (midwives, skilled birth attendants), equipment (delivery kits, resuscitation equipment, manual vacuum aspiration), medicines (oxytocin, magnesium sulphate, antibiotics, contraceptives), infrastructure (delivery room, privacy, water supply), and signal function performance for EmOC.

## 10. RMNCAH Evaluation Design Considerations

### Mortality as an Outcome: Feasibility and Alternatives

Maternal, neonatal, and child mortality are the ultimate outcome indicators for RMNCAH programmes but are often not feasible to measure at programme level due to rarity (maternal death), sample size requirements, and the need for long follow-up or large population surveys.

**Alternatives to mortality measurement**:
- **Process indicators as proxies**: Coverage of evidence-based interventions (skilled birth attendance, EmOC utilisation, essential newborn care) as proxies for mortality impact. Justified when the evidence linking the intervention to mortality reduction is strong.
- **Modelling**: Use the Lives Saved Tool (LiST) to estimate mortality impact from observed coverage changes. LiST applies published effectiveness estimates to coverage data to model lives saved. Useful for estimating plausible impact when direct mortality measurement is not feasible.
- **Facility-based mortality indicators**: Case fatality rates for obstetric complications, institutional neonatal mortality, and stillbirth rates. Available from facility records but reflect only facility-based events.
- **Composite indicators**: Effective coverage indices that combine coverage, quality, and outcome data.

### Evaluating Quality of RMNCAH Care

Quality of care measurement is methodologically demanding but essential. Options:

**Direct observation**: Trained observers watch clinical encounters (ANC consultations, deliveries, sick child consultations) and score adherence to protocols and guidelines. The most valid method for measuring clinical practice but resource-intensive, subject to the Hawthorne effect (providers perform better when observed), and ethically complex (consent from both provider and patient).

**Clinical vignettes and case simulations**: Present standardised clinical scenarios to providers and assess their diagnostic and management decisions. Measures knowledge and clinical reasoning but not actual practice (the know-do gap).

**Record review**: Extract data from clinical records to assess documentation of evidence-based practices. Limited by documentation quality — absence of documentation does not necessarily mean absence of practice.

**Client exit interviews**: Ask clients about the services they received. Subject to courtesy bias (clients may report positively) and recall limitations (clients may not know which clinical actions were appropriate). More useful for measuring experience of care than clinical quality.

**Standardised patients / mystery clients**: Trained actors present to providers with standardised symptoms. Providers do not know they are being evaluated. The gold standard for measuring actual clinical practice without Hawthorne effect, but expensive and ethically complex. Used increasingly in LMIC settings for quality assessment.

### Evaluating in High-Mortality, Low-Resource Settings

RMNCAH programmes in the highest-burden settings face specific evaluation challenges:
- Low facility delivery rates mean facility-based data miss a large proportion of births and deaths
- Community-based data collection is essential but expensive
- Community health workers may be the primary service providers — evaluate their quality, not just coverage
- Emergency referral systems may not function — evaluate the referral chain from community to facility
- Seasonal variation in nutrition, malaria, and mortality requires careful timing of evaluation activities
