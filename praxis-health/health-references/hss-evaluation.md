## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill guides evaluation of interventions that target the health **system** rather than a specific health **outcome**. The unit of analysis is the system or its components — not the patient, not the disease.

### What Belongs Here

Evaluations of health workforce programmes, supply chain reforms, governance and accountability mechanisms, health financing reforms (PBF, RBF, insurance, fee removal), HMIS strengthening, quality improvement initiatives, decentralisation reforms, health facility construction or upgrading, integrated service delivery models, and any intervention whose primary theory of change operates through strengthening one or more health system functions.

### What Belongs Elsewhere

- Evaluating a malaria programme that operates *through* the health system → Disease Programme Evaluation sub-skill, even though it touches system components
- Evaluating a maternal health programme → RMNCAH and Nutrition sub-skill
- Conducting a cost-effectiveness analysis of a health system reform → Health Economics sub-skill (cross-reference from here)
- Using RE-AIM or CFIR to frame an implementation evaluation → Implementation Science sub-skill (cross-reference from here)

### The System–Intervention Distinction

Many health evaluations blur the line between system and intervention. The practical test: **Is the primary purpose of the intervention to change how the system functions, or to deliver a specific health service/outcome?**

A PBF programme that incentivises facilities to improve service quality across multiple health areas is an HSS intervention. A community health worker programme delivering integrated community case management for childhood illness is closer to a disease programme, though it has HSS dimensions. When both are present, use this sub-skill for the system-level evaluation questions and the relevant disease/RMNCAH sub-skill for outcome-level questions.

### Common Commissioning Contexts

HSS evaluations are typically commissioned in these contexts:

- **Global Fund HSS grants**: Cross-cutting investments in HMIS, supply chain, community health systems, and health financing alongside disease-specific grants
- **World Bank health sector projects**: Often structured around the building blocks, with disbursement-linked indicators for system-level reforms
- **Bilateral HSS investments**: USAID health system strengthening activities, FCDO health governance programmes, GIZ health system support
- **Gavi HSS grants**: Health system investments to support immunisation and primary healthcare
- **Government-led reforms**: UHC reforms, decentralisation, performance-based financing, insurance expansion
- **Multi-donor sector-wide approaches (SWAps)**: Joint financing of national health plans

Each commissioning context comes with its own evaluation expectations. The Global Fund expects evidence on how HSS investments contributed to disease outcomes. The World Bank expects assessment against project development objectives with ratings. Bilateral donors vary but typically want contribution evidence and value-for-money assessment. This sub-skill helps navigate these expectations.

## 2. System-Level Theories of Change

### Why Standard Linear ToCs Fail for HSS

A standard linear Theory of Change — inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impact — assumes sequential causation and a reasonably bounded intervention. Health system interventions routinely violate both assumptions:

- **Multiple interacting components**: An HSS programme might simultaneously strengthen supervision, introduce new data tools, reform financing, and train health workers. These components interact with each other in ways that a linear chain cannot represent.
- **Feedback loops**: Better data quality (HMIS) enables better resource allocation (financing) which enables better supply (medical products) which generates better data. These loops are not decorative — they are the mechanism through which system strengthening works.
- **Emergent properties**: Health system resilience, responsiveness, and equity are properties of the system as a whole. They do not arise from a single causal chain.
- **Long and diffuse causal pathways**: The distance between an HSS input (e.g., training district health managers) and a population health outcome (e.g., reduced child mortality) passes through multiple intermediate system functions that the programme does not control.
- **Context dependence**: The same HSS intervention produces radically different outcomes depending on political economy, existing institutional capacity, concurrent reforms, and health worker motivation.

### Building a Systems-Aware ToC

A systems-aware ToC for an HSS intervention retains the discipline of making causal claims explicit and testable, but adds:

**Multiple causal pathways**: Map parallel pathways from different programme components to system-level outcomes. Do not force everything through a single chain.

**Cross-block interactions**: Explicitly show how strengthening one building block affects others. A health information system intervention does not only improve data quality — it may also enable evidence-based governance, improve supply chain management through better consumption data, and support performance-based financing by providing verification data.

**Feedback loops**: Identify reinforcing and balancing loops. Reinforcing: better data → better decisions → better outcomes → more demand for data. Balancing: increased service utilisation → health worker overload → reduced quality → reduced utilisation.

**Intermediate system outcomes**: Between programme outputs and population health outcomes, identify system-level changes that are observable and measurable: improved facility readiness, reduced stockout rates, increased data reporting completeness, improved supervision coverage, enhanced governance practices. These are the proximal outcomes the evaluation can most credibly assess.

**The attribution gap**: Be explicit about where the programme's plausible contribution claim ends and where broader system dynamics take over. Most HSS evaluations can credibly claim contribution to intermediate system outcomes but can only claim plausible contribution (not attribution) to population health outcomes.

### The WHO Building Blocks as ToC Scaffolding

The WHO framework identifies six building blocks — service delivery, health workforce, health information systems, medical products/vaccines/technologies, health financing, and leadership/governance — that produce four system-level outcomes: improved health, responsiveness, social and financial risk protection, and improved efficiency.

As a ToC scaffolding, this works as follows:

**Inputs**: Programme investments mapped to one or more building blocks.

**System functions**: Changes in how each building block operates (e.g., more equitable health workforce distribution, improved data completeness, reduced stockout rates).

**System outcomes**: Intermediate changes at the system level — improved service readiness, effective coverage, financial protection.

**Population outcomes**: Changes in health status, equity, and responsiveness that the strengthened system enables.

The ToC should map which building blocks the programme targets, how those blocks interact to produce system outcomes, and through which pathways system outcomes are expected to contribute to population outcomes.

### For Foundation Users

Present the ToC as a visual map with two layers: "what the programme does" (building block investments) and "what should change as a result" (system outcomes, then health outcomes). Use plain language: "If we train health workers AND improve their supplies, then service quality should improve, which should lead to better health for patients."

### For Practitioner Users

Produce a multi-pathway ToC with explicit cross-block linkages, assumption chains for each pathway, and intermediate system outcome indicators. Use the WHO framework but adapt it to the programme's specific theory.

### For Advanced Users

Engage with systems dynamics mapping, causal loop diagrams, and the distinction between programme theory (how the intervention is supposed to work) and implementation theory (how the intervention is supposed to be delivered). Consider nested ToCs for multi-component HSS programmes. Reference de Savigny and Adam (2009) *Systems Thinking for Health Systems Strengthening* and the Health Policy and Planning literature on HSS evaluation frameworks.

## 3. Evaluating by Building Block

### 3.1 Service Delivery

#### Quality of Care Evaluation Frameworks

**Donabedian framework** (structure–process–outcome): The foundational framework for health service quality evaluation.
- **Structure**: Facility infrastructure, equipment, staffing levels, training, guidelines availability. Measurable through facility surveys.
- **Process**: Clinical practices, adherence to guidelines, patient-provider interaction, waiting times, referral practices. Measurable through clinical observation, record review, and standardised patients.
- **Outcome**: Patient health status, satisfaction, complications, treatment success. Measurable through patient follow-up, record review, and population surveys.

**WHO Quality of Care framework**: Extends Donabedian with dimensions of effectiveness, safety, people-centredness, timeliness, equity, integration, and efficiency. Useful for structuring evaluation questions across multiple quality dimensions.

**The Lancet Global Health Commission on High Quality Health Systems (2018)**: Distinguishes between service availability (can people get to care?) and quality (does the care they receive improve health?). Introduced the concept of "quality-adjusted coverage" and estimated that poor-quality care causes more deaths than lack of access in most LMICs. Reference when framing evaluation questions that go beyond availability.

When evaluating quality of care, always specify which dimensions are being measured and at which level of the Donabedian framework. Most facility assessments measure structure but not process or outcome. Flag this gap when it occurs.

#### Measuring Access

Access is multidimensional. An HSS evaluation that claims to assess access must specify which dimensions:

- **Geographic/physical access**: Distance to facility, travel time, transport availability. Measurable through household surveys, GIS analysis, facility mapping.
- **Financial access**: Ability to pay for services, insurance coverage, out-of-pocket expenditure as a share of household income. Measurable through household expenditure surveys, exit interviews, facility billing data.
- **Informational access**: Awareness of available services, knowledge of entitlements. Measurable through household surveys, community assessments.
- **Cultural and social access**: Acceptability of services, language barriers, gender-related barriers, stigma. Measurable through qualitative methods, patient experience surveys.
- **Timeliness**: Waiting times, appointment availability, referral delays. Measurable through facility observation, patient exit interviews, record review.

For Foundation users: Explain that "access" means more than proximity to a facility — it includes whether people can afford care, know about services, and feel welcome using them.

#### Service Readiness and Availability Assessment Tools

| Tool | Developer | Scope | Typical Use | Sample | Key Outputs |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Service Availability and Readiness Assessment (SARA) | WHO | General service readiness across tracer indicators | Periodic national/subnational assessment of health system capacity | Census or sample of facilities | Service availability index, readiness scores by service area |
| Service Provision Assessment (SPA) | DHS Program (ICF) | Service availability, readiness, quality of care, client experience | In-depth assessment of facility capacity and quality, often alongside DHS | Sample of facilities with clinical observation and patient exit interviews | Readiness indices, quality indices, client satisfaction, service-specific quality scores |
| Health Resources and Services Availability Monitoring System (HeRAMS) | WHO | Health facility functionality, particularly in crisis/fragile settings | Emergency and post-emergency monitoring of health service availability | Census of facilities in affected areas | Functionality status, service availability, workforce availability |
| Harmonised Health Facility Assessment (HHFA) | WHO | Comprehensive facility assessment combining SARA and other tools | National assessment integrating service delivery, management, and quality | Flexible: census or sample | Integrated readiness, management, and quality scores |

**Choosing the right tool**: SARA for routine periodic monitoring of service readiness. SPA for deeper assessment including direct observation of clinical quality. HeRAMS for crisis/fragile settings where basic functionality is the question. HHFA when a comprehensive assessment is needed that integrates across tools.

**Adaptation guidance**: None of these tools should be used off-the-shelf without adaptation. Key adaptation decisions include: which service areas and tracer items to include (based on national priorities and programme scope), facility sampling strategy (census vs. sample, stratification), whether to include clinical observation and patient exit interviews (significantly increases cost and complexity), and how to integrate with existing facility reporting systems. Always pre-test adapted tools in the local context.

#### Effective Coverage

Effective coverage integrates need, access, quality, and outcomes into a single metric. It answers: "Of the population in need, what proportion received quality services and achieved a health gain?"

**Calculation**: Effective coverage = (Population in need × Service contact coverage × Quality-adjusted outcome) / Population in need

This is methodologically demanding. It requires population-level data on need, coverage data on service utilisation, and facility-level or clinical data on quality. Most HSS evaluations do not have all three. When they do not, report the components separately and flag the gap.

For Advanced users: Discuss the linking approaches (ecological, individual-level, modelled), their assumptions, and the Lozano et al. (2020) effective coverage cascade methodology.

#### Integration and Continuity of Care

When evaluating integrated service delivery models, assess:
- **Horizontal integration**: Across service areas at the same level of care (e.g., integrating TB screening into HIV services)
- **Vertical integration**: Across levels of care (community, primary, secondary, tertiary)
- **Continuity**: Whether patients receive coordinated care over time and across providers

Indicators include referral completion rates, patient tracking across levels, provider communication practices, and patient-reported coordination of care.

#### Quality Improvement Evaluation

Quality Improvement (QI) programmes — using PDSA cycles, collaborative improvement methodology, Standards-Based Management and Recognition (SBM-R), or similar structured approaches — are among the most common HSS interventions globally. Evaluating QI requires attention to both the improvement process and its outcomes.

**Evaluating QI as a process**:
- Fidelity: Were QI teams formed and functional? Were PDSA cycles actually conducted? Were data used for decision-making?
- Dose: How many improvement cycles were completed? Over what period?
- Spread: Did improvements diffuse beyond initial QI teams to other facility staff, departments, or facilities?
- Coaching and mentorship: Quality and frequency of external support to QI teams.

**Evaluating QI outcomes**:
- Process indicators: Changes in clinical practice adherence, protocol compliance, waiting times, documentation quality. These are the most proximal and measurable outcomes of QI.
- Outcome indicators: Changes in patient outcomes (mortality, morbidity, complication rates, treatment success). Harder to attribute to QI specifically; requires larger samples and longer timeframes.
- Sustainability: Do improvements persist after external QI support ends? Measure process indicators at 6 and 12 months post-support.

**Design considerations**: QI programmes are difficult to evaluate with standard experimental designs because they are adaptive by nature (the intervention changes based on data), they are typically implemented facility-wide (no within-facility comparison), and their effects may be small at the individual patient level but meaningful at the system level. Recommended designs include ITS using facility-level process indicators, stepped wedge designs when QI is rolled out in phases, and embedded case studies examining how and why QI did or did not produce sustained change.

**Collaborative improvement**: When evaluating multi-facility QI collaboratives (where facilities come together to share learning on a common topic), assess both the within-facility improvement trajectory and the cross-facility learning dynamics. The collaborative model assumes that peer learning accelerates improvement — test this assumption by comparing improvement rates between collaborative participants and non-participating facilities, and by assessing the quality and content of cross-facility exchange.

#### Community Health Systems

Beyond individual CHW performance (covered in Section 3.2), evaluate the broader community health system:

- **Health facility committees and governance structures**: Are community governance bodies (health facility committees, village health committees, community health boards) functional? Do they exercise genuine oversight or are they nominal? Assess meeting regularity, composition (gender, representation of marginalised groups), decision-making authority, and influence on facility management.
- **Demand-side interventions**: Evaluate community-level interventions that aim to increase health-seeking behaviour, health literacy, and community engagement with the health system. These include community mobilisation, social and behaviour change communication, and community-based health education.
- **Community-facility linkages**: Assess the strength of referral pathways between community health structures and facilities, the flow of information from communities to facilities and back, and community participation in health planning at the district level.
- **Social accountability at the community level**: Evaluate whether communities have effective channels to provide feedback on service quality and whether that feedback influences facility management and district health planning.

### 3.2 Health Workforce

#### Workforce Evaluation Dimensions

Health workforce interventions can target any of these dimensions. Match evaluation questions to the dimensions the intervention targets:

**Availability**: Number and distribution of health workers by cadre, geography, and facility type. Data sources: health workforce registries, payroll data, facility surveys.

**Distribution**: Geographic and facility-type equity of workforce deployment. Analyse using workforce-to-population ratios, Gini coefficients for distribution equity, and mapping against burden of disease.

**Competence**: Knowledge and clinical skills. Measurement approaches: knowledge tests, clinical vignettes (paper-based case scenarios), Objective Structured Clinical Examinations (OSCEs), direct clinical observation, and standardised patients (trained actors presenting with standardised clinical scenarios). Standardised patients are the gold standard for measuring actual clinical practice because they capture what providers do, not what they know.

**Performance**: What health workers actually do in practice. Distinct from competence (what they can do). Performance is influenced by motivation, supervision, workload, supplies, and working conditions. Measurement: clinical observation, record review, standardised patients, patient outcomes.

**Motivation and satisfaction**: Intrinsic and extrinsic factors affecting retention and effort. Measurement: validated motivation scales, job satisfaction surveys, qualitative methods exploring working conditions, compensation, recognition, career progression, and working environment.

**Retention and attrition**: Rate and patterns of health worker departure. Analyse by cadre, geography, facility type, and reason for departure. Data sources: HR records, exit interviews, cohort tracking.

#### WISN Methodology

The Workload Indicators of Staffing Need (WISN) method, developed by WHO, estimates the optimal number and skill mix of health workers needed at a facility based on actual workload. It provides a standardised approach to assessing whether facilities are understaffed, overstaffed, or appropriately staffed.

**When to use in evaluation**: When assessing whether workforce deployment matches service delivery needs; when evaluating task-shifting/task-sharing interventions; when workforce redistribution is a programme objective.

**Key outputs**: WISN ratio (current staff / required staff), difference (surplus or deficit by cadre), and workload pressure analysis.

**Limitations**: WISN depends on activity standards that may not reflect quality-adjusted practice time. Validate standards against local practice patterns.

#### Competency Assessment Approaches

| Method | Measures | Validity | Cost | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Knowledge tests (MCQ) | What providers know | Low (knowledge ≠ practice) | Low | Screening; pre-post training assessment |
| Clinical vignettes | Clinical reasoning and decision-making | Moderate (self-reported practice) | Low–moderate | Large-scale assessment; comparison across groups |
| Direct clinical observation | Actual practice during real consultations | High for observed cases | Moderate–high | Quality improvement; small-scale assessment |
| Standardised patients | Actual practice (provider unaware of assessment) | Highest for clinical practice | High | Gold standard for practice assessment; research |
| OSCE | Simulated clinical skills | High for procedural skills | High | Training assessment; licensing; small samples |

For Practitioner users: Most HSS evaluations use clinical vignettes for cost-efficiency, supplemented by facility observation where feasible. Flag the know-do gap: health workers consistently perform worse in practice than on knowledge tests.

#### Task-Shifting and Task-Sharing Evaluation

When evaluating task-shifting or task-sharing programmes, evaluate across five domains:
1. **Quality and safety**: Are task-shifted services of comparable quality? (Compare with services delivered by higher-level cadres.)
2. **Coverage and access**: Does task-shifting increase service coverage, especially in underserved areas?
3. **Efficiency and cost**: What are the unit costs and cost-effectiveness compared to standard service delivery?
4. **Health worker experience**: How does task-shifting affect workload, motivation, and job satisfaction for both the cadre receiving new tasks and the cadre relinquishing them?
5. **Health outcomes**: Do patient outcomes differ between task-shifted and standard services?

#### CHW Programme Evaluation

Community health worker programmes have specific evaluation considerations:

- **Scope of practice and training**: Assess whether training is sufficient for the assigned tasks and whether refresher training is provided.
- **Supervision**: Supervision quality and frequency are consistently the strongest predictor of CHW performance. Evaluate supervision systems, not just whether supervision occurs.
- **Attrition**: CHW attrition rates are typically high. Measure attrition by duration of service, reason for departure, and replacement rate. Distinguish between voluntary programmes and salaried CHW cadres.
- **Community embeddedness**: CHW effectiveness depends on community trust and acceptance. Evaluate using community perception surveys and qualitative methods.
- **Integration with the health system**: Assess referral linkages, data reporting, supply availability, and formal recognition within the health system.
- **Workload**: Assess whether the assigned tasks are feasible given the CHW's time availability, geographic coverage area, and other livelihood activities.

#### Training Programme Evaluation Beyond Kirkpatrick

The Kirkpatrick model (reaction, learning, behaviour, results) is widely used but limited for HSS evaluation because it treats training as a discrete event. In health systems, the pathway from training to health outcomes passes through system-level enablers: supervision, supplies, guidelines, workload, and patient demand. Evaluate training programmes by tracing this full pathway:

1. **Reaction**: Participant satisfaction (least informative, most commonly measured)
2. **Learning**: Knowledge and skill acquisition (pre-post tests, clinical assessments)
3. **Transfer**: Application of skills in practice (observation, standardised patients, record review — measured at 3–6 months post-training)
4. **System enablers**: Are the conditions in place for trained workers to apply new skills? (Supplies, equipment, supervision, guidelines, supportive management)
5. **Service quality change**: Has the quality of services actually improved? (Measured at facility level)
6. **Health outcomes**: Has patient health improved? (Most difficult to attribute to training alone)

Most training programme evaluations stop at level 2. Push evaluations to at least level 3, and assess system enablers (level 4) to explain why transfer does or does not occur.

### 3.3 Health Information Systems

#### DHIS2/HMIS Data Quality Assessment

Routine health data from DHIS2 and other HMIS platforms are increasingly used as evaluation data sources. Before using routine data for evaluation, assess data quality across four dimensions:

**Completeness**: Percentage of expected facility reports actually submitted. Calculate by: (reports received / reports expected) × 100. Disaggregate by facility type, geography, and reporting period. Threshold: completeness below 80% for a given indicator or facility type raises serious concerns about using that data for evaluation.

**Timeliness**: Percentage of reports submitted by the deadline. Late reports may be less accurate. Track timeliness trends over the evaluation period.

**Internal consistency**: Logical relationships between related data elements hold. For example: outpatient visits ≥ patients tested for malaria ≥ patients testing positive ≥ patients treated. Check for outliers (values >3 SD from the mean), negative values, and impossible ratios.

**External consistency**: Routine data triangulated against other data sources (surveys, facility assessments, supervision reports). Compare HMIS-derived coverage estimates with survey-derived estimates (DHS, MICS). Discrepancies are common and need to be characterised, not ignored.

#### WHO Data Quality Review (DQR) Framework

The WHO DQR provides a standardised methodology for assessing HMIS data quality with four components:

1. **Desk review**: Analyse reporting completeness and timeliness using DHIS2 data
2. **District verification**: Compare DHIS2 data with source documents at district and facility level (verification ratio)
3. **Facility data quality assessment**: Check source documents, registers, and tally sheets at sampled facilities
4. **System assessment**: Evaluate the M&E system's capacity, including data management practices, supervision, and feedback

**For evaluation purposes**: Conduct the DQR (or a streamlined version) as a prerequisite before using routine data in any impact or outcome evaluation. Report the results as part of the evaluation's methodology section.

#### PRISM Framework

The Performance of Routine Information System Management (PRISM) framework assesses three determinants of HMIS performance:

- **Technical factors**: Data collection tools, data processing, data transmission, ICT infrastructure
- **Organisational factors**: Governance, planning, training, supervision, financial resources, information culture
- **Behavioural factors**: Data demand and use, confidence in data, motivation to report, problem-solving capacity

PRISM is more comprehensive than DQR and examines the root causes of poor data quality. Use PRISM when evaluating HMIS strengthening interventions specifically, not just when assessing data quality for other evaluations.

#### Using Routine Data as Evaluation Data

**Opportunities**: Low cost, high temporal resolution (monthly/quarterly data), covers all reporting facilities, enables time-series designs (ITS), no recall bias for service-level indicators.

**Risks**: Reporting completeness varies across time and geography (creating artificial trends), indicator definitions may change during the evaluation period, data are facility-based (miss populations not accessing services), data quality is rarely uniform, and the Hawthorne effect (facilities report better when they know data are being scrutinised).

**Quality thresholds for evaluation use**:
- Completeness ≥80% for all reporting periods in the evaluation window
- Verification ratio within 10% (HMIS vs. source documents)
- No indicator definition changes during the evaluation period (or documented and adjusted for)
- Consistent reporting units and geographic boundaries

When routine data do not meet these thresholds, consider using routine data as one data source triangulated with primary data collection, rather than as the sole outcome data source.

#### Digital Health Intervention Evaluation

Evaluating mHealth, eLearning, telemedicine, and other digital health interventions requires attention to:

- **Adoption and use**: Download rates and registration tell you very little. Measure sustained, active use over time. Define and measure meaningful engagement metrics.
- **Usability**: System Usability Scale (SUS) or similar validated instruments. Combine with qualitative user experience research.
- **Data flow**: Does the digital tool actually improve data availability and use? Track data completeness and timeliness before and after introduction.
- **Health outcomes**: Do not assume that technology introduction leads to health improvement. The causal chain passes through user adoption, behaviour change, and system integration — each link needs evidence.
- **Equity effects**: Digital tools may widen equity gaps if access to devices, connectivity, or digital literacy is unequal.
- **Interoperability**: Does the tool integrate with existing systems (DHIS2, EMR, laboratory systems)?

This is not a full sub-skill. For comprehensive digital health evaluation, reference the WHO monitoring and evaluation framework for digital health interventions (2016) and the Principles for Digital Development.

### 3.4 Medical Products, Vaccines, and Technologies

#### Supply Chain Performance Evaluation

Evaluate health supply chains across these dimensions:

**Availability**: Percentage of facilities with tracer commodities in stock on the day of assessment. Stockout rate (percentage of facilities experiencing a stockout of a tracer commodity in a defined period). Measured through facility surveys, LMIS data, or supervisory checklists.

**Stock management**: Stock accuracy (physical count vs. recorded stock), overstock and understock rates, expiry/wastage rates. Poor stock management at the facility level is often a bigger problem than supply chain failures upstream.

**Order fulfilment**: Percentage of orders fulfilled completely and on time. Lead time from order to delivery. Measured through LMIS data and order tracking records.

**Last-mile delivery**: Reliability and frequency of deliveries to peripheral facilities. Particularly important in remote areas and FCV settings.

**Storage conditions**: Temperature monitoring (critical for vaccines and some medicines), storage space adequacy, compliance with Good Storage Practices. Measured through facility observation and temperature log review.

**Cost**: Supply chain cost per unit delivered, total supply chain cost as a percentage of commodity cost. Useful for efficiency assessment but data-intensive.

#### LMIS Data for Evaluation

Logistics Management Information System data (stock levels, consumption, orders, distribution) can serve as evaluation data for supply chain interventions. The same data quality precautions apply as for DHIS2 data: check completeness, timeliness, and accuracy before use. LMIS data are often less complete than HMIS data, especially at peripheral facilities.

Key LMIS indicators for evaluation: stock-on-hand by commodity, consumption rates, stockout duration, order fill rates, and wastage rates. Trend analysis of these indicators before and after a supply chain intervention provides the basis for an ITS or pre-post evaluation design.

#### Essential Medicines Availability and Affordability

The WHO/Health Action International (HAI) methodology provides a standardised approach for measuring:
- Availability of tracer medicines at public and private facilities
- Patient prices compared to international reference prices
- Affordability expressed as days of wages needed for a standard treatment course

Use when evaluating pharmaceutical sector reforms, essential medicines programmes, or financial access to healthcare. The methodology is standardised, enabling cross-country and temporal comparison.

#### Health Technology Assessment

Health Technology Assessment (HTA) is a systematic evaluation of properties, effects, and impacts of health technologies and interventions. It is distinct from programme evaluation: HTA assesses a technology's value (clinical effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, ethical, and organisational implications), while programme evaluation assesses how well a programme implemented and achieved results.

When an evaluation involves the introduction of new health technologies, reference HTA findings where they exist but do not attempt a full HTA within a programme evaluation. Flag to the user when an HTA would be more appropriate than a programme evaluation.

### 3.5 Health Financing

#### Evaluation Frameworks for Financing Reforms

**The UHC Cube**: The WHO framework for monitoring progress toward Universal Health Coverage along three dimensions:
- **Population coverage**: Who is covered? What proportion of the population has access to needed services?
- **Service coverage**: Which services are included? What is the range and quality of services available?
- **Financial protection**: What proportion of costs must people pay out-of-pocket?

The UHC cube provides a useful evaluation framework for health financing reforms because it requires assessing all three dimensions simultaneously. A reform that increases insurance enrolment (population coverage) but does not cover essential services (service coverage) or imposes high copayments (weak financial protection) has not achieved meaningful UHC progress.

**UHC Service Coverage Index**: A composite index of tracer indicators across reproductive/maternal/newborn/child health, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, and service capacity/access. Published by WHO/World Bank and tracked through the Global Monitoring Report. Useful as a benchmark indicator for HSS evaluations with UHC objectives.

#### Financial Protection Measurement

**Catastrophic health expenditure (CHE)**: The proportion of households whose out-of-pocket health spending exceeds a defined threshold of their income or consumption. The WHO/World Bank threshold is 10% of total household consumption or 25% of non-food consumption. Measured through household expenditure surveys (national health accounts household component, or dedicated surveys).

**Impoverishing health expenditure**: The proportion of households pushed below the poverty line (or pushed further into poverty) by out-of-pocket health spending. Measured by comparing pre- and post-health-spending poverty headcounts.

**For evaluation purposes**: Financial protection indicators are typically measured using nationally representative household expenditure surveys (LSMS, household budget surveys). These surveys are infrequent (every 3-5 years) and expensive. If the evaluation timeline does not coincide with a national survey, smaller-scale household surveys in programme areas can estimate financial protection locally, but lack national representativeness. Always report the threshold used and the denominator (total consumption vs. non-food consumption), as results differ significantly.

#### National Health Accounts

National Health Accounts (NHA) using the System of Health Accounts (SHA 2011) framework provide standardised data on health expenditure flows: who pays (financing sources), who manages the funds (financing agents), what services are purchased (health care functions), and who provides them (providers). NHA data are the primary source for tracking public, private, and out-of-pocket health spending trends.

**For evaluation**: NHA data can contextualise a health financing evaluation (showing trends in government spending, external funding, out-of-pocket share) but are produced at the national level and cannot typically be disaggregated to programme level. Use as background data and triangulation, not as direct outcome measures for programme-level evaluations.

#### Performance-Based Financing / Results-Based Financing

PBF/RBF programmes link financial payments to verified performance on pre-defined indicators, typically at the health facility level. Evaluating PBF/RBF programmes requires attention to:

**Incentive design and fidelity**: Were payments actually linked to performance as designed? Were verification processes functioning? Were payments timely? Non-payment or delayed payment undermines the causal theory.

**Service utilisation**: Did incentivised services increase in volume? Compare incentivised services with non-incentivised services (natural control group within the same facility) to assess whether gains are specific to incentivised areas.

**Quality effects**: Did quality of care improve, worsen, or remain unchanged? PBF can improve quality where indicators are well-designed and verified, but can also incentivise gaming, teaching to the test, or neglect of non-incentivised services.

**Unintended effects**: Document cherry-picking (selecting easier patients), gaming (falsifying records), distortion (neglecting non-incentivised services), and demoralisation effects on non-incentivised health workers. These are well-documented in the PBF literature and must be actively assessed.

**Equity effects**: Do PBF programmes benefit well-resourced urban facilities more than under-resourced rural facilities? Assess whether performance payments exacerbate or reduce existing inequities.

**Sustainability and cost**: What happens when external PBF funding ends? What is the total programme cost including verification, management, and technical assistance, relative to the payments disbursed?

**Common evaluation designs for PBF**: Cluster RCT (treatment facilities vs. control, randomised at facility or district level — the design used in most impact evaluations of PBF), DiD using routine data, and mixed-methods designs combining quantitative impact estimates with qualitative process evaluation.

For Advanced users: Reference the PBF impact evaluation evidence base, including the mixed results from large-scale RCTs (Cameroon, DRC, Zimbabwe, Tanzania) and the debate between PBF advocates and critics on attribution, cost-effectiveness, and health system effects.

#### Health Insurance Evaluation

When evaluating health insurance schemes (community-based, social, or national):

**Enrolment**: Coverage rates, enrolment trends, demographic and socioeconomic profile of enrollees. Assess adverse selection (sicker individuals disproportionately enrolling) and cream-skimming (insurers or schemes avoiding high-risk populations).

**Utilisation**: Changes in service utilisation among insured vs. uninsured populations. Assess both needed utilisation (appropriate care-seeking) and moral hazard (over-utilisation due to reduced cost at point of service).

**Financial protection**: Reduction in out-of-pocket spending and catastrophic health expenditure among enrollees. This is the primary intended outcome for most insurance schemes.

**Quality of care**: Does insurance status affect the quality of care received? Assess both actual quality (clinical processes) and perceived quality (patient experience).

**Provider behaviour**: How do providers respond to insurance payment mechanisms? Fee-for-service, capitation, and DRG-based payments create different incentive structures with different quality and efficiency implications.

**Fiscal sustainability**: Revenue, expenditure, claims ratio, administrative costs, and financial projections. Relevant for summative evaluations and evaluations informing scale-up decisions.

#### User Fee Removal Evaluation

User fee removal is a common health financing reform in LMICs. Evaluate across:

- **Utilisation effects**: Expected to increase, but the magnitude and sustainability vary. Assess utilisation trends before, immediately after, and 12+ months after removal (initial spikes often diminish).
- **Quality effects**: If fee removal is not accompanied by replacement financing, facilities may face revenue shortfalls that degrade quality. Assess drug availability, staffing, and infrastructure.
- **Equity effects**: Fee removal should disproportionately benefit the poorest. Assess utilisation changes by wealth quintile.
- **Fiscal and institutional effects**: How is the revenue gap filled? Are government transfers timely and adequate?

Design considerations: ITS using routine utilisation data is well-suited if fee removal has a clear implementation date and sufficient pre-policy data points exist.

#### Public Expenditure Tracking Surveys

PETS track the flow of public funds from central government through intermediate levels to the point of service delivery (typically health facilities). They identify leakage, delays, and discrepancies between allocated and received funds at each level.

Use PETS when evaluating: governance and accountability reforms, decentralisation of health financing, PBF verification systems, or when there is a hypothesis that funds are not reaching service delivery points as intended.

### 3.6 Leadership and Governance

#### Health Governance Evaluation

Governance is the most difficult building block to evaluate because it is diffuse, context-dependent, and involves power, politics, and institutional behaviour that are resistant to standardised measurement. Governance evaluation typically addresses:

**Transparency and accountability**: Are health budgets, plans, and performance data publicly accessible? Do accountability mechanisms (audits, oversight bodies, parliamentary committees, community monitoring) function?

**Participation**: Are affected populations and civil society meaningfully involved in health sector planning and oversight?

**Policy implementation**: Is there a gap between policy adoption (de jure) and practice (de facto)? Assess the policy implementation chain from national legislation through subnational planning to facility-level compliance.

**Regulation**: Are health sector regulations (licensing, accreditation, pharmaceutical quality, private sector oversight) enforced?

**Strategic vision and planning**: Are health sector plans evidence-based, costed, and monitored?

Governance evaluation relies heavily on qualitative methods: KIIs with government officials, civil society, and development partners; document review of policies, plans, budgets, and minutes; and institutional case studies. Quantitative governance indicators (e.g., health budget execution rates, audited financial statements, staffing norms compliance) complement qualitative assessment.

#### Social Accountability Tools

**Community scorecards**: Participatory tool where community members and service providers separately rate service quality, then come together to discuss discrepancies and agree on action plans. Evaluable as both a programme intervention (does the scorecard process improve services?) and as a data collection tool within an evaluation (what do communities think about service quality?).

**Citizen report cards**: Household survey-based assessment of citizen perceptions of public service quality, access, and satisfaction. Less participatory than community scorecards but more quantitatively rigorous.

**Social audits**: Community-led examination of public expenditure and service delivery, comparing planned/budgeted with actual. Common in health governance programmes.

**Budget tracking**: Community-level monitoring of health budget allocations and expenditures. Often combined with advocacy for increased or more equitable health spending.

When evaluating social accountability interventions, assess both process (was the tool implemented as designed, with genuine community participation?) and outcomes (did services change as a result?). The evidence base on social accountability and health outcomes is mixed — improvements in perceived quality and responsiveness are more consistently demonstrated than improvements in objective service quality or health outcomes.

#### Political Economy Analysis for Health

Political economy analysis (PEA) examines how power, incentives, and institutional interests shape health system performance and reform processes. PEA is increasingly expected in HSS evaluations — particularly for governance, financing, and decentralisation reforms — but most evaluators lack structured guidance on how to do it.

**When to include PEA in an HSS evaluation**: When the intervention involves policy reform or institutional change; when observed implementation patterns cannot be explained by technical factors alone; when recommendations need to account for political feasibility; when the evaluation needs to explain why a technically sound reform succeeded in some contexts and failed in others.

**Core PEA questions for health**:
- Who are the key actors (formal and informal) with influence over the reform or intervention? What are their interests, incentives, and sources of power?
- What are the formal rules (laws, regulations, policies) and informal rules (norms, patronage networks, political settlements) that shape health system behaviour?
- Where are the windows of opportunity for reform, and what are the binding constraints?
- How do electoral cycles, fiscal pressures, and political transitions affect health policy implementation?

**Methods**: PEA relies primarily on document review (policy documents, media, budget data, parliamentary records) and semi-structured KIIs with political actors, senior government officials, civil society leaders, and informed observers. Stakeholder mapping and influence analysis complement interviews. PEA should be conducted by evaluators with contextual knowledge and political sensitivity — it is not suited to rapid assessment by external consultants unfamiliar with the context.

**Integration into evaluation**: PEA can serve as a standalone analytical component of the evaluation (producing a political economy section in the report) or as a cross-cutting lens applied to all evaluation questions. The latter is more useful but more demanding. At minimum, ensure that recommendations are filtered through a political feasibility assessment informed by PEA findings.

For Foundation users: Explain PEA as understanding "who has power and what they want" in the health system, and why this matters for whether reforms actually happen. For Advanced users: Reference the DFID/ODI PEA frameworks (Thinking and Working Politically), the adapted PEA approaches for health (USAID Applied PEA), and the political settlements literature.

#### Decentralisation Evaluation

When evaluating health sector decentralisation reforms, assess across three dimensions:

- **Fiscal decentralisation**: Have decision-making powers over health budgets been transferred? Are subnational entities generating own-source revenue for health?
- **Administrative decentralisation**: Have management functions (staffing, planning, procurement) been transferred? Do subnational entities have the capacity to exercise them?
- **Political decentralisation**: Are subnational health leaders elected or appointed? To whom are they accountable?

Decentralisation evaluations are inherently multi-level (national, regional, district, facility) and require data collection at each level. Mixed-methods designs combining policy analysis, institutional case studies, and quantitative service delivery data are typically most appropriate.

#### Private Sector and Mixed Health Systems

In most LMICs, the private sector delivers a substantial share of health services — often 40-60% of outpatient care. HSS evaluations that ignore the private sector miss a large part of the system. Evaluate private sector dimensions when they are relevant to the programme's theory of change:

**Public-private partnerships (PPPs)**: Evaluate PPP arrangements (contracting out, franchising, voucher schemes, social marketing) against their stated objectives: did they increase access, improve quality, reach underserved populations, or achieve cost savings? Assess the contractual and regulatory framework, performance monitoring, and equity effects. PPPs often improve access but evidence on quality and equity effects is mixed.

**Private sector regulation and engagement**: When HSS programmes include components to regulate or engage the private sector (accreditation, quality standards, price regulation, information campaigns), evaluate compliance, enforcement, and effects on service quality and pricing.

**Mixed health system dynamics**: Assess how public and private sectors interact. Does public sector strengthening cause substitution effects (patients shifting from private to public) or complementary effects? Do private sector innovations spread to the public sector? Use household surveys to capture care-seeking behaviour across sectors.

## 4. System-Level Properties

System-level properties are characteristics of the health system as a whole that emerge from the interaction of its components. They cannot be fully explained by examining any single building block in isolation.

### Resilience

Health system resilience is the capacity to prepare for and respond to crises, maintain core functions during a crisis, and reorganise if conditions require it. Three capacities are commonly assessed:

- **Absorptive capacity**: The ability to cope with shocks using existing resources and structures (e.g., surge capacity, emergency stockpiles)
- **Adaptive capacity**: The ability to make deliberate adjustments in anticipation of or in response to change (e.g., redeploying health workers, modifying service delivery models)
- **Transformative capacity**: The ability to make fundamental changes to address root causes of vulnerability (e.g., restructuring governance arrangements, revising financing mechanisms)

**Evaluation design for resilience**: Resilience is most meaningfully evaluated around a shock (epidemic, natural disaster, conflict escalation). Prospective designs measure system capacity before a shock and assess response during and after. Retrospective designs reconstruct the response through document review, KIIs, and timeline analysis. Both require a clear conceptual framework linking system characteristics to response capacity to outcomes.

Key references: Kruk et al. (2015) What is a resilient health system?; Blanchet et al. (2017) resilience evaluation framework.

### Equity

Equity-focused evaluation in health examines whether the health system serves all population groups fairly, particularly the most marginalised.

**Measurement approaches**:
- **Disaggregated analysis**: At minimum, disaggregate all health indicators by sex, age, wealth quintile, geography (urban/rural, subnational), education, and disability status. Where data permit, also disaggregate by ethnicity, displacement status, and other relevant dimensions.
- **Concentration index**: Measures the degree to which a health variable (utilisation, coverage, outcomes) is concentrated among the poor or the rich. A concentration index of zero indicates equality; negative values indicate pro-poor distribution; positive values indicate pro-rich distribution.
- **Benefit-incidence analysis (BIA)**: Assesses who benefits from public health spending. Combines data on service utilisation (by wealth quintile) with data on unit costs of services. Reveals whether public health spending is progressive (benefits the poor proportionally more) or regressive.
- **Equity gap analysis**: Tracks changes in the absolute and relative gap between the most disadvantaged and most advantaged groups over time. Use both absolute difference (rate difference) and relative measures (rate ratio) — they can tell different stories.

**Barriers analysis**: When equity gaps are identified, evaluate the barriers that create them: geographic, financial, informational, cultural, gender-related, disability-related, and stigma-related. The Tanahashi (1978) bottleneck analysis framework (target population → availability → accessibility → acceptability → contact → effective coverage) can be adapted to identify where equity gaps emerge along the service delivery pathway.

### Responsiveness

Responsiveness measures how well the health system meets people's non-medical expectations: dignity, autonomy, communication, confidentiality, prompt attention, quality of amenities, choice, and access to social support networks. The WHO responsiveness framework (originally developed for the World Health Report 2000) provides a standardised measurement approach.

Responsiveness is distinct from satisfaction: satisfaction reflects expectations (which may be low), while responsiveness measures the system's performance against a normative standard. Evaluate responsiveness through patient experience surveys, exit interviews, and qualitative methods exploring people's experiences of care.

### Efficiency

**Technical efficiency**: Producing maximum output from given inputs (or minimising inputs for a given output). Measurable through input-output analysis at the facility level.

**Allocative efficiency**: Distributing resources across interventions and populations to maximise health gains. Measurable through cost-effectiveness analysis and priority-setting exercises.

**Evaluation methods for efficiency**:
- **Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA)**: Non-parametric method for comparing relative efficiency across facilities or districts. Identifies the efficiency frontier and measures how far each unit falls below it. Useful for comparing facilities within a health system.
- **Stochastic Frontier Analysis (SFA)**: Parametric alternative to DEA that accounts for random error. Requires distributional assumptions.
- **Unit cost analysis**: Cost per output (outpatient visit, inpatient day, vaccination, DALY averted). Compare across facilities, time periods, or programmes.

For Foundation and Practitioner users: Focus on unit cost analysis and simple input-output comparisons. DEA and SFA require specialised skills. For Advanced users: Discuss the assumptions, limitations, and software requirements of DEA and SFA.

### Integration

Evaluating integration requires assessing whether services, programmes, or systems that were previously separate are working together more effectively. Dimensions include:

- **Horizontal integration**: Across health programmes at the same level (e.g., integrating HIV, TB, and reproductive health at the primary care level)
- **Vertical integration**: Across levels of the health system (community → primary → secondary → tertiary)
- **Cross-sectoral integration**: Between health and other sectors (nutrition, WASH, education, social protection)

Integration is both a process (are services being delivered in a coordinated way?) and an outcome (does integration lead to better health outcomes, efficiency, or user experience?). Evaluate both. The evidence base on integration outcomes is mixed — integration can improve access and efficiency but can also overload health workers and dilute programme focus. Assess trade-offs explicitly.

### Sustainability

Sustainability is the most frequently asked evaluation question for HSS programmes and the most poorly assessed. It requires distinguishing between multiple dimensions:

**Financial sustainability**: Will the financing for the intervention continue after external support ends? Assess government budget commitments, domestic resource mobilisation trends, cost recovery mechanisms, and the fiscal space for health. A programme that depends on external financing for >50% of recurrent costs has a sustainability problem regardless of its effectiveness.

**Institutional sustainability**: Have the changes been embedded in institutional structures, policies, standard operating procedures, and job descriptions? Changes that exist only as parallel project structures (project management units, project-funded staff, project-specific reporting systems) are at high risk of reversal.

**Technical sustainability**: Does the system have the technical capacity to maintain the intervention without external technical assistance? Assess skills transfer, availability of local expertise, and institutional learning mechanisms.

**Political sustainability**: Is there domestic political commitment to the reform? Assess whether the intervention aligns with national health strategy, whether it has political champions, and whether it could survive a change in government or policy direction.

**Assessment approach**: Sustainability should be assessed prospectively (during implementation, identifying risks early) and at or near programme completion (assessing actual embedding). Use a structured sustainability assessment framework that examines all four dimensions. Key data sources include budget analysis, policy document review, institutional assessment, and KIIs with government decision-makers and financing officials.

Common pitfall: Evaluations that report "the programme is sustainable because stakeholders expressed commitment to continuing" without examining fiscal, institutional, and political evidence. Stated commitment is necessary but not sufficient — always triangulate with documentary and financial evidence.

## 5. Evaluation Design Considerations Specific to HSS

### Why Standard Counterfactual Designs Struggle with HSS

HSS interventions frequently violate the assumptions required for standard impact evaluation designs:

- **System-wide rollout**: Many HSS reforms (policy changes, financing reforms, governance restructuring) are implemented nationally or system-wide, eliminating the possibility of a comparison group.
- **Multiple interacting components**: HSS programmes often combine workforce, supply chain, information system, and governance interventions simultaneously. Isolating the effect of any single component is methodologically difficult.
- **Long causal chains**: The pathway from system input to population outcome passes through many intermediate steps, each influenced by factors outside the programme.
- **Context sensitivity**: HSS outcomes depend on political economy, institutional capacity, and system history that differ fundamentally between sites.

### Recommended Design Repertoire

Given these constraints, the following designs are most appropriate for HSS evaluation. Most HSS evaluations will use a combination of these approaches rather than a single design:

**Contribution analysis**: Structured assessment of whether and how the HSS intervention contributed to observed system changes. Well-suited for HSS because it works through the theory of change, assesses assumptions and alternative explanations, and produces a plausible contribution narrative without requiring a counterfactual. See `references/frameworks.md` for the full methodology.

**Realist evaluation**: Asks "what works, for whom, in what circumstances, and why?" using CMO configurations. Particularly valuable for multi-site HSS programmes where outcomes vary by context. The CMO framework naturally accommodates system-level complexity. See `references/evaluation-designs.md` for full guidance.

**Process tracing**: Within-case causal inference testing specific mechanisms linking HSS interventions to outcomes. Useful for policy reform evaluations where the question is whether a specific reform led to a specific system change. See `references/case-based-evaluation.md`.

**Interrupted Time Series (ITS)**: When routine health data (DHIS2, HMIS) are of sufficient quality and the HSS intervention has a clear start date, ITS can assess whether the intervention changed the level or trend of system-level indicators. Well-suited for evaluating PBF, fee removal, new policies, and HMIS strengthening. Requires 8+ data points before and after the intervention.

**Synthetic control**: For evaluating national or subnational reforms where the treated unit is a single aggregate (one country, one district). Constructs a counterfactual from a weighted combination of untreated units. Requires long pre-intervention time series. See `references/evaluation-designs.md`.

**QCA**: For cross-country or cross-district comparison of HSS reforms. Identifies which combinations of contextual and programmatic conditions are associated with successful system strengthening. See `references/case-based-evaluation.md`.

**Embedded case studies within quasi-experimental designs**: Combine a quantitative impact estimate (DiD, ITS) with purposively selected case studies that investigate mechanisms and context. The quantitative component estimates whether change occurred; the qualitative component explains how and why.

### Phased Implementation as an Evaluation Opportunity

When HSS reforms are rolled out in phases (pilot districts → wave 1 → wave 2), this creates a natural evaluation opportunity:

- **Stepped wedge design**: If rollout sequence is randomised, this is essentially an RCT. Even without randomisation, phased rollout enables DiD designs comparing early vs. late adopters if the parallel trends assumption is plausible.
- **DiD with rollout variation**: If sites adopt at different times due to logistical or political reasons (not related to outcomes), the variation in adoption timing can be exploited for causal identification.

Always assess whether the timing of rollout was related to site characteristics that also affect outcomes. If better-performing districts were prioritised for early rollout, naive DiD estimates will be biased.

### Multi-Level Evaluation

HSS evaluations typically need to collect data and assess changes at multiple levels:

| Level | What to Assess | Typical Data Sources |
|---|---|---|
| National/policy | Policy adoption, budget allocation, regulatory framework | Policy documents, NHA, budget data, KIIs with policymakers |
| Subnational/district | Planning, management, resource allocation, supervision | District health plans, supervision reports, KIIs with DHMTs |
| Facility | Service readiness, quality, workforce, supplies, data | Facility surveys, clinical observation, HMIS data, exit interviews |
| Community | Access, utilisation, experience, financial protection | Household surveys, community FGDs, community scorecards |
| Population | Health outcomes, equity, coverage | Population surveys (DHS, MICS), HMIS-derived estimates |

Design the evaluation to explicitly link across levels: national policy → district implementation → facility delivery → population outcomes. Collect data at each relevant level and analyse the linkages between them.

### Evaluating at Scale vs. Evaluating Pilots

Pilot evaluations and at-scale evaluations ask different questions and require different designs:

**Pilot evaluations**: Can this work? Under what conditions? Focus on feasibility, acceptability, early effects, and implementation lessons. Smaller sample, more intensive qualitative data collection, rapid feedback to programme design. Quasi-experimental designs with purposively selected comparison sites are common.

**At-scale evaluations**: Is this working across the system? With what variation? Focus on effectiveness at scale, equity of implementation, system-level effects, and sustainability. Larger sample, routine data as a primary source, attention to implementation fidelity across diverse contexts. ITS, DiD, and mixed-methods designs with purposive case studies are common.

The transition from pilot to scale is itself an important evaluation question. What was lost or gained in scaling? Was the intervention adapted? Did the theory of change hold at scale? Evaluators should be explicit about which phase they are evaluating.

### Evaluating Health Programmes in Humanitarian Settings

Health programme evaluation in emergencies and protracted crises requires adapted approaches. The core PRAXIS covers FCV-adapted evaluation design in general; this section addresses health-specific considerations.

**Health cluster performance**: In humanitarian responses coordinated through the health cluster system, evaluate cluster coordination effectiveness: gap identification, partner coordination, information management, advocacy, and response monitoring. Use the Inter-Agency Standing Committee (IASC) cluster performance monitoring framework.

**Minimum Initial Service Package (MISP) for reproductive health**: The MISP defines the minimum SRH services required in the initial phase of an emergency. Evaluate MISP implementation using the MISP assessment tools: availability of clean delivery kits, EmOC, clinical management of rape, STI treatment, and contraception.

**Epidemic response evaluation**: When evaluating outbreak response within a broader health programme evaluation, assess: detection speed (time from first case to alert), response activation (time from alert to response), case management quality (case fatality rate), and containment effectiveness (epidemic curve trajectory, reproduction number). Cross-reference to Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 10.

**Evaluation of health services for displaced populations**: Assess access to health services for refugees and IDPs (including barriers related to documentation, language, cost, and discrimination), quality of services compared to host population services, continuity of care for chronic conditions (HIV, TB, NCDs) during displacement, and mental health and psychosocial support (MHPSS) availability.

**Health system recovery evaluation**: After a crisis, evaluate health system recovery trajectory: facility functionality restoration, health workforce return, supply chain re-establishment, and health information system recovery. Use HeRAMS (Health Resources and Services Availability Monitoring System) as both a baseline and tracking tool.

For Foundation users: Explain that health evaluation in emergencies has to balance speed with rigour — decisions cannot wait for perfect data. Rapid assessments, routine monitoring data, and shorter evaluation cycles are the norm.

For Practitioner users: Use ALNAP and CHS (Core Humanitarian Standard) evaluation frameworks. Assess both the technical quality of health response and the accountability to affected populations.

For Advanced users: Discuss the ethical complexities of evaluation in active crises: risks to evaluators and respondents, informed consent under duress, the tension between evaluation rigour and humanitarian imperative, and the challenge of counterfactual reasoning in emergencies (what would have happened without the intervention is often catastrophic).

## 6. Data Sources and Measurement

### Routine Health Information Systems (DHIS2/HMIS)

See Section 3.3 for data quality assessment. When routine data pass quality thresholds, they offer monthly or quarterly facility-level data on service utilisation, commodity consumption, disease surveillance, and workforce reporting. Primary use in HSS evaluation: trend analysis, ITS designs, coverage estimation, and baseline/endline indicators for service delivery outcomes.

### Population-Based Surveys

| Survey | Developer | Frequency | Key HSS-Relevant Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) | USAID/ICF | Every 3-5 years | Coverage indicators (ANC, immunisation, family planning), child mortality, nutrition, household expenditure, quality of care (selected cycles) |
| Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys (MICS) | UNICEF | Every 3-5 years | Similar coverage indicators to DHS, child development, water/sanitation, education |
| SMART surveys | ACF/SMART Initiative | As needed, often annual | Nutrition (anthropometry, IYCF), mortality (retrospective, crude and under-5) |
| National Health Surveys | Government/WHO | Variable | Health status, risk factors, service utilisation, expenditure, satisfaction |
| Living Standards Measurement Study (LSMS) | World Bank | Variable | Household expenditure, income, health spending, access to services |

**Limitations for HSS evaluation**: Population surveys are infrequent, expensive, and designed for national representativeness. They may not be timed to coincide with programme baselines and endlines. They measure coverage and outcomes but rarely capture system-level processes. Use as one data source alongside routine data and primary collection.

### Facility Surveys

See Section 3.1 for tool selection (SARA, SPA, HeRAMS, HHFA). Facility surveys provide cross-sectional data on service availability, readiness, and quality. For HSS evaluation, they are essential for measuring structure and process quality, but are expensive and typically conducted only once during an evaluation. Consider using a lighter facility checklist at baseline and endline rather than a full SPA/SARA if budget is constrained.

### Administrative and Financial Data

National Health Accounts, public expenditure data, insurance claims data, health workforce registries, payroll data, and LMIS data. Administrative data are powerful for HSS evaluation because they reflect system functions directly, but they require access to government records and careful quality assessment.

### Qualitative Data in HSS Evaluation

Qualitative data are essential in HSS evaluation because system-level changes involve institutional behaviour, political dynamics, power relations, and organisational culture that cannot be captured quantitatively.

**Who to interview at each level**:
- National: Ministry of Health officials (planning, financing, HRH, HMIS), development partners, civil society leaders, parliamentary health committee members
- District: District health management team members, local government officials, district-level development partners
- Facility: Facility managers, health workers (different cadres), pharmacists, data clerks
- Community: Community health workers, community leaders, health committee members, service users, non-users

**Common HSS-specific interview themes**: Decision-making authority and processes, resource flow and adequacy, supervision and accountability, data use for planning, inter-institutional coordination, health worker motivation and working conditions, community trust and engagement, perceived changes in system function, and political economy dynamics.

### Geospatial Data

GIS and geospatial analysis are increasingly used in HSS evaluation for:
- Mapping facility distribution against population density and disease burden
- Calculating geographic access (travel time, distance to nearest facility)
- Identifying underserved areas and inequities in service distribution
- Overlaying health outcome data with system inputs (spending, workforce) for spatial analysis

## 7. Reporting and Use

### HSS Evaluation Audiences

HSS evaluations typically serve multiple audiences with different information needs:

- **Ministry of Health**: Actionable findings on system performance, specific recommendations for policy and management decisions, evidence for budget justification
- **Donors**: Accountability evidence, contribution story, value-for-money assessment, lessons for future programming
- **Subnational managers**: Practical findings on local implementation, benchmarking against other areas, specific recommendations for their level of authority
- **Global learning platforms**: Generalisable lessons, methodological innovations, evidence for global HSS discourse

Tailor the reporting products to each audience. A single comprehensive report plus a policy brief, a presentation deck, and a district-level feedback summary is a common and effective reporting strategy.

### Common HSS Evaluation Reporting Pitfalls

**Findings too high-level to be actionable**: "The health system needs strengthening" is not a finding. Specify which system function, in which locations, with what evidence.

**Recommendations that ignore political economy**: Recommending governance reforms without acknowledging the political interests that maintain the status quo. HSS recommendations must be politically feasible, not just technically sound.

**Ignoring implementation feasibility**: Recommending that districts strengthen supervision without acknowledging that supervision budgets have been cut by 40%. Recommendations must account for resource reality.

**Attribution overreach**: Claiming that the HSS programme "improved health outcomes" when the evaluation can only credibly assess contribution to intermediate system changes. Be honest about where the evidence chain runs out.

**Treating building blocks in isolation**: An evaluation that assesses each building block separately without examining their interactions misses the system-level story. Synthesis across building blocks should be a distinct section of the report.

**Missing the equity dimension**: Reporting average improvements without disaggregating by population group. If the evaluation data permit disaggregation, always present equity findings — even if the news is uncomfortable.

### Donor-Specific HSS Reporting

**Global Fund**: HSS grant evaluations should assess contribution to the three disease outcomes (HIV, TB, malaria) through system-level pathways. Use the Global Fund's modular framework and key performance indicators. Report against HSS investment areas: health workforce, procurement and supply chain management, HMIS, community systems strengthening, health financing, and national health strategies.

**World Bank**: Project-level evaluations report against the Project Development Objective (PDO) and PDO-level indicators. Use the ICR format for completion evaluations. HSS projects often use disbursement-linked indicators (DLIs) — assess whether DLIs were achieved and whether achievement translated into system-level change.

**Gavi**: HSS grant evaluations assess contribution to immunisation outcomes and broader primary healthcare strengthening. Report against Gavi's results framework indicators and the Full Country Evaluation methodology where applicable.

**Bilateral donors (USAID, FCDO, GIZ)**: Vary by donor and programme. USAID emphasises learning questions and adaptive management findings. FCDO emphasises value for money using the 4E framework (economy, efficiency, effectiveness, equity). GIZ emphasises capacity development and sustainability.

Detailed donor-specific reporting templates and indicator frameworks will be covered in the Donor Frameworks sub-skill when built.
