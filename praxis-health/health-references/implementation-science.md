## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill introduces the implementation science frameworks and concepts that health evaluators encounter with increasing frequency. Implementation science studies the methods to promote the systematic uptake of evidence-based interventions into routine practice. For evaluators, it provides structured tools for answering the question: "The intervention works in principle — but how well is it implemented, why, and with what variation?"

### What Belongs Here

Implementation frameworks (RE-AIM, CFIR, Proctor's taxonomy), implementation outcomes, hybrid effectiveness-implementation designs, fidelity and adaptation assessment, context analysis frameworks, and scaling-up evaluation.

### What Belongs Elsewhere

- General evaluation design methodology → PRAXIS core `evaluation-designs.md`
- Disease-specific programme evaluation → Disease Programme sub-skill
- Health system-level assessment → HSS sub-skill
- Process evaluation methodology (generic) → PRAXIS core

### Why This Matters for Health Evaluators

Many health evaluations implicitly address implementation questions ("Was the programme delivered as intended? What explains variation across sites?") but lack structured frameworks for doing so. Implementation science provides:
- Standardised taxonomies for implementation outcomes (beyond the binary "implemented/not implemented")
- Frameworks for systematically assessing context and its influence on outcomes
- Designs that simultaneously evaluate effectiveness and implementation
- A vocabulary shared across health systems, public health, and clinical research communities

## 2. Core Implementation Science Concepts

### The Efficacy-Effectiveness-Implementation Gap

**Efficacy**: Does the intervention work under ideal conditions? (Controlled trials, selected populations, strict protocols)

**Effectiveness**: Does the intervention work in real-world conditions? (Diverse populations, routine settings, variable implementation)

**Implementation**: Can the intervention be delivered reliably, at scale, in routine practice? (Focus on the delivery system, not the intervention itself)

The gap between efficacy and real-world impact is primarily an implementation gap. An intervention with strong efficacy evidence can fail at scale if implementation is poor — insufficient training, inadequate supplies, weak supervision, provider resistance, or misalignment with existing workflows. Implementation science addresses this gap.

### The Intervention vs. Implementation Distinction

**Intervention** (or clinical/programme innovation): The evidence-based practice, programme, or policy being delivered. Examples: a clinical guideline, a training curriculum, a service delivery model, a drug regimen.

**Implementation strategy**: The method used to deliver, support, or promote the intervention. Examples: training, supervision, audit and feedback, opinion leaders, financial incentives, reminder systems, facilitated networks.

This distinction matters for evaluation: poor outcomes may result from an ineffective intervention (the intervention doesn't work) or from poor implementation (the intervention works but was not delivered properly). Evaluation must assess both.

### Implementation Determinants

Factors that facilitate or hinder implementation. Organised across multiple levels:

- **Innovation characteristics**: Complexity, relative advantage, adaptability, trialability, cost
- **Individual**: Knowledge, skills, attitudes, self-efficacy, motivation of providers and implementers
- **Organisation**: Culture, leadership, resources, infrastructure, readiness for change, absorptive capacity
- **External environment**: Policy, regulation, financing, peer pressure, guidelines, professional norms, patient demand

Implementation frameworks (Section 3) provide structured approaches to assessing these determinants.

## 3. Implementation Frameworks

### RE-AIM

Developed by Glasgow and colleagues. Assesses five dimensions of public health impact:

**Reach**: Proportion and representativeness of the target population that participates in the intervention.
- Individual level: What proportion of eligible people actually received the intervention? Are participants representative of the target population, or are the most marginalised excluded?
- Measures: Participation rate, demographic comparison of participants vs. eligible population, reasons for non-participation.

**Effectiveness**: Impact of the intervention on outcomes, including negative effects and quality of life.
- Individual and population level: Did the intervention produce the intended outcomes? Were there unintended effects?
- Measures: Effect sizes on primary and secondary outcomes, subgroup analyses, adverse events.

**Adoption**: Proportion and representativeness of settings and providers that adopt the intervention.
- Organisation and provider level: What proportion of eligible facilities or providers adopted the intervention? What characterises adopters vs. non-adopters?
- Measures: Adoption rate, characteristics of adopting vs. non-adopting settings, reasons for non-adoption.

**Implementation**: Fidelity, consistency, and cost of intervention delivery.
- Provider level: Was the intervention delivered as intended? What adaptations were made? At what cost?
- Measures: Fidelity scores, consistency across sites, adaptations documented, cost per unit.

**Maintenance**: Sustainability of outcomes and institutionalisation of the intervention over time.
- Individual and organisation level: Are individual outcomes sustained post-intervention? Is the intervention maintained in routine practice after initial support ends?
- Measures: Outcome sustainability at 6-12+ months post-intervention, proportion of settings continuing implementation, integration into policies or standard operating procedures.

**For evaluation use**: RE-AIM provides a comprehensive checklist for evaluation planning. Not every evaluation will measure all five dimensions equally, but the framework ensures evaluators consider all dimensions rather than focusing exclusively on effectiveness. Particularly useful for structuring evaluation matrices and for ensuring that reach, equity (within reach), and sustainability are not afterthoughts.

### CFIR (Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research)

Developed by Damschroder and colleagues. A meta-framework consolidating constructs from 19 implementation theories into five domains:

**Intervention characteristics**: Evidence strength and quality, relative advantage, adaptability, trialability, complexity, design quality and packaging, cost.

**Outer setting**: Patient needs and resources, cosmopolitanism (networking with external organisations), peer pressure, external policies and incentives.

**Inner setting**: Structural characteristics, networks and communications, culture, implementation climate (tension for change, compatibility, relative priority, organisational incentives and rewards, goals and feedback, learning climate), readiness for implementation (leadership engagement, available resources, access to knowledge and information).

**Characteristics of individuals**: Knowledge and beliefs about the intervention, self-efficacy, individual stage of change, individual identification with the organisation, other personal attributes.

**Process**: Planning, engaging (opinion leaders, champions, external change agents), executing, reflecting and evaluating.

**For evaluation use**: CFIR is a diagnostic tool. Use it to systematically assess barriers and facilitators to implementation. Particularly useful during formative evaluation (identifying implementation challenges before or during rollout), process evaluation (understanding why implementation succeeded or failed in different sites), and cross-site comparison (explaining variation in implementation across facilities or districts).

CFIR is most commonly operationalised through qualitative data collection: KIIs and FGDs with implementers, managers, and frontline providers, coded to CFIR constructs. The CFIR interview guide tool provides a template for developing CFIR-informed interview guides.

### Other Frameworks

**Theoretical Domains Framework (TDF)**: 14 domains from behavioural science (knowledge, skills, beliefs about capabilities, beliefs about consequences, reinforcement, intentions, goals, memory/attention, environmental context, social influences, emotion, behavioural regulation, social/professional role, optimism). Useful for understanding individual-level behaviour change among providers. Often used to design implementation strategies targeting specific barriers.

**Normalisation Process Theory (NPT)**: Explains how new practices become routinely embedded in everyday work. Four constructs: coherence (making sense of the practice), cognitive participation (committing to the practice), collective action (doing the work), and reflexive monitoring (appraising the practice). Useful for evaluating whether a new practice is becoming "normal" in organisational routines.

**Exploration-Preparation-Implementation-Sustainment (EPIS)**: A phased framework for the implementation lifecycle. Useful for structuring evaluations that track implementation across phases and for identifying phase-specific determinants.

## 4. Implementation Outcomes

### Proctor's Implementation Outcome Taxonomy

Proctor and colleagues (2011) defined eight implementation outcomes, distinct from service outcomes (effectiveness, safety, equity) and client outcomes (health status, satisfaction). These are the outcomes of implementation itself:

| Outcome | Definition | Example Measures |
|---|---|---|
| Acceptability | Perception that the intervention is agreeable or satisfactory | Provider and client satisfaction surveys, qualitative assessment of attitudes |
| Adoption | Decision to employ the intervention | Proportion of facilities/providers initiating the intervention |
| Appropriateness | Perceived fit or relevance of the intervention for the setting and target population | Provider ratings, stakeholder interviews, contextual assessment |
| Feasibility | Extent to which the intervention can be carried out in the setting | Implementation rate, provider reports of barriers, pilot success |
| Fidelity | Degree to which the intervention was delivered as prescribed | Adherence to protocols, dosage, frequency, quality of delivery |
| Implementation cost | Cost of implementation strategies (distinct from intervention cost) | Cost of training, supervision, monitoring, technical assistance |
| Penetration | Integration of the intervention within a service setting | Proportion of eligible patients receiving the intervention; proportion of providers delivering it |
| Sustainability | Extent to which the intervention is maintained over time | Continued delivery at 12+ months post-support; institutional embedding |

**For evaluation use**: These eight outcomes provide a structured framework for the implementation component of any evaluation. Select the most relevant outcomes based on the evaluation stage: acceptability and feasibility in early/pilot stages; fidelity, adoption, and penetration during scale-up; sustainability in later evaluation.

### Measuring Implementation Outcomes

Most implementation outcomes require mixed methods:
- **Quantitative**: Programme records (adoption rates, coverage as penetration), structured observation (fidelity checklists), surveys (acceptability and appropriateness scales — e.g., Acceptability of Intervention Measure, Intervention Appropriateness Measure, Feasibility of Intervention Measure by Weiner et al.)
- **Qualitative**: KIIs and FGDs with providers and managers exploring why implementation succeeded or failed, what adaptations were made, and what factors influenced sustainability

## 5. Hybrid Effectiveness-Implementation Designs

### The Three Types

Curran and colleagues (2012) defined three hybrid designs that simultaneously evaluate clinical effectiveness and implementation:

**Type 1 Hybrid**: Primary aim is to test the clinical intervention's effectiveness. Secondary aim is to understand implementation context and processes.
- Evaluation focus: Outcome evaluation with embedded process evaluation
- When to use: Intervention has moderate evidence but implementation questions are emerging
- Example: RCT of a new clinical guideline with a process evaluation component documenting implementation barriers and facilitators

**Type 2 Hybrid**: Dual aims — equal weight to testing intervention effectiveness and testing an implementation strategy.
- Evaluation focus: Both outcome evaluation and implementation strategy evaluation
- When to use: Intervention has reasonable evidence of effectiveness and specific implementation strategies are being tested
- Example: Comparing two implementation approaches (training-only vs. training + mentorship) for an evidence-based intervention, measuring both health outcomes and implementation fidelity

**Type 3 Hybrid**: Primary aim is to test an implementation strategy. Secondary aim is to monitor clinical outcomes.
- Evaluation focus: Implementation strategy evaluation with safety monitoring of health outcomes
- When to use: Intervention has strong evidence of effectiveness; the question is which implementation approach works best
- Example: Comparing facility-level QI collaborative vs. district-level supportive supervision for delivering an established clinical package, with clinical outcomes monitored for safety

**For evaluation use**: Most health programme evaluations in LMIC settings function as Type 1 or Type 2 hybrids even if they are not labelled as such. A programme evaluation that measures both outcomes and implementation fidelity is a hybrid design. Labelling it as such connects the evaluation to the implementation science literature and provides a structured framework for the implementation component.

## 6. Implementation Fidelity and Adaptation

### Fidelity Assessment

Implementation fidelity measures whether the intervention was delivered as intended. Five dimensions (Carroll et al., 2007):

**Adherence**: Were the prescribed activities and components delivered?
**Dose**: Was the intervention delivered in the prescribed amount (frequency, duration, intensity)?
**Quality of delivery**: Was the intervention delivered with competence and skill?
**Participant responsiveness**: Did the target population engage with the intervention?
**Programme differentiation**: Is the intervention distinguishable from comparison/control conditions?

### The Fidelity-Adaptation Balance

Strict fidelity to a protocol may be neither feasible nor desirable when an intervention is implemented in a context different from where it was developed. Adaptation is often necessary — but uncontrolled adaptation can undermine the active ingredients of the intervention.

**Core components**: The essential, non-negotiable elements of the intervention that the evidence suggests are responsible for its effects. These must be preserved.

**Adaptable periphery**: Elements that can be modified to fit the local context (language, materials, delivery channel, scheduling) without compromising the core mechanism.

**Framework for planned adaptation**: ADAPT-ITT, the Dynamic Adaptation Process (DAP), or the Stirman et al. adaptation framework provide structured approaches to documenting and managing adaptations.

**For evaluation**: Document all adaptations: what was changed, why, by whom, and how. Assess whether adaptations preserved or undermined core components. Analyse the relationship between adaptation and outcomes — did sites with greater adaptation achieve better or worse outcomes? This analysis is essential for informing replication and scale-up.

## 7. Context Assessment

### Why Context Matters

Context — the conditions in which implementation occurs — is the primary determinant of whether an evidence-based intervention produces its expected effects in practice. The same intervention implemented in two different contexts may succeed in one and fail in the other, not because of the intervention itself but because of contextual differences.

### Context Assessment in Evaluation

**Pre-implementation context assessment**: Conduct before or at the start of implementation to identify facilitators and barriers, inform adaptation, and set realistic expectations. Use CFIR or a similar framework to structure the assessment.

**Ongoing context monitoring**: Track contextual changes during implementation that may affect outcomes — leadership changes, policy shifts, resource cuts, competing priorities, health system shocks, political events. Document using process evaluation methods.

**Context in outcome interpretation**: When outcomes vary across sites, assess whether contextual differences explain the variation before attributing it to implementation quality. Use CFIR constructs, QCA, or realist CMO configurations to analyse context-outcome relationships.

### The Context and Implementation of Complex Interventions (CICI) Framework

The CICI framework provides a structured approach to assessing context in evaluation. It distinguishes:
- **Macro context**: Political, economic, epidemiological, socio-cultural, ethical, and legal factors
- **Meso context**: Organisational and community-level factors
- **Micro context**: Provider and patient-level factors

For each level, assess the relevant contextual factors and their hypothesised influence on implementation and outcomes.

## 8. Scaling Up and Sustainability

### Evaluating Scale-Up

When an intervention moves from pilot to scale, evaluation questions shift:

- **Does effectiveness hold at scale?** Pilot conditions (motivated implementers, intensive supervision, additional resources) may not hold at scale. Evaluate whether the effect size diminishes at scale (the "voltage drop").
- **What was adapted during scale-up?** Document changes to the intervention, implementation strategy, and delivery system that occurred during scaling.
- **Is implementation fidelity maintained?** Fidelity often declines at scale as implementation intensity decreases and provider diversity increases.
- **What is the cost at scale?** Scale-up costs differ from pilot costs (see Health Economics sub-skill, Section 3 — scale-up cost estimation).
- **Are equity gaps widening or narrowing?** Scale-up often reaches easier-to-reach populations first. Track equity throughout scale-up.

### Evaluating Sustainability

Sustainability is the continued delivery and outcomes of an intervention after the initial implementation period and external support. It is one of the most poorly assessed aspects of health programme evaluation.

**Sustainability frameworks**:
- **Dynamic Sustainability Framework (Chambers et al.)**: Views sustainability as ongoing adaptation to fit a changing context, not as freezing an intervention in its original form.
- **NHS Sustainability Model**: Assesses sustainability across staff, organisation, and process factors.
- **Programme Sustainability Assessment Tool (PSAT)**: Scored assessment across eight domains (political support, funding stability, partnerships, organisational capacity, programme evaluation, programme adaptation, communications, strategic planning).

**What to measure**: Continued delivery of the intervention (fidelity and coverage); continued health outcomes; institutional embedding (policies, job descriptions, training curricula, budgets); funding sustainability (transition from external to domestic financing); and community demand.

**When to assess**: Sustainability should be assessed prospectively (during implementation, identifying risks) and retrospectively (6-12+ months after external support ends, measuring actual continuation). Most evaluations assess sustainability only at the end of the project — which is too late to influence it.

## 9. Applying Implementation Science in Evaluation Practice

### Practical Integration

Implementation science does not replace evaluation methodology — it enriches it. For health evaluators:

**In the evaluation matrix**: Add implementation outcomes (fidelity, adoption, acceptability, sustainability) alongside programme outcomes. Link each to specific data sources and methods.

**In data collection**: Add CFIR-informed interview questions to KII and FGD guides. Add implementation fidelity checklists to facility assessment tools. Add RE-AIM dimensions to evaluation reporting.

**In analysis**: Use implementation data to explain outcome variation. Sites with high fidelity and strong implementation climate are expected to achieve better outcomes — test this hypothesis. Use CFIR constructs as analytical codes for qualitative data.

**In reporting**: Report implementation outcomes alongside programme outcomes. Present fidelity data to contextualise effectiveness findings. Discuss adaptations and their implications for replication. Address sustainability with evidence, not aspiration.

### Common Mistakes

**Treating implementation as binary**: "The programme was implemented" is not informative. Assess degree, quality, and variation of implementation.

**Measuring fidelity without assessing adaptation**: Strict fidelity scores may penalise appropriate adaptations. Document and assess adaptations alongside fidelity.

**Ignoring context in cross-site comparison**: Attributing outcome variation solely to implementation quality without assessing contextual differences.

**Conflating implementation strategies with the intervention itself**: Training, supervision, and mentorship are implementation strategies, not the intervention. Evaluate both.

**Using implementation science jargon without substance**: Mentioning RE-AIM or CFIR in a proposal without actually measuring their constructs adds no value. Use the frameworks operationally or do not reference them.
