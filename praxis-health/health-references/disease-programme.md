## 1. Scope and Orientation

This sub-skill guides evaluation of programmes targeting specific diseases or disease groups. It covers the disease-specific measurement cascades, indicators, evaluation designs, and analytical approaches that distinguish disease programme evaluation from generic programme evaluation.

### What Belongs Here

Evaluation of HIV prevention, testing, treatment, and care programmes; TB case-finding and treatment programmes; malaria prevention and case management programmes; immunisation programmes (routine and campaign); NCD prevention and management programmes; neglected tropical disease control programmes; and any evaluation where the primary theory of change operates through disease-specific pathways.

### What Belongs Elsewhere

- Health system-level interventions that serve multiple disease areas → HSS Evaluation sub-skill
- Maternal, newborn, child health, and nutrition programmes → RMNCAH sub-skill
- Epidemiological methods underlying disease measurement → Epidemiological Methods sub-skill
- Economic evaluation of disease interventions → Health Economics sub-skill

### How This Sub-Skill Relates to Other Sub-Skills

Disease programmes operate through health systems. The evaluation of a malaria net distribution programme requires understanding both the disease-specific measurement (parasitaemia, incidence, mortality) from the Epidemiological Methods sub-skill and the supply chain and service delivery assessment from the HSS sub-skill. This sub-skill provides the disease-specific layer that sits on top of those foundations.

## 2. Cross-Cutting Principles for Disease Programme Evaluation

### The Cascade Framework

Most disease programmes can be evaluated using a cascade (or continuum) framework: a sequence of steps from the target population through diagnosis, treatment, and outcome. Drop-off at each step represents a programme gap. The cascade framework is powerful because it identifies where losses occur and therefore where programme improvement is most needed.

Each disease has its own cascade (detailed in disease-specific sections), but the logic is universal:
- **Target population** → **Reached/screened** → **Diagnosed/detected** → **Linked to care** → **Initiated on treatment** → **Retained on treatment** → **Achieved outcome**

Evaluating cascade steps requires different data sources at each level: population surveys for the target population and screening coverage, facility data for diagnosis and treatment initiation, cohort data for retention and outcomes.

### Coverage vs. Effective Coverage

**Coverage** (or contact coverage): The proportion of the target population that received a service. Measured from surveys (self-report) or programme data (service delivery records divided by target population). Standard evaluation indicator but does not account for whether the service was of adequate quality.

**Effective coverage**: The proportion of the target population that received quality-adjusted services sufficient to achieve a health benefit. Requires linking coverage data with quality data. More informative than coverage alone but more difficult to measure.

For disease programme evaluation, always assess both coverage and quality. A programme can achieve high coverage with poor-quality services (e.g., high immunisation coverage but poor cold chain management) — the health impact will be less than coverage data suggest.

### Intervention-Specific Outcome Hierarchies

Most disease programmes have a hierarchy of outcome indicators:

**Input/process indicators**: Resources deployed, activities conducted. Under programme control. Examples: nets distributed, health workers trained, drugs procured.

**Output indicators**: Services delivered. Under programme influence. Examples: patients tested, patients initiated on treatment, children vaccinated.

**Outcome indicators**: Changes in disease burden attributable to the programme. Partially under programme influence. Examples: reduced incidence, improved treatment success rates, reduced prevalence of risk factors.

**Impact indicators**: Population-level health changes. Influenced by the programme and many other factors. Examples: disease-specific mortality, all-cause mortality, DALYs averted.

Match evaluation design to the appropriate level. Impact evaluation requires strong counterfactual designs. Outcome evaluation can use before-after or dose-response approaches. Output evaluation can rely on programme monitoring data with quality verification.

### Donor Frameworks for Disease Programme Evaluation

**Global Fund**: Evaluations of Global Fund grants assess performance against grant-specific indicators (from the modular framework), National Strategic Plan targets, and the investment case. Performance is monitored through PUDR (Progress Update and Disbursement Request) data. Independent evaluations (Prospective Country Evaluations, Thematic Evaluations) use mixed methods. Key evaluation frameworks include the funding model results chain and the Strategic Framework performance indicators.

**PEPFAR**: Performance is tracked through MER (Monitoring, Evaluation, and Reporting) indicators. PEPFAR evaluations assess contributions to the 95-95-95 targets, programme quality, sustainability, and transition to partner government leadership. Country Operational Plan (COP) targets structure performance expectations.

**Gavi**: Evaluations assess immunisation coverage, equity (reaching zero-dose children), vaccine introduction, and health system strengthening. Full Country Evaluations provide independent assessment of Gavi support. Post-introduction evaluations (PIEs) assess new vaccine introductions.

**WHO Global Programmes**: WHO coordinates normative guidance for disease programmes. Evaluation frameworks include the End TB Strategy targets, the Global Technical Strategy for Malaria, and the Global HIV/AIDS Strategy. Country-level evaluation should align with these global frameworks.

## 3. HIV/AIDS Programme Evaluation

### The HIV Treatment Cascade (95-95-95)

The UNAIDS 95-95-95 targets (by 2025, replacing the earlier 90-90-90) define the core evaluation framework for HIV treatment programmes:

**First 95**: 95% of all people living with HIV (PLHIV) know their status.
- Measurement: Estimated number of PLHIV who have been diagnosed / Estimated total PLHIV
- Data sources: Programme data on diagnoses, population HIV prevalence estimates (from surveys like PHIA, DHS with HIV testing, or spectrum modelling)
- Evaluation questions: Is the testing strategy reaching undiagnosed populations? What is the yield of different testing modalities (facility-based, community-based, index testing, self-testing)?

**Second 95**: 95% of those diagnosed are on sustained antiretroviral therapy (ART).
- Measurement: Number on ART / Estimated number diagnosed with HIV
- Data sources: ART registers, facility data (HMIS), patient tracking systems
- Evaluation questions: What are the barriers to ART initiation? What is the time from diagnosis to treatment start? Are differentiated service delivery models improving linkage?

**Third 95**: 95% of those on ART achieve viral suppression.
- Measurement: Number with viral load <1000 copies/ml / Number on ART with a viral load test
- Data sources: Viral load testing data, laboratory information systems, patient records
- Evaluation questions: Is viral load testing accessible and routine? What factors predict virological failure? Are adherence support interventions working?

**Combined cascade**: Multiply across the three steps to assess overall population-level viral suppression (0.95 × 0.95 × 0.95 = 86% of all PLHIV virally suppressed).

### HIV Prevention Programme Evaluation

**Behavioural prevention**: Evaluate using knowledge-attitude-practice (KAP) surveys, behavioural surveillance surveys (BSS), and biological markers (HIV incidence, STI prevalence as proxy). Measurement challenges: social desirability bias in reporting sexual behaviour; difficulty attributing behaviour change to a specific programme.

**Biomedical prevention**:
- VMMC (Voluntary Medical Male Circumcision): Evaluate coverage against targets, safety (adverse event rates), age targeting, and modelled impact on incidence.
- PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis): Evaluate uptake, continuation, adherence (self-report, drug level monitoring), discontinuation, and HIV seroconversion among users.
- PMTCT (Prevention of Mother-to-Child Transmission): Evaluate the cascade: ANC attendance → HIV testing → ART initiation for positive mothers → infant prophylaxis → early infant diagnosis → infant HIV-free survival. Each step is an evaluation indicator. The final outcome is the MTCT rate (infant HIV positivity at 6-18 months, measured through EID programmes or survey-based estimates).

**Key populations**: Evaluation of HIV programmes for key populations (sex workers, MSM, people who inject drugs, transgender people, prisoners) requires specific methodological adaptations: respondent-driven sampling (RDS) or time-location sampling for hard-to-reach populations; programmatic mapping and size estimation; bio-behavioural surveillance surveys (BBSS); attention to stigma, legal barriers, and ethical considerations.

### HIV-Specific Evaluation Design Considerations

- **Ecological analysis using surveillance data**: Compare HIV incidence or viral load suppression rates across areas with different programme intensity. Useful for national programme evaluation but subject to ecological fallacy.
- **Cohort analysis using patient-level data**: Follow ART cohorts through the cascade. Assess retention, viral suppression, and mortality over time. Loss-to-follow-up is a major bias — patients lost may have died, transferred to another facility, or disengaged from care. Use tracing studies to characterise LTFU outcomes.
- **Plausibility assessment**: When experimental designs are not feasible, assess whether trends in HIV indicators are consistent with programme scale-up. Use contribution analysis to assess plausibility.
- **Population-based HIV Impact Assessments (PHIA)**: National surveys that measure HIV incidence, prevalence, viral load suppression, and ARV detection in population samples. The strongest data source for evaluating national HIV programme performance. Available for an increasing number of high-burden countries.

### Key Validated Instruments for HIV Evaluation

- HIV stigma scales (Berger HIV Stigma Scale, People Living with HIV Stigma Index)
- Adherence measures: self-report (recall of missed doses), pharmacy refill records, pill counts, drug-level monitoring (hair, DBS)
- Quality of life: WHOQOL-HIV, EQ-5D

## 4. Tuberculosis Programme Evaluation

### The TB Cascade

The TB cascade identifies drop-off points from estimated TB incidence to successful treatment:

**Estimated incident cases** → **Detected/notified** → **Initiated on treatment** → **Completed treatment / Cured**

The gap between estimated incidence and notifications represents the "missing cases" — a key metric for TB programme evaluation. The WHO provides country-level estimates of TB incidence that can be compared with national notification data to estimate the detection gap.

### TB Treatment Outcome Framework

The WHO standard TB treatment outcome categories:

| Outcome | Definition |
|---|---|
| Cured | Bacteriologically confirmed at start and end of treatment (smear/culture negative) |
| Treatment completed | Completed treatment without bacteriological confirmation of cure |
| Treatment failed | Smear/culture positive at month 5 or later |
| Died | Died from any cause during treatment |
| Lost to follow-up | Treatment interrupted for ≥2 consecutive months |
| Not evaluated | No treatment outcome assigned (transferred out without confirmation) |

**Treatment success rate** = (Cured + Treatment completed) / Total notified cases in the cohort. The primary programme performance indicator. Global target: ≥90%.

### TB Programme Evaluation Domains

**Case detection**: Evaluate the yield and coverage of different case-finding strategies — passive case-finding (facility-based), active case-finding (community-based screening, household contact investigation, screening in high-risk populations), and systematic screening (prisons, mines, urban slums, PLHIV). Assess using notification rates, case detection rate (notifications / estimated incidence), and the number needed to screen (NNS) for each strategy.

**Diagnostic evaluation**: Assess access to and quality of TB diagnostics — sputum smear microscopy, GeneXpert/Xpert MTB/RIF, culture, chest X-ray (including computer-aided detection — CAD), and drug susceptibility testing (DST). Key indicators: diagnostic algorithm adherence, time from symptom onset to diagnosis, proportion of bacteriologically confirmed cases, and DST coverage for rifampicin resistance.

**Drug-resistant TB**: MDR/RR-TB and XDR-TB programmes require separate evaluation. Evaluate the DR-TB cascade: estimated DR-TB cases → tested for resistance → diagnosed with DR-TB → initiated on appropriate regimen → completed treatment. Treatment regimens are longer, more toxic, and more expensive. Evaluate access to newer drugs (bedaquiline, delamanid, pretomanid-based regimens), adverse event monitoring, and treatment outcomes using the WHO DR-TB outcome framework.

**TB-HIV co-management**: Evaluate integration of TB and HIV services. Key indicators: proportion of TB patients tested for HIV, proportion of TB/HIV co-infected patients on ART, proportion of PLHIV screened for TB, and TB preventive therapy (TPT) coverage among PLHIV.

**TB preventive therapy**: Evaluate TPT initiation and completion among eligible groups (PLHIV, household contacts of TB cases, children under 5 in contact with TB). Key indicators: number eligible, number initiated, completion rate.

### TB-Specific Data Sources

- National TB notification data (reported to WHO, available through DHIS2 in many countries)
- TB patient registers and treatment outcome cohort data
- Drug-resistant TB surveillance data
- TB prevalence surveys (the gold standard for measuring TB burden, but expensive and infrequent)
- Inventory studies (comparing TB case notifications with other data sources to estimate underreporting)

## 5. Malaria Programme Evaluation

### Malaria Intervention Framework

Malaria programmes operate through three main intervention channels. Evaluations typically assess one or more of these:

**Vector control**:
- Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs/LLINs): Evaluate distribution coverage (proportion of households with at least one net), access (proportion of population with access to a net in their household), and use (proportion of population sleeping under a net the previous night). The gap between access and use is a behavioural indicator. Also assess net durability, physical integrity, and insecticidal activity (bioefficacy) over time.
- Indoor residual spraying (IRS): Evaluate coverage (proportion of targeted structures sprayed), quality (spray technique, insecticide concentration), timing (before transmission season), and entomological impact (mosquito density, species composition, insecticide resistance).

**Case management**:
- Test: Proportion of suspected malaria cases receiving a parasitological test (RDT or microscopy) before treatment. The test positivity rate (TPR) is a key surveillance indicator for malaria transmission intensity.
- Treat: Proportion of confirmed cases receiving appropriate first-line ACT within 24 hours of symptom onset. Assess drug quality, adherence, and treatment outcomes.
- Track: Completeness of case reporting through surveillance systems.

**Chemoprevention**:
- Intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp): Evaluate the proportion of pregnant women receiving IPTp-SP at ANC visits, with the target of ≥3 doses (IPTp3+).
- Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC): Evaluate coverage across monthly cycles during the transmission season among children under 5. Assess adherence to the full course per cycle.
- Perennial malaria chemoprevention (PMC): Evaluate coverage at each eligible contact point.

### Malaria-Specific Outcome Indicators

**Parasitaemia prevalence**: Measured through cross-sectional surveys (MIS, DHS with malaria biomarkers) using RDT and/or microscopy. The standard population-level indicator for malaria burden. Stratifiable by age, geography, net use, and wealth.

**Malaria incidence**: New confirmed malaria cases per 1,000 population at risk per year. Derived from routine surveillance data. Subject to the same data quality caveats discussed in the Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 6 — changes in testing rates, health-seeking behaviour, and reporting completeness create artefactual trends.

**Test positivity rate (TPR)**: Confirmed malaria cases / Total malaria tests conducted. Less affected by changes in testing volume than case counts. Useful as a proxy for transmission intensity trends.

**Malaria mortality**: Difficult to measure directly outside of verbal autopsy surveys and CRVS. Most evaluations use modelled estimates (WHO, MAP, IHME) or proxy indicators (severe malaria case fatality, all-cause child mortality in high-burden areas).

### Malaria-Specific Evaluation Design Considerations

**Entomological monitoring as evaluation data**: Mosquito density (measured by human landing catches, CDC light traps, pyrethrum spray catches), species composition, insecticide resistance status (WHO susceptibility bioassays, molecular markers), and sporozoite rates provide direct evidence of vector control impact. Evaluators should understand these data types even if they do not collect them.

**Stratification and heterogeneity**: Malaria transmission varies enormously within countries. National averages mask critical subnational variation. Stratify all evaluation indicators by transmission intensity (endemic, pre-elimination, elimination), geography (ecological zone, urban/rural), and target population (children under 5, pregnant women).

**Seasonality**: Malaria is highly seasonal in most endemic settings. Evaluation timing matters — surveys conducted during low transmission season will underestimate parasitaemia. ITS analysis of routine data must include seasonal adjustment. Cross-reference to Epidemiological Methods sub-skill, Section 11.

**Net durability monitoring**: ITN evaluation cannot assume that distributed nets remain functional for their rated lifespan (typically 3 years). Conduct prospective durability monitoring using the WHO net durability methodology: assess physical integrity (proportionate hole index — pHI) and insecticidal activity at 12, 24, and 36 months post-distribution.

### Key Data Sources for Malaria Evaluation

- Malaria Indicator Surveys (MIS): Cross-sectional surveys measuring net ownership, access, use, IPTp coverage, care-seeking for fever, and malaria parasitaemia (RDT and microscopy)
- DHS/MICS with malaria modules
- Routine surveillance data (HMIS/DHIS2): case counts, test positivity, deaths
- Entomological monitoring data
- Net distribution and IRS coverage records
- Health facility surveys (SARA, SPA) for malaria service readiness

### Malaria Elimination Evaluation

In low-transmission and elimination settings, evaluation shifts from coverage and burden reduction to surveillance sensitivity and case investigation. Key evaluation elements for elimination programmes:

- **Surveillance as an intervention**: In elimination settings, surveillance is the core intervention — every case must be detected, investigated, and responded to. Evaluate case detection sensitivity (active and passive), time from symptom onset to detection, investigation completeness, and response timeliness (foci investigation and response within defined timeframes).
- **Case classification**: Evaluate correct classification of cases as indigenous, imported, induced, or relapsed. This determines whether local transmission is ongoing.
- **Receptivity and vulnerability**: Assess whether areas cleared of malaria remain at risk due to vector presence (receptive) or importation (vulnerable). Entomological and human movement data are relevant.
- **Subnational verification**: WHO provides a framework for subnational elimination verification. Evaluate whether criteria are met and sustained.

## 6. Immunisation Programme Evaluation

### Coverage Measurement

Immunisation coverage is the foundational evaluation indicator, and it is measured in two fundamentally different ways that often produce different results:

**Administrative coverage**: Doses administered (from facility records, HMIS) / Target population (from census projections). Advantages: available continuously, disaggregable by facility and district. Limitations: numerator may be inflated (double-counting, recording doses given to people outside the target population); denominator may be inaccurate (outdated census); resulting coverage can exceed 100%.

**Survey-based coverage**: Proportion of children in a population survey who received specified vaccines, verified by vaccination card or maternal recall. Advantages: population-based, captures children not reached by services. Limitations: recall bias (mothers may not remember accurately), card availability (cards may be lost, unavailable, or never issued), and sample size constraints for subnational estimates.

**Discrepancies**: Administrative coverage typically exceeds survey-based coverage. The gap is informative — a large discrepancy suggests problems with either the numerator (over-reporting) or the denominator (under-projection). Evaluations should present both estimates and assess the reasons for discrepancy.

**WHO coverage survey methodology**: The updated WHO immunisation cluster survey methodology provides standardised guidance for multi-antigen coverage surveys. Uses two-stage cluster sampling with probability proportional to size. Sample size calculations must account for the design effect (DEFF typically 2-3 for coverage indicators) and subgroup analysis requirements.

### Key Immunisation Indicators

**Full immunisation coverage (FIC)**: Proportion of children who received all doses of all vaccines in the national schedule by a specified age (typically 12 or 24 months). Requires age-appropriate assessment and knowledge of the national schedule.

**Dropout rate**: DPT1 − DPT3 / DPT1 (or Penta1 − Penta3). Measures retention through the vaccination series. A dropout rate >10% indicates access problems (children start but do not complete the schedule).

**Zero-dose children**: Children who have not received any dose of DPT/Penta-containing vaccine. A Gavi priority indicator reflecting the most marginalised children missed by the immunisation system entirely. Identifying and reaching zero-dose children requires equity analysis by geography, wealth, and urban/rural status.

**Timeliness**: Whether vaccines are given at the recommended age. Delayed vaccination extends the period of susceptibility. Assess using vaccination dates from cards compared with birth dates.

**Effective vaccination coverage**: Coverage adjusted for cold chain quality, vaccine potency, and age at vaccination. More informative than administrative coverage alone but rarely available.

### Equity in Immunisation

Immunisation coverage often shows significant equity gradients. Evaluate equity by:
- Wealth quintile (using DHS/MICS asset indices)
- Urban/rural
- Mother's education
- Geographic area (subnational, district)
- Gender (typically small gaps, but assess)
- Conflict-affected and hard-to-reach areas

Calculate absolute and relative equity gaps (rate difference and rate ratio between most and least advantaged groups). Track whether gaps are narrowing or widening over time. Present equity findings using equity disaggregation dashboards or concentration indices.

### Vaccine-Specific Evaluation Considerations

**New vaccine introduction**: Post-Introduction Evaluations (PIEs), recommended by WHO 6-12 months after introduction, assess programmatic impact, cold chain capacity, AEFI surveillance, community acceptance, and coverage. WHO provides a standardised PIE tool.

**Vaccine-preventable disease surveillance**: VPD surveillance data (case-based surveillance for measles, rubella, neonatal tetanus, AFP for polio, meningitis) serve as outcome indicators for immunisation programmes. Declining VPD incidence provides evidence of programme impact — but interpretation requires assessing surveillance sensitivity and reporting completeness. An absence of reported cases may reflect either successful control or poor surveillance.

**Campaign evaluation**: Supplementary Immunisation Activities (SIAs) and mass campaigns require specific evaluation approaches: rapid coverage monitoring (RCM) during or immediately after the campaign, post-campaign coverage surveys, and adverse event monitoring. Independent monitoring (IM) provides external verification of campaign coverage.

**Cold chain assessment**: Evaluate the cold chain from national store to service delivery point. Key indicators: proportion of facilities with functional cold chain equipment, temperature monitoring compliance, cold chain capacity relative to vaccine volume needs, and vaccine wastage rates.

### COVID-19 Vaccination Programme Evaluation

COVID-19 vaccination programmes introduced specific evaluation challenges:
- Multiple vaccine products with different characteristics, efficacy profiles, storage requirements, and dosing schedules
- Rapidly evolving evidence on effectiveness (against infection, severe disease, death, and transmission) across variants
- Supply constraints and equity of access across countries and within populations
- Vaccine hesitancy and misinformation as barriers to uptake
- Novel delivery strategies (mass vaccination sites, mobile teams, integration with routine services)

Evaluate using: coverage rates by dose, product, and population group; equity analysis (age, geography, comorbidity, socioeconomic status); AEFI surveillance; breakthrough infection monitoring; and community acceptance/hesitancy surveys.

## 7. Non-Communicable Disease Programme Evaluation

### The NCD Challenge for Evaluation

NCD programmes present distinct evaluation challenges compared to communicable disease programmes:

- **Long latency**: The causal pathway from risk factor to disease to death spans decades. Programme effects on morbidity and mortality may not be observable within typical evaluation timeframes (2-5 years).
- **Multiple risk factors**: NCDs are caused by the interaction of behavioural, metabolic, environmental, and genetic factors. Attributing change to a single programme is difficult.
- **Chronic management**: Unlike acute infectious diseases, NCDs require lifelong management. Evaluation must assess sustained engagement, not just one-time intervention delivery.
- **Health system readiness**: NCD services are less developed in many LMIC health systems than communicable disease services. Evaluation may need to assess basic service availability before assessing programme effectiveness.

### The WHO NCD Framework

WHO targets for NCD prevention and control (updated in the Global Action Plan) provide a monitoring and evaluation framework:

**Risk factor targets**: Reductions in tobacco use, harmful alcohol use, physical inactivity, salt intake, and raised blood pressure, diabetes, and obesity prevalence.

**Health system targets**: Drug therapy and counselling coverage for people at high cardiovascular risk; essential NCD medicines and technologies availability.

**Mortality target**: 25% relative reduction in premature mortality from cardiovascular disease, cancer, diabetes, and chronic respiratory disease (the "25 by 25" target, now updated).

### NCD-Specific Evaluation Approaches

**Risk factor surveillance**: The WHO STEPS survey (STEPwise Approach to NCD Risk Factor Surveillance) is the standard tool for measuring NCD risk factors at the population level. Three steps: questionnaire (demographics, behavioural risk factors), physical measurements (height, weight, blood pressure, waist circumference), and biochemical measurements (fasting blood glucose, total cholesterol). STEPS data provide baseline and trend data for NCD programme evaluation.

**Service cascade for NCDs**: Analogous to the HIV cascade:
Estimated population with condition → Screened → Diagnosed → Initiated on treatment → Retained on treatment → Controlled (achieving treatment targets)

For hypertension: Population with raised BP → Aware of status → On treatment → BP controlled (<140/90 mmHg). The HEARTS technical package provides a standardised framework for cardiovascular disease management evaluation.

For diabetes: Population with raised blood glucose → Aware of status → On treatment → Glucose controlled (HbA1c <7% or equivalent).

**Cancer programme evaluation**: Evaluate across the cancer control continuum: prevention (HPV vaccination, tobacco control) → screening (cervical, breast, colorectal) → diagnosis → treatment → palliative care → survivorship. Each stage requires different indicators and data sources. Screening programme evaluation specifically assesses coverage of the target population, screening test quality (sensitivity, specificity — see Epidemiological Methods sub-skill), time from positive screening to diagnosis and treatment, and cancer stage at diagnosis (a key indicator of early detection effectiveness).

**Tobacco control evaluation**: Evaluate policy implementation (MPOWER indicators: Monitor, Protect, Offer, Warn, Enforce, Raise) and behavioural outcomes (smoking prevalence, initiation rates, quit attempt rates, secondhand smoke exposure). The Global Adult Tobacco Survey (GATS) and Global Youth Tobacco Survey (GYTS) provide standardised data.

**Mental health programme evaluation**: Mental health is the most underevaluated area of global health programming, yet the treatment gap (proportion of people with mental health conditions not receiving treatment) exceeds 75% in most LMICs.

**Evaluation of mental health integration (mhGAP)**: The WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) provides evidence-based guidelines for integrating mental health into primary care. Evaluate mhGAP integration across:
- Training: Number and proportion of primary care workers trained in mhGAP. Knowledge and skill retention at 6-12 months post-training.
- Detection: Number of mental health conditions detected at primary care level (depression, psychosis, epilepsy, alcohol/substance use, child mental health). Detection rates compared to estimated prevalence (the treatment gap).
- Treatment: Proportion of detected cases receiving appropriate treatment (psychosocial interventions, medication where indicated). Assess medication availability (essential psychotropic medicines on the WHO Essential Medicines List).
- Outcomes: Symptom reduction (PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety), functional improvement (WHODAS 2.0), recovery, and continued engagement with care.
- Supervision and referral: Quality of supervision for non-specialist providers delivering mental health care, and functioning of referral pathways to specialist services.

**MHPSS in emergencies and conflict settings**: Mental health and psychosocial support programmes in humanitarian settings operate across the IASC MHPSS intervention pyramid: basic services and security → community and family supports → focused non-specialised supports → specialised services. Evaluate each level:
- Layer 1: Are basic needs met and are protection mechanisms functioning?
- Layer 2: Are community-based psychosocial activities (support groups, community mobilisation, structured recreational activities) reaching affected populations?
- Layer 3: Are focused interventions (Problem Management Plus — PM+, psychological first aid, group interpersonal therapy) being delivered by trained non-specialists with adequate supervision?
- Layer 4: Are specialised mental health services (psychiatrist, psychologist) available for severe conditions?

Measurement: Use validated cross-cultural instruments with caution — most mental health instruments were developed in high-income settings. Use instruments validated in the specific cultural context where possible. The WHO-5, PHQ-9, GAD-7, and PCL-5 (already in PRAXIS core) have the broadest cross-cultural validation. For programme-level evaluation, consider locally developed wellbeing measures and participatory assessment approaches alongside standardised instruments.

**Substance use programme evaluation**: Evaluate prevention (knowledge, attitudes, social norms), treatment (access, retention, outcomes using validated instruments such as AUDIT for alcohol, ASSIST for substance use), harm reduction (coverage of services for people who inject drugs — needle/syringe programmes, opioid substitution therapy), and reintegration.

For Foundation users: Explain the treatment gap as "the number of people who need mental health care but aren't getting it" and present evaluation as checking whether the programme is closing that gap.

For Practitioner users: Use the mhGAP cascade (training → detection → treatment → outcomes) as the evaluation framework. Assess system-level enablers: psychotropic medicine supply, supervision infrastructure, referral pathways.

For Advanced users: Discuss the methodological challenges of mental health evaluation in LMICs: cross-cultural validity of measurement instruments, the interaction between mental health and other health conditions (comorbidity with HIV, TB, NCDs, maternal health), stigma measurement, and the evidence base for task-shifted mental health interventions.

### NCD Data Sources

- WHO STEPS surveys (population-based risk factor surveillance)
- DHS/MICS (selected NCD indicators in recent cycles)
- Health facility surveys (SARA/SPA) for NCD service readiness
- Population-based cancer registries (where they exist)
- HMIS data for NCD service delivery indicators (outpatient visits, screening, medication dispensing)
- Disease-specific registries (diabetes, hypertension, cancer)

## 8. Neglected Tropical Disease Programme Evaluation

### NTD Programme Framework

NTD programmes use two main intervention strategies with different evaluation approaches:

**Preventive chemotherapy (PC)**: Mass drug administration (MDA) for helminth infections (soil-transmitted helminths, schistosomiasis, lymphatic filariasis, onchocerciasis, trachoma). Evaluation focuses on:
- Treatment coverage: programme-reported vs. survey-verified coverage
- Drug efficacy: pre- and post-treatment parasitological surveys measuring infection prevalence and intensity (egg reduction rate)
- Transmission assessment surveys (TAS): standardised surveys to determine whether MDA can be stopped (for lymphatic filariasis)
- Impact on morbidity: anaemia prevalence, nutritional status, school performance (for STH); hydrocele and lymphoedema prevalence (for LF)

**Intensified disease management (IDM)**: Case detection and treatment for NTDs requiring individual diagnosis (HAT, Chagas, visceral leishmaniasis, leprosy, Buruli ulcer). Evaluation focuses on:
- Case detection rates (active and passive)
- Treatment outcomes and cure rates
- Case fatality rates
- Disability prevention and management

### NTD-Specific Indicators

**Programmatic coverage** (reported): Number of people treated / Number of people targeted. Reported by programme staff. Subject to inflation.

**Survey coverage** (independent verification): Proportion of the target population reporting having swallowed the drug during the most recent MDA round. Measured through coverage evaluation surveys (CES) using cluster sampling.

**Infection prevalence and intensity**: Measured through parasitological surveys. For STH: Kato-Katz stool microscopy or other egg detection methods. For schistosomiasis: Kato-Katz or urine filtration. For LF: immunochromatographic test (ICT) or Filariasis Test Strip (FTS) for antigenemia. For trachoma: clinical grading (TF/TI) by trained graders.

**Mapping and threshold decisions**: NTD programmes use prevalence thresholds to determine when MDA should start, continue, or stop. Evaluation must assess whether mapping was adequate and whether threshold decisions were correctly applied.

### Elimination and Eradication Evaluation

Some NTD programmes target elimination or eradication, which requires distinct evaluation:

- **Elimination as a public health problem**: Reducing infection/disease to below a defined threshold where it is no longer a public health problem, even though transmission may continue. Requires sustained surveillance to confirm the threshold is maintained.
- **Elimination of transmission**: Reducing incidence to zero in a defined geographic area. Requires post-elimination surveillance to detect re-emergence.
- **Eradication**: Permanent reduction to zero incidence worldwide. Only targeted for dracunculiasis (Guinea worm) and potentially polio.

Evaluation of elimination programmes must assess whether surveillance systems are sensitive enough to detect remaining cases, whether high-risk populations have been reached, and whether interrupted transmission is sustained.

## 9. Integrated and Multi-Disease Programme Evaluation

### When Integration Matters for Evaluation

Many health programmes now operate across disease boundaries: integrated community case management (iCCM) for childhood illness; combined HIV/TB programmes; integrated NCD screening platforms; malaria in pregnancy programmes linked to ANC. Evaluating integrated programmes requires assessing both disease-specific outcomes and integration-specific questions.

### Integration-Specific Evaluation Questions

- Did integration increase service utilisation across conditions? (Or did one condition's services crowd out another's?)
- Did integration improve efficiency (reduced unit costs, shared resources)? Or increase complexity and reduce quality?
- Did integration improve the patient experience (fewer visits, more comprehensive care)?
- Were there unintended effects of integration (diagnostic errors from multitasking, reduced specialisation, data system confusion)?

### Design Considerations for Multi-Disease Evaluation

**Outcome attribution**: When a programme addresses multiple diseases simultaneously, attributing outcomes to specific programme components is more difficult. Use contribution analysis, theory-based evaluation, or factorial designs where feasible.

**Indicator overload**: Multi-disease programmes can generate dozens of disease-specific indicators. Prioritise a core set of indicators across conditions (e.g., contact coverage, effective coverage, cascade completion) and supplement with disease-specific indicators where needed.

**Cross-disease effects**: Assess whether addressing one disease has spillover effects on others. HIV treatment reduces TB incidence among PLHIV. Malaria prevention during pregnancy improves birth outcomes. These cross-disease effects should be captured in the evaluation.

**Data system integration**: Assess whether integrated service delivery is supported by integrated data systems. If each disease programme has a separate reporting system, integration at the service delivery level may not be captured in the data. Evaluate data system integration as both a process and outcome indicator.

### Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) Programme Evaluation

AMR is an emerging cross-cutting evaluation domain that intersects with disease programme evaluation, HSS, and infection prevention and control.

**Evaluation of AMR stewardship programmes**: Assess prescribing practices (antibiotic use indicators: DDD per 1,000 patient-days, proportion of empiric vs. targeted therapy), compliance with treatment guidelines, stewardship committee functionality, and prescriber behaviour change. Data sources include pharmacy dispensing records, prescription audits, and clinical record review.

**Evaluation of AMR surveillance**: Assess quality and representativeness of AMR surveillance data (Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System — GLASS), laboratory capacity for culture and susceptibility testing, and trends in resistance patterns for priority pathogens.

**Evaluation of infection prevention and control (IPC)**: Assess IPC programme functionality, compliance with hand hygiene and standard precautions, healthcare-associated infection rates, and environmental cleaning practices. The WHO IPC core components provide the evaluation framework.

AMR evaluation is methodologically young. Most evaluations focus on process indicators (guidelines adherence, stewardship activities) rather than health outcomes (resistant infections averted, mortality from resistant organisms). Impact evaluation of AMR interventions requires long-term surveillance data and is complicated by the ecology of resistance (selection pressure, horizontal gene transfer, community-hospital dynamics).
